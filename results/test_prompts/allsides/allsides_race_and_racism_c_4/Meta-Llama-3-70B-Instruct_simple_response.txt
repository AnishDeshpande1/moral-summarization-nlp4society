SUMMARY:

The 1921 Tulsa race massacre, considered the "single worst incident of racial violence in American history," has been largely forgotten and buried. The event, which occurred in the thriving Greenwood District of Tulsa, Oklahoma, known as "Black Wall Street," was sparked by an unproven accusation of a black man assaulting a white woman, leading to white mobs looting, burning, and killing, leaving thousands homeless and an estimated 36-300 deaths. The massacre was subsequently erased from history through a systematic effort to destroy evidence, omit it from historical records, and downplay its significance. Many Americans only became aware of the incident this year through a fictionalized reenactment in the HBO drama "Watchmen." The discovery of mass graves using ground-penetrating radar has brought new attention to the event, with plans for further examination and potential excavation. The massacre's erasure from history is a moral outrage, contradicting the popular narrative of black progress in America and highlighting the need for a more honest reckoning with the country's racist past.

END OF SUMMARY.
