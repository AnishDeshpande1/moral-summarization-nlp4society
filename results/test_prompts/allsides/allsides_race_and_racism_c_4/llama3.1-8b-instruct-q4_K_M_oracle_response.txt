SUMMARY:

A horrific event, known as the Tulsa massacre, occurred on May 31, 1921, when white mobs flooded into Greenwood, a thriving black neighborhood in Tulsa, Oklahoma, unleashing violence that left thousands homeless and hundreds dead. The incident was sparked by an unproven accusation of assault against a white woman, which ignited long-bubbling resentment among whites towards the successful black community. The rampage resulted in 35 city blocks being burned to the ground, with some historians estimating that up to 300 people lost their lives.

For decades, the event was mislabeled as a riot, minimizing its impact and absolving the perpetrators of blame. Local leaders have been hesitant to discuss the event, and law enforcement and insurance companies declined to act in its aftermath, making it difficult to document the violence. A systematic effort was made to erase the event from history, with news accounts being cut out of records and the incident omitted from public schools' curricula until 2000.

The massacre contradicts the popular narrative of black progress in America, highlighting a period of racial violence that is often overlooked.

END OF SUMMARY.
