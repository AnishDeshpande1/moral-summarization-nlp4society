SUMMARY: Scientists using ground‑penetrating radar in Tulsa have identified potential mass‑grave sites from the 1921 Tulsa race massacre, the deadliest episode of racial violence in U.S. history. The massacre erupted after a disputed accusation against a Black man, prompting white mobs to destroy the prosperous Greenwood District—“Black Wall Street”—burning 35 city blocks, killing dozens (officially 36, but estimates up to 300) and leaving thousands homeless. The event was long suppressed: authorities ignored victims, insurance claims were denied, and the press sanitized the story, calling it a “riot” and erasing records. Consequently, the massacre faded from mainstream awareness until recent media revivals, such as HBO’s *Watchmen* and upcoming films and TV series. Historians argue the erasure fits a broader pattern of downplaying post‑Civil‑War racial violence that challenges the narrative of linear Black progress. Researchers plan further non‑invasive study of the graves, with DNA testing if remains are exhumed, aiming to complete work before the centennial commemorations. The piece includes commentary highlighting the mislabeling of the event, its lingering knowledge within Black communities, and the systematic attempts to remove it from curricula and textbooks. END OF SUMMARY.
