SUMMARY:

A mass grave discovery in Tulsa, Oklahoma, sheds light on the 1921 race massacre that was once buried, but is now being uncovered by scientists using ground-penetrating radar. This incident of violence, which occurred after an accusation of assault against a white woman by a black man, resulted in 35 city blocks being burned to the ground and thousands left homeless. The official count of deaths was 36, but historians believe it could have been as high as 300. The massacre was minimized through euphemisms like "Tulsa race riot," which absolved the white mob and blamed black Tulsans. For decades, the event was erased from history books, with news accounts cut out and the incident omitted from public schools' curriculum until 2000. Local leaders hope to complete further examination of possible mass grave sites by 2021, the 100th anniversary of the massacre, which contradicts the popular narrative of black progress in America.

END OF SUMMARY.
