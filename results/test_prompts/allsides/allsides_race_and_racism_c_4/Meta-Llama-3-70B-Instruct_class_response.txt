SUMMARY:

Scientists in Tulsa, Oklahoma, have uncovered evidence of mass graves connected to the 1921 race massacre, a brutal incident that has been largely forgotten in American history. The massacre, which occurred in the thriving Greenwood District, known as "Black Wall Street," was sparked by an unproven accusation of a black man assaulting a white woman, leading to a rampage of white mobs flooding into the neighborhood, looting homes, burning businesses, and killing residents. The violence left 35 city blocks burned to the ground, thousands homeless, and an estimated 36 to 300 deaths. The event was subsequently buried, with a widespread effort to strip the historical record of evidence, and mislabeled as a "riot" to minimize its true impact. Historians believe this incident is part of a larger pattern of racial violence after the Civil War, often omitted from the narrative of black progress in America. Further examination of the mass grave sites is necessary, and local leaders hope to complete the project by the 100th anniversary of the event in 2021.

END OF SUMMARY.
