**SUMMARY:**  
Texas Governor Greg Abbott became the first GOP governor to reject refugee resettlement, arguing the “broken” immigration system places a “disproportionate” burden on the state and that resources must be devoted to those already here—including refugees, migrants, and the homeless. The decision provoked a wave of condemnation, labeled “inhumane,” “shameful,” and “morally wrong” by figures such as presidential candidate Tom Steyer, columnist Monica Rhor, and advocacy group RAICES, who accused Abbott of xenophobia and fear. Critics liken the backlash to “morality police” enforcing a twisted definition of virtue. The author counters that refugees and migrants are conflated with broader immigration, portraying them as “poor,” “downtrodden,” and “dangerous” to national character and social fabric. He argues that mass migration harms both destination and origin countries, draining “good” people and straining resources, and that the true moral course is to help people where they are rather than invite more refugees. He warns that even modest numbers, combined with future immigration expansion, could eventually erode “freedom‑loving” America.
