SUMMARY: The article critiques the political extremes surrounding abortion legislation, highlighting President Trump’s mischaracterization of Virginia’s proposed late‑term abortion bill and contrasting New York’s liberal reforms with Alabama’s near‑total ban. It notes that while both states’ laws appear extreme, public opinion generally supports early‑term abortions and opposes third‑trimester procedures. The piece argues that the 1973 Roe v. Wade decision forced a binary, absolutist debate, prompting pro‑life activists to adopt incremental strategies, whereas now some Republicans, exemplified by Alabama’s GOP, are pursuing a “go‑for‑broke” approach to overturn Roe. Conversely, New York’s “abortion sanctuary” laws aim to protect rights if Roe is reversed. The author suggests that most Americans favor compromise, but partisan politics and activist influence push both parties toward portraying the opposition as extreme. Overturning Roe could allow state‑level majorities to shape policy, but may not ease political tensions, leaving the nation to confront the outcome. END OF SUMMARY.
