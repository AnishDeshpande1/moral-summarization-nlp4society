SUMMARY: The article argues that the national debate over abortion is being driven by political extremes rather than the moderate views of most Americans. It contends that President Trump’s inflammatory claim about “executing” babies misrepresents a Virginia governor’s nuanced comment on late‑term care, and that Democratic proposals in New York and Virginia—though framed as protecting women—would have permitted abortions up to the 40th week, an outcome far beyond public comfort. Polling shows strong support for early‑term abortions but overwhelming opposition to third‑trimester procedures, highlighting a gap between policy and popular sentiment. The piece blames the 1973 Roe v. Wade decision for freezing abortion law at a national level, forcing both sides into absolutist positions: pro‑life activists now push an all‑or‑nothing strategy exemplified by Alabama’s near‑total ban, while Democrats seek “sanctuary” laws in states like New York to guard against a potential overturning of Roe. The author warns that the go‑for‑broke approach of Alabama’s legislators miscalculates political reality, noting that most citizens desire a compromise, yet parties cater to activist bases and portray each other as extremist. Overturning Roe could return authority to states and eventually reveal whether a more balanced, majority‑based approach is possible. END OF SUMMARY.
