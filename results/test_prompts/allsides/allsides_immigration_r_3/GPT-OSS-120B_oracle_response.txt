SUMMARY:
New York City’s Commission on Human Rights issued guidance interpreting the city’s Human Rights Law to prohibit the pejorative use of “illegal alien” when it is used to demean, humiliate, or harass a person. The decree warns that even a single comment that signals discriminatory views about immigration status could be treated as harassment, making the employer liable for damages. While the guidance does not criminalize casual street remarks or threats to call ICE, it expands liability beyond the “severe or pervasive” standard that federal law requires. Critics argue that this broad interpretation eliminates the modest limit on liability and therefore creates a problem for free‑speech protections. Attorneys note that government decrees to prohibit speech must come with clear limiting conditions to satisfy the First Amendment. The city’s approach goes further than previous anti‑discrimination ordinances, raising concerns that the guidance may be unconstitutional because it could prohibit statements that are merely offensive rather than truly harassing.  

END OF SUMMARY.
