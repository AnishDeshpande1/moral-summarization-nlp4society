SUMMARY: New York City’s Commission on Human Rights announced that using “illegal alien” in a demeaning way violates the city’s Human Rights Law and can lead to fines up to $250,000. The guidance expands the protected category of perceived immigration status, stating that even a single hostile comment may constitute harassment in workplaces, housing, or public accommodations. However, casual remarks on the street or threats to involve ICE are not covered. Critics argue the rule eliminates the “severe or pervasive” standard required by federal law, risking a First Amendment violation because it could punish speech that is merely offensive rather than truly harassing. Attorney Hans Bader warns that without clear limits, the ordinance may be unconstitutional, as free‑speech protections demand that conduct be both unwelcome and sufficiently severe to create a hostile environment. The debate centers on balancing anti‑discrimination goals with constitutional free‑speech rights. END OF SUMMARY.
