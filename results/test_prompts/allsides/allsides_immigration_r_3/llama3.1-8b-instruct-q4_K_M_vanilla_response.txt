SUMMARY:

New York City's Commission on Human Rights has declared that using the term "illegal alien" pejoratively to describe an undocumented person is against the law, and could result in fines of up to $250,000. However, the city's interpretation of the law may be unconstitutional under the First Amendment. The guidance issued by the commission states that using certain terms with intent to demean or harass a person is illegal, but only applies to workplace harassment, tenants' rights, and public accommodations.

Critics argue that this guidance goes too far in limiting free speech, as it does not require conduct to be severe or pervasive enough to be considered harassment. Hans Bader, an attorney and former official, notes that the government cannot simply prohibit people from making politically incorrect statements about undocumented people without limiting the scope of anti-discrimination mandates.

The city's interpretation may push the guidance into unconstitutional territory, as government decrees must come with certain limiting conditions to survive a First Amendment test. The issue raises questions about the balance between free speech and anti-discrimination laws.

END OF SUMMARY.
