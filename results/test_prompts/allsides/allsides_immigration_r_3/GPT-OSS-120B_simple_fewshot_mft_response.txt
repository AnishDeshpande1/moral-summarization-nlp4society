SUMMARY:
New York City’s Commission on Human Rights has ruled that using the phrase “illegal alien” in a demeaning or harassing way violates the city’s Human Rights Law and can result in fines up to $250,000. While the guidance is limited to workplace, housing and public‑accommodation contexts, it expands the definition of harassment so that even a single, hostile comment about a person’s immigration status may be actionable. Critics argue this over‑broad authority subverts constitutional limits on free speech, stripping away the “severe or pervasive” standard that normally protects even unpopular speech. At the same time, the commission frames the rule as a matter of care, seeking to shield undocumented workers and tenants from humiliation and harm. Yet opponents contend that the policy sacrifices fairness by privileging one group’s protection at the expense of broader First‑Amendment rights, effectively cheating the public of its constitutional guarantees. The tension between governmental authority to curb discrimination, the desire to protect vulnerable individuals, and the need for equitable, constitutionally sound enforcement lies at the heart of the debate.  
END OF SUMMARY.
