SUMMARY:
New York City’s Commission on Human Rights issued sweeping guidance that labeling an undocumented person an “illegal alien” with intent to demean constitutes illegal harassment under the city’s Human Rights Law, exposing employers and landlords to fines up to $250,000. While the rule applies only to workplace, housing and public‑accommodation contexts—not casual street remarks—the guidance expands liability to even a single disparaging comment, eliminating the traditional “severe or pervasive” threshold required by federal law. Legal experts warn this broad interpretation may clash with the First Amendment, arguing that anti‑discrimination measures must still respect free‑speech limits. Critics contend the city is overreaching by criminalizing politically incorrect speech about immigration status, and that such measures risk being struck down as unconstitutional unless they are narrowly tailored to genuine harassment. The debate highlights the tension between protecting vulnerable groups from discrimination and preserving robust free‑speech protections.

END OF SUMMARY.
