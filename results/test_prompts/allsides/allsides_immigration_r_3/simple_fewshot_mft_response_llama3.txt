SUMMARY:

New York City's Commission on Human Rights has declared that using the term "illegal alien" pejoratively to describe an undocumented person violates laws designed to protect employees and tenants from discrimination, potentially resulting in fines of up to $250,000. However, the city's interpretation of the law may be unconstitutional under the First Amendment. The guidance notes that even a single comment made with discriminatory intent can constitute harassment, raising concerns about limiting free speech. Critics argue that this decree eliminates the "severe or pervasive" standard found in federal law, which is necessary to satisfy broad free speech guarantees.

The moral framing of this article relies heavily on the dimensions of Authority/Subversion (questioning the authority of the city's Commission on Human Rights), Care/Harm (concerns about the potential harm caused by limiting free speech), and Fairness/Cheating (criticism that the decree is unfair and may lead to cheating or exploiting the system).

END OF SUMMARY.
