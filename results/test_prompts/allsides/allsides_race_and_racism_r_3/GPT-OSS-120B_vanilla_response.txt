SUMMARY: The article argues that Confederate monuments, such as the Robert E. Lee statue in Richmond, represent a glorified and false narrative of the Civil War that honors leaders who fought to preserve slavery. While acknowledging that these statues have been vandalized and removed amid a broader “cancellation” wave, the author contends that the proper response is lawful relocation—not mob action. He calls for moving prominent statues to museums or battlefields, preserving graves and memorials that acknowledge the war’s human cost, and shunning the Confederate flag as a symbol of a flawed cause. The piece also critiques the naming of military bases after Confederate generals, noting the conflict it creates for African‑American service members. Citing historical context, including the Lost Cause myth and statements by figures like Frederick Douglass and Alexander Stephens, the author emphasizes that secession was a traitorous act driven by slavery, not states’ rights. Ultimately, Confederate statues should be removed from public honorific spaces to respect black citizens and accurately reflect history. END OF SUMMARY.
