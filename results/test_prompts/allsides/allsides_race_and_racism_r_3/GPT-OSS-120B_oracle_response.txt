SUMMARY:
Secession was a traitorous act that threatened to destroy the Union, and the South’s leaders do not deserve a place of honor. In the current wave of cancellations, Confederate statues have been vandalized, graffitied, and torn down, while authorities move to remove them. President Donald Trump has acted as a defender of these monuments, saying we should preserve heritage rather than offend sensibilities. Conservatives often oppose “woke” iconoclasm, fearing it will shun not only Jefferson Davis but eventually George Washington. Yet this impulse is a mistake. The statues are an unnecessary affront to Black citizens, who should not have to see defenders of chattel slavery put on pedestals. Many were erected to enshrine a falsified Lost‑Cause narrative, a creation meant to justify the Confederacy’s flawed cause. Big monuments in prominent public spaces should be transferred—ideally to museums or battlefields—through lawful action, not mob vandalism. The Confederate flag should be shunned, while obelisks marking the dead and battlefields should be preserved to acknowledge the conflict’s human cost. Bases named for Confederate generals present a harder case, but as the origins are done, it will be impossible for African‑American soldiers to sacrifice without confronting that history. The Richmond Lee statue, whose unveiling in 1890 marked the movement to glorify Lee, is coming down, reflecting a broader reassessment. END OF SUMMARY.
