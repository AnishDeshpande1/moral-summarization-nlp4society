SUMMARY:

The author reflects on the moral implications of forgiveness in the face of racial violence, particularly in the context of the Charleston church shooting. While acknowledging the nobility of Christian forgiveness, the author expresses frustration and irritation towards those who praise the victims for not seeking revenge. The author argues that it's understandable to respond to deadly assaults with righteous rage, and that nationalism and a willingness to countenance political violence can be a legitimate response to systemic oppression. The author contrasts the predominantly Christian approach of African-Americans to suffering and oppression with a less dominant, nationalist dimension that emphasizes black pride and self-defense. The author suggests that White America is fortunate that the victims of its gravest injustices have chosen a less confrontational response, and wonders why there isn't more violence directed against white America. The author's moral framing emphasizes the importance of authority, care, fairness, loyalty, and sanctity in the face of racial violence and systemic oppression.

END OF SUMMARY.
