SUMMARY:  
Relatives of the victims of the Charleston church **shooting** expressed **forgiveness** toward white supremacist Dylann Roof, presenting a striking example of Christian **love** that seeks to overcome **evil** and **injustice**. The author admires this **noble** **act** of **sacrifice**, comparing it to the ultimate **sacrifice** of God’s son and to soldiers who **die** **saving** others. Yet the writer also feels **frustration**, questioning whether **responding** with **righteous rage** to a violent **assault** is any less **noble** than **passivity**. Drawing on a **secular Jew** background and **Zionist** pride, the piece contrasts the African‑American tradition of Christian **forgiveness** with a **nationalist** strand that has **responded** to **bondage**, **lynch**‑mob **abuse**, and **racism** with **rioting**, **violence**, and calls to **fight** white supremacy. The author wonders why, given centuries of **wrenching** **suffering**, African‑Americans have not turned outward to **terrorism** or broader **political violence** as some Jewish groups once did. He concludes that **White America** is “fortunate” that its most grievous **abuses** have been met largely with **forgiveness** rather than **violent** retaliation.
