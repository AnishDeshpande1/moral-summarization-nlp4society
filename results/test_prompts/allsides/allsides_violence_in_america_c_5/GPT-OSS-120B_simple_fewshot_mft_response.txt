SUMMARY:
The author praises the Charleston church‑goers’ forgiveness of white supremacist Dylann Roof as a striking act of Christian love that transcends the natural urge for vengeance, invoking the sanctity of self‑sacrificial, unmerited love central to Christian doctrine. Yet the piece also voices frustration, questioning whether such passive forgiveness is morally superior to a righteous, angry response that defends the community from its oppressors. Drawing on personal background as a secular‑Jew turned Christian, the writer highlights the tension between two Black moral traditions: the non‑violent, faith‑based loyalty to higher authority exemplified by Martin Luther King Jr., and the nationalist, militant strand represented by figures such as Malcolm X, which stresses fairness by confronting cheating power structures and defending the group against betrayal. The author suggests that, unlike many white victims who accept forgiveness, oppressed groups might justifiably choose defiant, even violent, self‑defense to uphold communal honor and protect the loyal, betrayed community. This tension underscores the interplay of authority versus subversion, care for victims, fairness against systemic cheating, loyalty to one’s people, and the sanctity of self‑sacrifice.  

END OF SUMMARY.
