SUMMARY:  
The author admires the Charleston church members’ forgiveness of shooter Dylann Roof as a powerful Christian act that mirrors the “noble” sacrifice central to Christianity, yet also feels frustration and irritation toward that response and toward white Christians who praise it. He questions whether passive forgiveness is truly more commendable than righteous anger or even violent self‑defense, drawing on his secular‑Jewish background and support for Zionist nationalism as a protective force. He contrasts the predominantly Christian, non‑violent tradition of African‑American resistance—exemplified by Martin Luther King Jr.—with a less‑known nationalist, militant strand represented by figures like Malcolm X, which embraces anger and the threat of force. The author asks why, given centuries of oppression, Black Americans have not turned outward violence against white America, and imagines that if Jews faced comparable conditions they might respond with militant retaliation rather than forgiveness. He concludes that White America is “extraordinarily fortunate” that its historically oppressed groups have largely chosen non‑confrontational, forgiving responses.  
END OF SUMMARY.
