SUMMARY:

The author reflects on the expression of forgiveness by relatives of those gunned down in Charleston's Emanuel African Methodist Episcopal Church, calling it a stunning display of Christian love and capacity for sacrifice. However, the author also expresses frustration and irritation at this response, questioning whether it is truly admirable to respond with passivity and acceptance to an act of merciless violence. The author contrasts this approach with the nationalism and willingness to countenance political violence that has characterized some aspects of the black struggle in America, particularly in response to repeated acts of racial injustice.

The author notes that African-Americans have taken a distinctively Christian approach to their suffering and oppression, which has been both beneficial and limiting. They suggest that had Jews in America lived as African-Americans do, they might have responded with more violent resistance, such as plotting an American revival of the Irgun. The article raises questions about whether passivity is truly the most effective or admirable response to systemic injustice.
