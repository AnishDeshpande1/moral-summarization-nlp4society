SUMMARY:
The author praises the Charleston church members’ public forgiveness of shooter Dylann Roof as a powerful illustration of Christian love that can transcend the instinct for vengeance, linking it to the highest Christian notion of noble, gratuitous sacrifice. Yet the author also admits feeling frustrated and irritated, questioning whether such passive forgiveness is truly more commendable than a righteous, defensive anger. Drawing on a personal background as a secular‑Jew turned Christian, the piece contrasts the African‑American tradition of Christian non‑violence—exemplified by Martin Luther King Jr.—with a parallel Black nationalist current that embraces anger and the threat of self‑defense, citing figures like Malcolm X. The author wonders why, given centuries of oppression, Black Americans have not resorted more often to outward violence, and speculates that if Jews faced similar conditions they might respond with militant retaliation rather than forgiveness. Ultimately, the essay reflects on the moral tension between noble forgiveness and the legitimacy of angry self‑preservation, suggesting that White America is “fortunate” that its victims have historically chosen the former.  
END OF SUMMARY.
