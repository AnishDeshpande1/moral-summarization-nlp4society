SUMMARY:

African-American victims of the Charleston church shooting expressed forgiveness for their white supremacist attacker, inspiring admiration from many Americans. However, some critics question whether this response is truly admirable or if it allows perpetrators to escape accountability. The author suggests that a more nuanced approach to responding to violence and oppression may be necessary. They note that African-Americans have historically been influenced by Christianity, which emphasizes forgiveness, but also point out that there has been another dimension of the black struggle in America, dominated by nationalism and a willingness to use political violence in self-defense.

The author wonders why more African-Americans do not respond with nationalist violence or calls for self-defense against persistent white supremacy. They suggest that if Jews faced similar treatment as African-Americans, they might choose a more confrontational approach. The article concludes that White America is fortunate that its victims have consistently chosen non-violence, but questions whether this is truly the most effective way to address systemic racism and oppression.
