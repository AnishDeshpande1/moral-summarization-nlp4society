SUMMARY:

The article discusses the moral implications of the forgiveness expressed by the relatives of the victims of the Charleston church shooting towards the alleged perpetrator, Dylann Roof. While the author acknowledges the nobility of this act, inspired by Christian values of gratuitous love, they also express frustration and irritation at the expectation that victims should respond with passivity and acceptance to acts of violence. The author contrasts this response with the nationalist approach of post-Holocaust Jews, who refuse to become victims again, and the less dominant nationalist dimension of the black struggle in America, which advocates for self-defense and political violence. The article questions why African-Americans, who have suffered enormous abuses, do not respond with more violence directed against white America, and notes that White America is fortunate that they have chosen a less confrontational response. The author suggests that a more radical response, inspired by nationalism and self-defense, may be morally justifiable.

END OF SUMMARY.
