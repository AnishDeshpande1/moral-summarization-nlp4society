SUMMARY: The author reflects on the forgiveness expressed by survivors of the Charleston church shooting, praising its Christian magnitude but also feeling frustration. He questions whether non‑violent, forgiving responses are inherently more admirable than righteous anger or violent self‑defense, especially given the history of African‑American resistance. While acknowledging the vital role of Christian non‑violence in the civil‑rights movement, he notes a parallel tradition of Black nationalism and militant leaders (e.g., Garvey, Malcolm X) who advocated self‑defense. He contrasts this with the predominantly forgiving stance of many Black Christians and wonders why more overt nationalist or violent retaliation against white supremacy has not emerged. Using a hypothetical scenario of American Jews facing similar oppression, he suggests they might adopt militant tactics instead of forgiveness. The piece ultimately argues that White America is “fortunate” that its oppressed groups have largely chosen less confrontational, forgiving responses to injustice. END OF SUMMARY.
