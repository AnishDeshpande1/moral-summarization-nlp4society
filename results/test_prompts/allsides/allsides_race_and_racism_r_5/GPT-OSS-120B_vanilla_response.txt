SUMMARY: President Obama urged Americans to stay optimistic about race relations after the fatal shooting of two Black men by police and a subsequent retaliatory attack that killed five Dallas police officers. Speaking in Warsaw at the NATO summit, he rejected claims that U.S. race relations have worsened to 1960s levels and highlighted declining violent crime over the past decade. Obama announced the revival of the task force created after the Michael Brown shooting, promising new members from law enforcement, community and civil‑rights groups to discuss next steps. He condemned the Dallas sniper, a Black Army veteran, as a “demented individual,” emphasizing that such actors do not represent the nation. Obama linked gun violence to broader safety concerns for police, noting widespread firearm presence in Texas and other incidents like the Philando Castile shooting. He reaffirmed U.S. commitments to counterterrorism, NATO, and a stable Brexit process, while acknowledging the complexities of globalization and inequality. END OF SUMMARY.
