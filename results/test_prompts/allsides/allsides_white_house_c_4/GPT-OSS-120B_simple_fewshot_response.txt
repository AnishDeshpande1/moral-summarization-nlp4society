SUMMARY:
In the wake of Superstorm Sandy’s massive devastation, the White House is preparing an emergency funding request of $50 billion‑$60 billion to Congress, urging that a significant portion be earmarked for rebuilding and for strengthening the region’s resilience against future storms. Senators from the hardest‑hit states report staggering losses—over 300,000 homes, 265,000 businesses, and tens of thousands of homeless residents—with total damage estimates exceeding $80 billion across the Northeast. HUD Secretary Shaun Donovan stresses that aid must go beyond a quick “down payment” and instead fund smarter, safer reconstruction now, from sturdier materials to underground power lines where feasible. FEMA chief Craig Fugate warns that some mitigation measures can create new problems. With only $4.8 billion left in the disaster relief fund and competing budget pressures, Democrats push for full, immediate assistance, while Republicans remain uncertain about the scale and timing of the aid. The moral imperative is clear: delay or partial aid would prolong suffering for families, small businesses, and whole communities still reeling from the storm’s aftermath.  
END OF SUMMARY.
