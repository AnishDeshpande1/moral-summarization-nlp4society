SUMMARY:  
In the wake of Hurricane Sandy, federal officials confront a stark moral dilemma: billions of dollars in damage versus a dwindling disaster fund. The White House plans to request $50‑$60 billion from Congress, urging that a portion be earmarked for rebuilding and for fortifying the region against future storms—a call to “build back stronger, smarter, safer.” Senators highlighted the human toll—over 300,000 homes, 265,000 businesses, and thousands of homeless New Yorkers—and estimated state damages at a conservative $32.8 billion, with the Northeast seeking about $80 billion in aid. HUD Secretary Shaun Donovan stressed the ethical urgency of immediate, not delayed, assistance, warning that a modest “down payment” would prolong hardship for families and communities. FEMA’s Craig Fugate noted the limits of the remaining $4.8 billion disaster relief fund and cautioned against offsetting aid with budget cuts. While Democrats broadly support full funding, the political battle with Republicans remains uncertain. The core moral framing is clear: the nation must act responsibly and compassionately now to help victims recover and to protect future generations from similar devastation.  

END OF SUMMARY.
