SUMMARY: Mayors across the United States are confronting a grim circle of mass‑shootings that leave behind deaths, tragedy and agonizing loss. The violence, often driven by racist malice and hate speech broadcast from powerful halls, gives extremists easy access to military‑grade firepower that can wreak havoc and destroy lives. With Washington stalled, local leaders are taking action, empowering young people to call out intolerance and to push for concrete steps that keep guns out of the hands of those who shouldn’t have them. Personal histories—Gilroy, Dayton and El Paso—link the author’s ancestors’ flight from persecution to today’s mourning of needless loss. Youth councils, formed after the Marjory Stoneman Douglas shooting, now advise mayors, fight normalized gun culture, and reject accepting a status quo that normalizes bullet‑proof backpacks. Though the news can make citizens feel discouraged, the collective effort aims to create a nation where everyone feels safe and belongs, an inheritance the author hopes to leave to future generations. END OF SUMMARY.
