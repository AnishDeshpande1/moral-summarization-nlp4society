SUMMARY:
Mayors across the United States—from Las Vegas to Orlando, San Bernardino to Parkland—are confronting a relentless wave of mass shootings driven by hate, racism and inflammatory rhetoric. With Washington stalled, they are refusing to rely on NRA‑style talking points and are instead mobilizing local action, especially by empowering young people who demand an end to hate speech and stricter gun controls. The author ties this fight to personal family histories of fleeing oppression—Gilroy, Dayton and El Paso symbolize the nation’s promise of freedom and safety that their ancestors sought. At the inaugural US Conference of Mayors Youth Summit, youth leaders from the author’s own Youth Council to End Gun Violence presented campaigns against normalized gun violence, challenging the market for “bullet‑proof backpacks.” Though the work is painful and often feels overwhelming, the mayor stresses that perseverance, guided by the hopes of immigrant forebears, can build a nation where everyone belongs, feels safe, and can inherit a legacy of liberty for future generations. END OF SUMMARY.
