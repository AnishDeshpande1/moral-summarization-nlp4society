SUMMARY:  
In Kentucky, a protest organized by Take Back Kentucky culminated in the hanging of an effigy of Democratic Governor Andy Beshear outside the state capitol, an act the demonstrators framed as a stand against what they called an “unconstitutional shutdown” and a celebration of the Second Amendment. The effigy, bearing Beshear’s likeness and the Latin warning “sic temper tyrannis” (“thus always to tyrants”), was displayed briefly before being removed. The stunt provoked swift moral rebuke across the political spectrum. Republican Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell, while affirming First‑Amendment rights, called the act “unacceptable” and warned there is “no place for hate in Kentucky.” Republican Secretary of State Michael G. Adams denounced it as “disgusting,” invoking the legacy of John Wilkes Booth. Democratic leaders, including Louisville Mayor Greg Fischer and Kentucky House Democrats, condemned the effigy as “vile,” “reprehensible,” and antithetical to the sacrifices made during the pandemic, urging a return to science, safety, and true freedom. The incident highlights a moral clash over protest tactics and the perceived limits of dissent.  
END OF SUMMARY.
