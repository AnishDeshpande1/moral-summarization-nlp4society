SUMMARY:
The hanging of two men in Iran—possibly for consensual sodomy—highlights a paradox in U.S. politics. While the executions are a clear human‑rights atrocity, they have become a low‑priority issue for the Obama administration, which is focused on diplomatic engagement with Tehran to curb its nuclear program and stabilize Iraq. Conservatives, who normally oppose LGBT rights at home, seize the incident to condemn Obama’s “engagement” policy, portraying themselves as champions of gay rights only when it serves their hawkish agenda. This selective advocacy exemplifies Jasbir Puar’s concept of “homonationalism”: the United States imposes its own liberal sexual norms abroad, using LGBT concerns as a tool for foreign‑policy pressure while ignoring broader cultural contexts. Consequently, both the administration and the broader LGBT movement are unlikely to mount a strong outcry, lest they jeopardize delicate diplomatic efforts. The tragedy thus illustrates how human‑rights rhetoric can be instrumentalized, and why the U.S. response may remain muted despite the horror of the executions.  
END OF SUMMARY.
