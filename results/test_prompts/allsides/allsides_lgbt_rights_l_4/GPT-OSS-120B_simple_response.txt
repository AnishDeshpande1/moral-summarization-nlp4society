SUMMARY:  
The hanging of two men in Iran—likely for consensual homosexuality—highlights a paradox in U.S. politics: while the atrocity should spur outrage, the Obama administration’s diplomatic strategy with Tehran limits a strong response. Progressives and conservatives alike condemn Iran’s anti‑LGBT violence, yet conservatives seize the issue to attack the administration’s “engagement” policy, framing gay‑rights defense as a weapon against Obama. This reflects Jasbir Puar’s concept of “homonationalism,” where Western powers champion LGBT equality abroad only when it serves geopolitical aims, often imposing Western sexual norms and undermining indigenous gender concepts. Consequently, despite the horror of the executions, both the U.S. government and the broader LGBT movement are unlikely to mount a vigorous campaign, fearing that vocal criticism could jeopardize strategic interests in Iran and the wider Middle East. The article warns that well‑intentioned advocacy might backfire, making the situation even worse.  
END OF SUMMARY.
