SUMMARY: Two men, Abdullah Ghavami Chahzanjiru and Salman Ghanbari Chahzanjiri, were hanged in southern Iran on August 6, possibly for consensual homosexual acts. Their deaths occur amid a broader wave of Iranian executions—over 400 in the first half of 2014. While the exact charges remain unclear, the case highlights Iran’s harsh anti‑LGBT laws and raises questions about U.S. response. The Obama administration, pursuing diplomatic engagement with Iran over its nuclear program and regional stability, is reluctant to confront Iran on LGBT rights, fearing it could jeopardize strategic cooperation. Conversely, U.S. conservatives have seized the incident to criticize the administration, invoking “homonationalism” – the use of LGBT rights abroad to advance foreign policy aims while often opposing LGBTQ equality at home. Scholars argue this reflects a Western imposition of its own sexual norms on other cultures. Consequently, both the administration and the broader LGBT movement are unlikely to mount a strong public outcry, as doing so could complicate delicate diplomatic efforts. END OF SUMMARY.
