SUMMARY: The First Step Act, a bipartisan criminal‑justice reform bill, cleared the Senate 87‑12, uniting progressives, law‑and‑order conservatives, and public figures in a moral push to heal a broken system. Advocates frame the legislation as a compassionate correction to a war‑on‑drugs legacy that has over‑incarcerated non‑violent offenders, especially the poor, mentally ill, and addicted. By restoring judicial discretion, reducing mandatory minimums, ending harsh practices (e.g., shackling pregnant women), and expanding rehabilitation and re‑entry programs, the act promises safety, hope, and a second chance for those who earn it. Leaders from Trump to Cory Booker and the ACLU present the bill as a historic, moral step toward “unwinding the catastrophic harm of the incarceration industry” and offering dignity to thousands of inmates, even as it affects only federal prisoners (under 10% of the total prison population). END OF SUMMARY.
