SUMMARY: The First Step Act, a bipartisan criminal‑justice reform bill, cleared the Senate 87‑12. It gives judges discretion to waive mandatory minimums for non‑violent drug offenses, reduces those minimums, ends “three‑strikes” life sentences (except for serious violent felonies), and eliminates certain firearm‑stacking provisions. The law expands rehabilitation programs, bans shackling of pregnant inmates, provides release pathways for elderly or terminally ill prisoners, and retroactively applies the 2010 Fair Sentencing Act to address crack‑powder sentencing disparities, potentially freeing up to 4,000 inmates. Though it affects only federal prisoners (under 10% of U.S. inmates), supporters—including progressives, law‑and‑order conservatives, public figures, and President Trump—hail it as a historic step toward reducing mass incarceration and saving billions. Critics note it is imperfect but a necessary move. The bill now returns to the House for a final vote before presidential signature. END OF SUMMARY.
