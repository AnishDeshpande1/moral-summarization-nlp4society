SUMMARY:
The Senate’s 87‑12 passage of the First Step Act marks a rare bipartisan triumph, uniting progressive reformers and law‑and‑order conservatives around a moral imperative to curb the nation’s over‑incarceration of non‑violent drug offenders. The bill loosens mandatory minimums, lets judges use discretion, expands rehabilitation, ends inhumane practices such as shackling pregnant inmates, and offers compassionate release for elderly or terminally ill prisoners. It also retroactively applies the Fair Sentencing Act, potentially freeing thousands serving disproportionate crack‑cocaine sentences. Leaders from both parties—Chuck Grassley, Dick Durbin, Cory Booker—and public figures like Patricia Arquette and Kim Kardashian praised the legislation as a “first step” toward healing communities damaged by mass incarceration. While the ACLU notes the law is imperfect, it welcomes the common‑sense, humane approach that promises safer communities, billions in savings, and a second chance for those who earn it. The act now heads back to the House and, if signed by President Trump, will become law, affecting only federal inmates but signaling a moral shift in criminal‑justice policy.

END OF SUMMARY.
