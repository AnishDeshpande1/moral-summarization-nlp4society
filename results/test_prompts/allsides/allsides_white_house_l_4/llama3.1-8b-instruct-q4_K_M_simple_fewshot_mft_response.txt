SUMMARY:

President Obama's foreign policy approach, dubbed the "Obama Doctrine," has been marked by a lack of commitment to lasting action and a focus on appearances over realities. He often appears to care about suffering innocents when it's convenient for him, but ultimately does little to combat evil. This pattern is evident in his handling of crises in Egypt, Iran, Ukraine, Nigeria, Syria, and now Iraq, where he drops bombs and blusters, only to move on to the next crisis, leaving allies to die. In contrast, former President George W. Bush recognized the need for change and instituted a sweeping reordering of his administration, its policies, and priorities in his second term, leading to better results in region after region. The author argues that Obama needs to recognize when he has made mistakes and have the ability to admit them, like Bush did, in order to produce constructive change.

Bush's willingness to admit errors and seek outside perspectives into his second-term team is highlighted as a key factor in his success, whereas Obama's aloofness from his Cabinet has contributed to damaged relations and crises. The article concludes that Obama can learn from Bush's example and grow in office by recognizing the need for big change and standing up to himself to demand administration-wide change.
