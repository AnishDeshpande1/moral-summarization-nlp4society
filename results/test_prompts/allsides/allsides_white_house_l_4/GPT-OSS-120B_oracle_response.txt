SUMMARY:  
Since 9/11, U.S. foreign policy has swung from the unilateralist excess of Bush’s first term—highlighted by the invasion of Iraq—to Obama’s disengagement and dithering. The author argues that Obama’s errors have strained relations in Syria, Iraq, Libya, Egypt, Israel, Ukraine, Afghanistan and Africa, and have even led to domestic crises such as border problems and NSA eavesdropping. Yet the American system can change if leaders admit their mistakes. The author points to George W. Bush as a model who, after many errors, instituted a sweeping reordering of his administration: he replaced and modified key officials, rewrote policies, and launched the Iraq surge, initiating a “light‑footprint” terror strategy and responding to the financial crisis. Bush also brought new perspectives, bucked his aides when needed, and restored confidence, actions that contrasted with Obama’s tendency to sideline his Cabinet. The moral lesson is that, amid strains and dysfunctions, a president must give up blame, not punt the problem, and stand to lead by admitting mistakes and demanding internal change.  

END OF SUMMARY.
