SUMMARY:
The post‑9/11 era has been marked by a succession of flawed U.S. foreign‑policy swings—from Bush’s unilateral Iraq invasion to Obama’s hesitant disengagement—leaving the nation bruised and its interests compromised. While many of these missteps stem from systemic vulnerability, the author argues they are correctable through honest self‑assessment. Citing George W. Bush’s second‑term turnaround—where he publicly recognized his errors, reshuffled his team, adopted new strategies like the Iraq surge, and welcomed outside expertise—the piece holds up Bush’s willingness to “stand up to himself” as a moral exemplar for Obama. By contrast, Obama’s aloof, White‑House‑centric style has alienated cabinet members and stalled decisive action. The author urges Obama to emulate Bush’s humility and willingness to admit mistakes, using that accountability to drive comprehensive, administration‑wide reforms and restore effective foreign policy. Only by confronting his own failures can Obama lead the United States toward constructive change.

END OF SUMMARY.
