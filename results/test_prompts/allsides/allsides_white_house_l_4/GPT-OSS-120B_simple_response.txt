SUMMARY:  
The author argues that U.S. foreign policy since 9/11 has been marked by a succession of flawed approaches, from Bush’s unilateralism and the Iraq invasion to Obama’s reactive disengagement. While many of these missteps stem from a climate of insecurity and political dysfunction, the piece emphasizes a moral lesson: effective leadership requires the humility to recognize and own one’s errors. The writer cites George W. Bush’s second term as a model—he admitted past blunders, reshuffled his team, adopted new strategies (the Iraq surge, “light footprint” counter‑terrorism, financial crisis response), and brought in outside perspectives, resulting in measurable improvements. In contrast, Obama is portrayed as aloof, sidelining his cabinet and missing opportunities for internal reform. The moral framing urges Obama to emulate Bush’s willingness to confront his own failings, to lead a systemic change from within, and thereby restore competent, ethical U.S. foreign policy.  

END OF SUMMARY.
