SUMMARY: The article argues that U.S. foreign policy since 9/11 has been marked by successive missteps, first under Bush’s unilateralism and the Iraq invasion, then under Obama’s reactive disengagement. While many challenges stem from a prolonged sense of vulnerability, the author contends that human error can be corrected if leaders acknowledge their mistakes. He cites George W. Bush’s second term as a model: Bush recognized his earlier errors, overhauled his staff, adopted new strategies such as the Iraq surge, and embraced outside perspectives, leading to improved outcomes in war zones, diplomacy, and domestic issues. In contrast, Obama is portrayed as aloof, sidelining his cabinet and failing to foster internal change. The piece suggests Obama should emulate Bush’s willingness to admit faults and drive comprehensive reform to address current foreign‑policy crises. END OF SUMMARY.
