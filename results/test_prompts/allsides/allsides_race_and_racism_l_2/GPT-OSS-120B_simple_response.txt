SUMMARY: The author likens today’s Black‑Lives‑Matter protests to the 1960s civil‑rights generation, recalling his own 1960 sit‑in at a whites‑only Greenville library that sparked a lifelong fight against segregation. He recounts how legal victories—Public Accommodations Act (1964), Voting Rights Act (1965)—opened doors for Black Americans, yet structural racism persisted in housing, education, employment, and criminal justice. The murder of George Floyd ignited the largest protest movement in U.S. history, with 15‑26 million participants across 2,500 locations, including unprecedented white involvement, prompting policy shifts such as police reforms and the removal of Confederate symbols. The author warns that entrenched interests will resist, but stresses the moral imperative: as the 1960s showed, collective, conscience‑driven action can overcome seemingly insurmountable injustice. He calls on anyone with a conscience to join this generational struggle for true equality.  
END OF SUMMARY.
