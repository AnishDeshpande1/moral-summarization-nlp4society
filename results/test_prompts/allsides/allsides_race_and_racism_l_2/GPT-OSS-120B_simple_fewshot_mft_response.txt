SUMMARY:
The article recounts the author’s 1960 sit‑in at a whites‑only Greenville library as a bold subversion of Jim Crow authority, illustrating the moral courage that challenged unlawful segregation and demanded care for Black citizens denied basic services. It links that historic fight for fairness—ending legal apartheid through the Public Accommodations Act and the Voting Rights Act—to today’s massive protests sparked by George Floyd’s murder, which have mobilized millions across 2,500 locations, including unprecedented white participation. The new movement again confronts entrenched authority, seeking to correct systemic harms such as red‑lined housing, unequal schools, discriminatory hiring, and a biased criminal‑justice system. Recent political responses—police‑reform measures and the removal of Mississippi’s Confederate flag—show that fairness can be reclaimed when collective moral pressure confronts unjust power structures. The author urges anyone with a conscience to join this generational uprising, emphasizing that subverting oppressive authority, caring for the oppressed, and demanding equitable treatment are essential to achieving lasting justice.

END OF SUMMARY.
