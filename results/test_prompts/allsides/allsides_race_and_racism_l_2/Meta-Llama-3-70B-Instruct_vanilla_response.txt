SUMMARY:

The author reflects on the 1960s civil rights movement, which he was a part of, and draws parallels with the current generation of protesters fighting against racial injustice. He recalls the segregated society of Greenville, South Carolina, where he grew up, and the protests that led to the desegregation of public facilities and the passage of the Voting Rights Act. Although significant progress was made, the author notes that racism and biased institutional structures persist. The current movement, sparked by George Floyd's murder, has seen unprecedented participation, with 15-26 million Americans protesting in 2,500 locations. The author is hopeful that this new generation can bring about meaningful change, but acknowledges that they will face resistance from entrenched interests. He urges everyone with a conscience to join the movement, which has the potential to make America better and improve the lives of millions.

END OF SUMMARY.
