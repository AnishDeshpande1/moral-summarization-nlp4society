SUMMARY:
The article draws a moral parallel between today’s Black‑Lives‑Matter protests and the 1960s civil‑rights movement, arguing that a new, multiracial generation is awakening to confront the unfinished work of racial justice in America. The author recounts personal experience of being arrested in 1960 for challenging library segregation, celebrates the landmark victories of the Public Accommodations Act (1964) and the Voting Rights Act (1965), and acknowledges that legal segregation was abolished but systemic racism persists in housing, education, employment and criminal justice. Massive demonstrations after George Floyd’s murder—participated in by 15‑26 million people across 2,500 locations, with unprecedented white involvement—have already spurred policy shifts such as police‑reform measures and the removal of Confederate symbols. The piece warns that entrenched interests will resist, but urges anyone with a conscience to join the moral struggle, emphasizing that, as history showed, collective, conscience‑driven action can overturn oppressive structures and build a more equitable America. END OF SUMMARY.
