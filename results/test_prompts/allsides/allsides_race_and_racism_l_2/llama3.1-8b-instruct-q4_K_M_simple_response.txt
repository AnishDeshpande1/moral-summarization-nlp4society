SUMMARY:

The author, a veteran of the civil rights movement, reflects on the parallels between the current generation's protests against racial injustice and their own struggle 60 years ago. They recall being arrested in 1960 for attempting to integrate a whites-only library in Greenville, South Carolina, and how the movement eventually led to landmark legislation like the Public Accommodations Act and Voting Rights Act. Despite these gains, the author notes that institutional racism still persists, and the new generation's protests are crucial in addressing systemic inequalities. The demonstrations following George Floyd's murder have been historic, with 15-26 million Americans participating and politicians beginning to respond with reforms. However, the movement will face resistance from entrenched interests, but as history has shown, "when people move, change is possible." The author urges anyone with a conscience to join this extraordinary movement for a better America.

END OF SUMMARY.
