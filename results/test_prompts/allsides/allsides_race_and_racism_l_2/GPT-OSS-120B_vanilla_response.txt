SUMMARY: The author reflects on the civil‑rights struggle that began with a 1960 sit‑in at a whites‑only library in Greenville, South Carolina, and traces how that generation dismantled legal segregation through the Public Accommodations Act (1964) and the Voting Rights Act (1965). Despite these victories, systemic racism persists in housing, education, employment, and criminal justice. The piece links this legacy to the current wave of protests sparked by George Floyd’s murder, noting unprecedented participation—15‑26 million Americans in over 2,500 locations, with significant white involvement—and early policy responses such as police reforms and the removal of Confederate symbols. The author argues that, as in the 1960s, broad grassroots activism can overcome entrenched power structures, urging anyone with a conscience to join the movement for racial and economic justice. END OF SUMMARY.
