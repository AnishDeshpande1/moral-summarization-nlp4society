SUMMARY:

The author reflects on the rise of a new generation protesting against racial injustice, reminiscent of the civil rights movement 60 years ago. The author recounts their personal experience of participating in a sit-in at a whites-only library in 1960, which marked a turning point in their life. Despite the progress made since then, the author acknowledges that racism persists, and biased institutional structures still enforce racial injustice. The current wave of protests, sparked by George Floyd's murder, holds great promise for America, with 15-26 million Americans participating in demonstrations across 2,500 locations. The movement's focus on reforming areas like criminal injustice, economic inequalities, and basic rights threatens the privileged and powerful, but the author believes that when people move, change is possible. This is a moral imperative, and anyone with a conscience should join this extraordinary movement to make America better and improve the lives of millions.

END OF SUMMARY.
