SUMMARY:  
The rise of a new generation protesting racial injustice is driving change that has emerged from the same moral struggle that built the civil‑rights movement of the 1960s. In 1960 the author was arrested for violating segregation laws when he and friends walked into a whites‑only library, an act that highlighted the lie of “separate but equal” and the violation of constitutional rights. Their protests spread, burst the bubble of segregation, and claimed basic rights through sit‑ins and demonstrations despite violent resistance, being denounced, tarred and attacked by vigilantes and police. The movement kept building, won the Public Accommodations Act and the Voting Rights Act, ended legal apartheid, and opened the way for desegregated schools, universities, and institutions, breaking and transforming a biased system that still enforces racism, red‑lining, unequal schools, wage discrimination and a discriminatory criminal‑justice system. Today, after George Floyd’s murder, an emerging wave of protest—larger than ever, with millions joining and white allies participating—has challenged injustices, prompting reforms such as police policy changes and the retirement of Confederate symbols. The struggle faces desperate attempts to discredit it and entrenched forces that resist change, but the moral force of this generation can stake a better future for all who join.  
END OF SUMMARY.
