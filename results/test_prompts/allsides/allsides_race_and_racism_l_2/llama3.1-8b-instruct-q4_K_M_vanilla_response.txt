SUMMARY:

The author reflects on the civil rights movement of the 1960s, drawing parallels between that era and the current wave of protests against racial injustice in America. The author recalls their own experience participating in sit-ins and demonstrations as a young person, facing violent resistance but ultimately contributing to significant changes, including the passage of the Public Accommodations Act and the Voting Rights Act.

The author notes that while progress was made, racism and institutionalized inequality persist, and Dr. King's drive for economic equality was cut short by his assassination. However, the current generation of protesters is showing great promise, with widespread demonstrations, white participation exceeding that in the 1960s, and politicians responding with reforms.

The author concludes that this new generation has an opportunity to create lasting change, but will face resistance from entrenched interests. The movement's focus on reforming areas such as criminal injustice, economic inequalities, and basic rights holds great potential for improving the lives of millions.

END OF SUMMARY.
