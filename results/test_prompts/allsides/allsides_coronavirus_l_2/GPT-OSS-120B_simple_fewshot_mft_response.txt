SUMMARY:
The article argues that keeping children safe in schools is a matter of caring for the most vulnerable and respecting legitimate authority, not of defying public‑health guidance. It notes that severe COVID‑19 illness in kids is rare and far less lethal than flu, so school closures are not justified on that basis alone. The primary way to protect students and staff is through community‑wide control of the virus, following the CDC’s authoritative recommendations: wear masks, wash hands, maintain distance, and implement comprehensive testing, isolation, contact tracing and supportive quarantine. Schools should obey these expert guidelines and adapt them locally, shielding high‑risk children, older employees and those with health conditions by allowing remote participation until transmission is low. Risk‑reducing measures such as eliminating large assemblies, modifying cafeterias, limiting shared surfaces, and adjusting extracurricular activities reflect a care‑oriented approach that prioritizes health while still providing essential services. In areas with uncontrolled spread, the authority‑based decision may be to keep schools virtual; where transmission is low, schools can reopen safely by following the eight CDC‑endorsed safety steps. The moral thrust is that respecting public‑health authority and protecting vulnerable lives are the ethical foundations for school‑reopening decisions.

END OF SUMMARY.
