SUMMARY:
The United States has lost more than 54,000 nursing‑home residents and workers to COVID‑19, accounting for over 40 % of the nation’s coronavirus deaths despite seniors comprising less than 1 % of the population. The crisis exposes deep flaws in the elder‑care system—under‑funded facilities, inadequate oversight, low staff wages, and profit‑driven structures—that have left the most vulnerable to harm. Experts argue that these systemic failures constitute a subversion of the authority entrusted to protect the elderly, calling for a radical redesign of care. Proposals emphasize a compassionate shift toward government‑funded at‑home services, advanced monitoring technology, and community support, aiming to reduce suffering and safeguard seniors. Critics caution that any overhaul must avoid abandoning low‑income and minority elders, urging stronger public investment, equitable health policies, and accountable leadership to ensure care is humane and just. The pandemic thus becomes a moral imperative to confront authority’s neglect and to prioritize the health and dignity of America’s aging citizens.  
END OF SUMMARY.
