SUMMARY: The COVID‑19 pandemic has exposed a moral crisis in U.S. elder‑care: over 54,000 deaths in nursing homes—more than any nation except Brazil—show that a system built on underfunded, understaffed facilities and profit‑driven shortcuts fails its most vulnerable citizens. Although seniors are less than 1 % of the population, they account for over 40 % of the nation’s coronavirus deaths, highlighting the injustice of congregate care that sacrifices dignity for efficiency. Experts argue that this tragedy demands a moral reckoning: either radically expand publicly funded at‑home care, leveraging technology and community support to let elders age with autonomy, or, if reforms simply pour more money into the existing model, risk deepening inequities that leave poor and minority seniors behind. Voices from the press call for government action, media attention, and innovative family‑centered solutions, urging society to honor the right of older adults to live safely and respectfully rather than be consigned to unsafe institutions. The pandemic, while tragic, could become a catalyst for a more compassionate, equitable elder‑care system. END OF SUMMARY.
