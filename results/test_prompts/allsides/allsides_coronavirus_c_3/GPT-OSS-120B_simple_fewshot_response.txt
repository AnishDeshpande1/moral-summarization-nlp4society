SUMMARY:
COVID‑19 has exposed a deadly crisis in U.S. nursing homes, where more than 54,000 residents and staff have died, accounting for over 40 % of the nation’s coronavirus fatalities despite representing less than 1 % of the population. Experts argue the pandemic has laid bare systemic flaws—underfunding, low wages, inadequate oversight and profit‑driven models—that demand moral action. One view calls for a radical shift toward government‑funded, at‑home elder care, leveraging technology and community support to let seniors age safely in familiar surroundings. Another warns that such a transformation could marginalize low‑income and minority seniors unless equity measures accompany it, suggesting instead that increased funding, better pay for staff, and stronger accountability could improve existing facilities. The debate centers on a moral imperative: either overhaul the elder‑care system to protect the most vulnerable or risk repeating these tragedies as the elderly population nearly doubles by 2050.  

END OF SUMMARY.
