SUMMARY:  
COVID‑19 has killed at least 54,000 U.S. nursing‑home residents and workers, accounting for over 40 % of the nation’s roughly 131,000 coronavirus deaths despite representing less than 1 % of the population. The high fatality rate—three times the national average—exposes longstanding flaws in the American elder‑care system, including underfunding, staffing shortages, inadequate oversight, and financial incentives that compromise care quality. With the elderly population projected to nearly double by 2050, experts debate how to reform the system. One view advocates a shift toward government‑funded, technology‑enhanced at‑home care, which could be cheaper and improve outcomes while reserving nursing homes for the most medically complex cases. Critics warn that such a transition could disadvantage low‑income and minority seniors lacking stable housing or resources, arguing instead for increased funding, better staff wages, and stronger accountability within existing facilities. The pandemic has thrust these issues into the spotlight, prompting calls for comprehensive policy changes to prepare for an aging society.  

END OF SUMMARY.
