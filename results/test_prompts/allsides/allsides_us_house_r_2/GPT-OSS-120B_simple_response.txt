SUMMARY:
The House opened the first public hearing of the impeachment inquiry into President Donald Trump, framing the process as a moral test of whether a commander‑in‑chief can abuse the highest office for personal political gain. Chairman Adam Schiff warned that the answer will shape “the future of the presidency itself” and the standards Americans should expect of their leader. The hearing, broadcast live, featured career diplomats William Taylor and George Kent describing a president who allegedly leveraged Ukraine aid to pressure President Zelenskyy into investigating political rivals. The inquiry, sparked by a whistleblower’s report of Trump’s July 25 call, pits constitutional duty against alleged “high crimes and misdemeanors,” while Trump denounces it as a “witch hunt.” Despite partisan splits, the proceedings underscore the constitutional check on executive overreach, echoing past impeachments yet unfolding under unprecedented real‑time scrutiny. Whether this moment ends a presidency or fortifies Trump’s hold on power, it represents a rare instance where a president’s conduct is placed before the nation’s moral judgment.  
END OF SUMMARY.
