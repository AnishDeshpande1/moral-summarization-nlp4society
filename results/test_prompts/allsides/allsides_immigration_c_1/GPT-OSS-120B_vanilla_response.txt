SUMMARY: The Trump administration announced new visa regulations that will deny tourist visas to applicants deemed to be traveling primarily for “birth tourism”—the practice of giving birth in the United States to secure citizenship for the child. Consular officers must now determine whether a traveler’s main purpose is childbirth versus a legitimate medical need, and those with genuine medical reasons must prove they can cover all costs. The rules, effective Friday, aim to close what officials call an “immigration loophole,” protect taxpayers, and address perceived national‑security risks. While birth tourism is legal, it has spawned a lucrative industry, with agencies charging up to $80,000 to arrange trips, often from Russia and China. The State Department states the activity is not a legitimate recreational purpose. Critics warn the policy may endanger pregnant women and raise enforcement challenges, as officers cannot directly ask about pregnancy. The rule follows broader immigration restrictions, including turning away pregnant asylum seekers at the U.S.–Mexico border. Estimates suggest tens of thousands of foreign‑born women gave birth in the U.S. in 2012 and then left. The administration claims the measure will curb criminal activity linked to the birth‑tourism market. END OF SUMMARY.
