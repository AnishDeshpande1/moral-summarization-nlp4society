SUMMARY:
New Pew Research data shows a sharp rise in American support for gun‑rights, now at a 20‑year high, as 52 % say the Second Amendment outweighs gun control. The shift is especially notable among Black voters, many of whom now view firearms as protection against crime—a change that the pollsters describe as “dramatic.” Proponents argue that owning guns helps safeguard families and communities, framing the issue as a matter of **care**: the right to defend oneself from harm.  

Opponents counter that the growing emphasis on rights overlooks the **harm** caused by gun violence and point to broad public backing for specific safety measures—universal background checks enjoy about 90 % support, and a slim majority favor limits on rifles and magazines. They frame the debate as one of **fairness**, insisting that all citizens deserve equal protection from gun‑related danger and that the current tilt toward rights “cheats” public safety.  

Both sides cite the Newtown tragedy to underscore their moral stakes: gun‑rights advocates stress the need for personal protection, while gun‑control advocates stress collective responsibility to prevent further loss of life. The data reveal a deep partisan split, yet the underlying moral contest remains between protecting individuals from harm and ensuring fair, community‑wide safety.  
END OF SUMMARY.
