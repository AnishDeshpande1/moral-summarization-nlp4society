SUMMARY:  
New Pew Research data reveal a dramatic rebound in American support for gun rights, reaching the highest level in two decades—52 % now say the Second Amendment outweighs gun control, up 7 points since the 2012 Sandy Hook tragedy. The surge is especially notable among Black voters, whose view of firearms as a public‑safety benefit jumped from 29 % to 54 % in two years. This reversal comes despite President Obama’s post‑Sandy Hook push for stricter laws and continued Democratic calls for new restrictions, exemplified by Nancy Pelosi’s pledge to act. While universal background checks still enjoy about 90 % approval and modest majorities back limits on rifles and magazines, the overall partisan split has widened: Republicans and independents now largely favor gun rights, whereas Democrats remain largely opposed. Critics argue the poll’s wording—framing the issue as “protecting a right” versus “controlling ownership”—may have inflated pro‑rights numbers. Nonetheless, the trend marks a 20‑point decline from the 66 % gun‑control peak of the late 1990s and cuts across gender, age, and most racial lines, with only Hispanics showing a slight dip in gun‑rights support.  

END OF SUMMARY.
