SUMMARY:
New Pew Research data shows a dramatic resurgence of support for gun rights, reaching the highest level in two decades. Now 52 percent of Americans say the Second Amendment outweighs gun‑control measures, up seven points since the 2012 Sandy Hook tragedy. The shift is especially notable among black voters, whose view of firearms as a safety tool has risen from 29 percent to 54 percent, while fewer see guns as a public‑safety risk. Support for gun rights has risen across gender, age, race and party lines, with whites moving from 53 percent to 61 percent and Republicans and independents now favoring the right to own guns over additional controls. Nevertheless, broad majorities still back specific safety measures: about 90 percent favor universal background checks and roughly half support limits on rifles and high‑capacity magazines. Critics argue the poll’s wording—framing the issue as “protecting rights” versus “controlling ownership”—inflates pro‑gun sentiment, and they caution that the moral balance remains between safeguarding communities from gun violence and preserving constitutional freedoms. The findings underscore a deepening partisan divide as lawmakers continue to debate how best to protect citizens while respecting the right to bear arms.  
END OF SUMMARY.
