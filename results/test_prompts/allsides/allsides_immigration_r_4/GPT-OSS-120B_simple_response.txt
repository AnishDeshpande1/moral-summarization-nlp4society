SUMMARY:
The author argues that law enforcement is essential not merely to punish individual offenders but to sustain a civil society through deterrence—the fear of punishment that compels obedience. Citing George Savile and Kant, the piece warns that selective or lax enforcement—whether in “sanctuary cities,” the presence of millions of undocumented migrants, campus protests, or celebrity threats toward the president—creates a contagious precedent that erodes all laws. When one violation goes unpunished, it signals that other statutes can be ignored, encouraging further disobedience and threatening the rights of all, including conservatives and political leaders. Restoring uniform, prompt enforcement would re‑establish deterrence, likely preventing phenomena such as the Central American caravan, violent campus demonstrations, and “assassination chic” among celebrities. The moral thrust is that a robust, impartial legal system is the only safeguard against the spread of lawlessness and the decay of civil order.  
END OF SUMMARY.
