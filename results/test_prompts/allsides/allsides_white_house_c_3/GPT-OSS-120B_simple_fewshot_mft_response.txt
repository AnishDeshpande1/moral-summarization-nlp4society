SUMMARY:
The United States and Russia announced a diplomatic agreement to secure and destroy Syria’s chemical‑weapons stockpile, removing the imminent threat of U.S. military action that President Obama had been trying to justify to Congress and the public. By insisting on “verify and verify,” Secretary of State John Kerry framed the deal as an exercise of responsible authority, holding both the Syrian regime and Russia accountable to international standards. The agreement‑terms—complete disclosure within a week, removal or destruction by 2014, inspections starting in November, and any violations referred to the U.N. Security Council—reflect a moral commitment to protect civilians from further chemical attacks, emphasizing the care/harm foundation. At the same time, the deal demands fairness: Syria must honor its commitments, and the U.S. will not accept empty promises, while Russia’s role is scrutinized to ensure it does not enable Assad’s cheating of the chemical‑weapons ban. Critics note the arrangement’s political risks and the “leap of faith” required, but observers argue that, despite poor optics, the substance safeguards lives and upholds a fair, authoritative response to a grave humanitarian threat.  
END OF SUMMARY.
