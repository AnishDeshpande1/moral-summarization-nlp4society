SUMMARY:
The United States and Russia have forged a last‑minute agreement to secure and destroy Syria’s chemical‑weapons stockpile, removing the immediate threat of U.S. military strikes that President Obama had been trying to sell to Congress and the public. While the deal was driven by Russian diplomacy, the Obama administration entered it knowingly, stressing the need for verification and insisting that Assad must honor his commitments. President Obama and Secretary of State John Kerry warned that trust in Putin and Assad will be hard‑won, but framed the pact as a moral imperative to prevent further chemical attacks on civilians. The framework requires Syria to disclose its arsenal, destroy the weapons by 2014 and submit to inspections, with any violations to be referred to the UN Security Council. Critics note the deal’s reliance on Russian and Syrian good faith, but experts argue that, despite poor optics at home, it represents a substantive diplomatic win that averts war and curbs Assad’s capacity for mass murder. The agreement thus reflects a cautious, ethically driven choice to prioritize civilian safety over military action.

END OF SUMMARY.
