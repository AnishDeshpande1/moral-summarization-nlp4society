SUMMARY:
The United States and Russia announced an agreement to secure Syria’s chemical‑weapons stockpile, removing the immediate threat of U.S. military action. After two weeks of saber‑rattling and an unpopular push for a limited strike, diplomatic caution prevailed. President Obama acknowledged that trusting Russia and Assad would be a leap of faith, noting the tense relationship with Putin and the need for concrete verification. “We have committed to a standard that says verify and verify,” Secretary of State John Kerry said, stressing serious, responsible oversight. While critics call the optics bad and question whether Syria’s willingness is open, experts argue the deal makes the U.S. a winner on substance, even if the Russians look like the primary winner. The framework requires Syria to disclose its arsenal, destroy the weapons by 2014, and face UN Security Council scrutiny for any breach. Though the agreement is not perfect and leaves many dubious questions, it offers a diplomatic path that avoids further brutal conflict. END OF SUMMARY.
