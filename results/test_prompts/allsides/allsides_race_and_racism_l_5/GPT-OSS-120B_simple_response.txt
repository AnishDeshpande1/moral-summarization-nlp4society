SUMMARY: A year after Michael Brown’s killing sparked the Ferguson protests, the Black Lives Matter movement is making measurable moral progress. Americans are increasingly recognizing systemic racism in policing: Gallup finds more people say Black people are unfairly treated, and Pew reports a 20‑year high in those calling racism a “big problem.” Media outlets now track police shootings in dedicated databases, reflecting heightened public interest that began with Ferguson’s dominance on Twitter in 2014. Crucially, prosecutors are more willing to hold officers accountable, charging those involved in the deaths of Walter Scott, Eric Harris, Freddie Gray and, most recently, Samuel DuBose—an unusual stance even from Republican officials. Lawmakers are also responding; President Obama and candidate Hillary Clinton advocate body‑camera mandates and better federal tracking of police killings, and Clinton devoted her first 2016 campaign speech to criminal‑justice reform. Though Black people still die at alarming rates and many Republicans remain skeptical, the shift in public opinion, media focus, and legal action signals that Black Lives Matter is winning its moral battle, even if the slow American political system lags behind. END OF SUMMARY.
