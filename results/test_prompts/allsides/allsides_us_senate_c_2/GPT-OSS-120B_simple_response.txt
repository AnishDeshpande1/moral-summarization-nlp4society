SUMMARY:  
House Democrats have introduced a bill to give permanent protection and a path to citizenship for the “Dreamers” – undocumented immigrants brought to the U.S. as children – framing the issue as a moral imperative to stop punishing innocent children for their parents’ choices. Historically, many Senate Republicans supported the DREAM Act out of a sense of fairness, but over the past two decades the party’s conservative wing has recast the proposal as “amnesty,” eroding bipartisan backing. The article tracks this shift through dwindling GOP co‑sponsors and the 2017 backlash to President Trump’s attempt to end DACA, leaving Dreamers in legal limbo. Senior Republicans like the late Richard Lugar recall the internal conflict between compassion and political risk, while current senators such as Roy Blunt, Lindsey Graham, Tim Scott and Marco Rubio argue that any standalone Dreamer bill must be tied to broader immigration compromises, fearing it will become a “vehicle for multiple amendments.” Consequently, although the Dream and Promise Act is poised to pass the Democratic‑controlled House, its moral promise faces a bleak outlook in the Republican‑led Senate.  

END OF SUMMARY.
