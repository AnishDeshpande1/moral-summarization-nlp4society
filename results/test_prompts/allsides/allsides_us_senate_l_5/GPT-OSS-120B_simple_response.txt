SUMMARY:  
In an election‑year showdown, Democrats pushed a bill they framed as a moral imperative to end women’s pay discrimination, arguing that the nation cannot tolerate a system where women earn only 77 cents to a man’s dollar. They portrayed the measure as a step toward equal rights and a correction of the 1963 Equal Pay Act’s loopholes, insisting that employers must justify pay differences by performance, not gender, and that workers should be free to share salary information.  

Republicans, however, cast the legislation as a partisan stunt that would chiefly benefit trial lawyers at the expense of businesses, warning that it would unleash costly class‑action suits and overburden employers. They dismissed the bill as “politics” and “overreach,” offering only tepid alternatives and suggesting other, less punitive ways to address wage gaps.  

Despite the defeat—52 votes, none from Republicans—Democratic leaders, backed by President Obama and pay‑equity advocates, condemned the opposition as putting politics above American women and families. Sponsor Barbara Mikulski vowed to reintroduce the bill until “equal pay, for equal rights” is achieved.  

END OF SUMMARY.
