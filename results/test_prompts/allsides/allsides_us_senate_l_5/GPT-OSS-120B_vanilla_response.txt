SUMMARY: An election‑year Democratic Senate proposal aimed at strengthening the 1963 Equal Pay Act was defeated, receiving only 52 votes—none from Republicans—short of the 60 needed. The bill would require employers to justify pay differences based on performance, protect salary‑information sharing, and permit women to sue for damages, measures Democrats say would close the gender‑pay gap (women earn 77 cents to a man’s dollar). Republicans criticized the measure as partisan, a “trial‑lawyer bonanza” that would burden employers with costly class‑action lawsuits, and argued it was drafted without their input. Only Sen. Dean Heller voiced opposition on the floor, proposing an alternative deemed inadequate by Democrats. Senate leaders Harry Reid and Barbara Mikulski pledged to reintroduce the legislation, while President Obama condemned the Republican vote as putting politics above women and families. Moderate Republican Olympia Snowe also called the bill an overreach. The debate highlights partisan divisions over how best to address pay discrimination. END OF SUMMARY.
