SUMMARY:
The Trump administration is weighing a new executive order that would block migrants—especially Central Americans seeking asylum—from entering the United States, using the same sweeping authority that powered the 2017 travel ban.  By framing the move as a national‑security measure, the White House seeks to subvert established asylum law and constitutional protections, granting border officials unchecked power to deny refuge to those fleeing persecution.  Critics denounce the plan as a morally repugnant act of cruelty that abandons the vulnerable, exposing them to danger and violating basic human dignity.  Immigration‑rights groups label it a “racism‑driven cruelty” and a “Latin ban,” arguing that it cheats the principles of fairness by discriminating on the basis of nationality and ethnicity.  The proposal also threatens to degrade the sanctity of U.S. humanitarian commitments, while the administration simultaneously threatens to cut aid to Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador and to deploy additional troops to enforce the ban—tactics seen as an authoritarian overreach that weaponizes fear.  Legal experts warn the order will face intense challenges, but the administration appears prepared to push the limits of executive power to satisfy a political agenda ahead of the midterm elections.  
END OF SUMMARY.
