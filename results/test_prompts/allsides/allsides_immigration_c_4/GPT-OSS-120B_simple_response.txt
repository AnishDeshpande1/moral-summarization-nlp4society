SUMMARY:  
The Trump administration is weighing a new executive order that would bar migrants— including asylum seekers—from entering the United States at the southern border, invoking the same sweeping authority used for the 2017 travel ban. The proposal would deny asylum claims on “national security” grounds and give border officials broad discretion, while the White House also plans to deploy additional troops and threatens to cut U.S. aid to Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador. President Trump has framed the Central American “caravan” as a Democratic‑created crisis and has used the issue to rally voters ahead of the November midterms.  

Civil‑rights groups have condemned the plan as “racism‑driven cruelty” and a “huge moral failure,” warning that it would violate the Constitution and international law. The ACLU called the consideration “disgraceful,” and the National Immigration Law Center pledged to fight any ban that imposes a “Latin ban.” Legal scholars note that, despite the policy’s cruelty and lack of sense, courts—especially a Supreme Court that recently upheld the original travel ban—might still uphold it.  

The move signals a dramatic escalation of presidential power over immigration, raising intense legal challenges and a stark moral debate over the United States’ duty to protect refugees.  

END OF SUMMARY.
