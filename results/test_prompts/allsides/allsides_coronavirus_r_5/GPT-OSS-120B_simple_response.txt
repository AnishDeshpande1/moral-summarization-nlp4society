SUMMARY: The author argues that keeping the U.S. economy shut is not just an economic issue but a health‑versus‑health dilemma, claiming that lockdowns cause serious health harms—rising suicide and substance‑abuse crises, missed cancer screenings, halted vaccinations, and increased domestic violence. Citing statistics on mental‑health hotline spikes, delayed cancer diagnoses, projected deaths from drug abuse, and the threat of mass starvation among the poorest, the piece contends that economic inequality itself creates deadly health outcomes. While acknowledging COVID‑19 risks, the author asserts that hospitals now have sufficient beds and testing, and that “flattening the curve” was meant to buy time for resources, not to enforce indefinite closures. They note that many U.S. counties have few or no deaths and that a small fraction of cases accounts for most fatalities, suggesting selective reopening is feasible. The moral thrust is a call for empathy toward working‑class Americans who must earn a living, urging officials to allow them the choice to take calculated risks rather than be judged by elites who can work remotely. The piece concludes that reopening, guided by local data, is a necessary, responsible step to protect both lives and livelihoods.  
END OF SUMMARY.
