SUMMARY:  
A gunman opened fire inside West Freeway Church of Christ in Texas, but former deputy sheriff Jack Wilson, a licensed concealed‑carry volunteer, took seconds to stop the shooter and save the parishioners. Wilson’s actions illustrate how legal protections that allow law‑abiding citizens to carry firearms can protect congregations from violence. The article contrasts this outcome with other recent attacks on Orthodox Jews in New York, where knife‑wielding assailants were only deterred by chairs and tables, and notes that the killer in a New Jersey yeshiva had a gun. Governor Greg Abbott signed a law that makes it legal for licensed carriers to bring guns into churches, a move framed by opponents as “irrational” but defended as essential for self‑defense. The author argues that allowing trained, background‑checked citizens to carry weapons can mitigate damage in shootings, citing Stephen Willeford’s AR‑15 response in Sutherland Springs and Jeanne Assam’s intervention in Colorado Springs. While mass shootings are rare, the piece claims there is no downside to letting congregations decide their own security, warning that draconian limits on guns compel people to sit defenseless and inhibit the right to defend oneself and one’s community.  

END OF SUMMARY.
