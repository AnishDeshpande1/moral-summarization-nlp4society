SUMMARY: A police report revealed that Devin Kelley, the former Air Force serviceman responsible for the deadliest mass shooting in Texas history, had escaped a mental‑hospital in 2012 while awaiting a court‑martial for domestic‑violence offenses. Despite warnings that he was a danger to himself and others and that he had threatened his commanders, his conviction was never entered into the federal criminal database used for background checks. Consequently, Kelley passed two background screenings and purchased the weapons used to kill 26 people (including an unborn child) and wound dozens more at a Sutherland Springs church before taking his own life. The failure of the Air Force to report his domestic‑violence conviction is described by lawmakers as an “appalling” lapse and has spurred bipartisan proposals to block anyone convicted of domestic abuse—whether in civilian or military courts—from buying guns. The tragedy also exposed broader systemic weaknesses in background‑check procedures, prompting investigations by the Pentagon and calls for stricter enforcement of existing gun‑ownership laws. END OF SUMMARY.
