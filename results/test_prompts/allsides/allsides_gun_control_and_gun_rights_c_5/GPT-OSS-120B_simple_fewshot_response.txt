SUMMARY:
The deadliest mass shooting in Texas history was carried out by former Air Force airman Devin Kelley, who escaped a mental‑hospital in 2012 while facing a court‑martial for domestic‑violence offenses that later resulted in a conviction. Police were warned that Kelley was “a danger to himself and others,” yet his conviction was never entered into the National Criminal Information Center database, allowing him to pass background checks and purchase the weapons used in the Sutherland Springs church massacre that killed 26 people and wounded dozens more. Investigations revealed a pattern of violent and disruptive behavior dating back to his school years and continued turmoil in his adult life, including alleged threats to family members. Lawmakers from both parties are now proposing legislation to ensure that any domestic‑violence conviction—civilian or military—is automatically blocked from gun purchases, aiming to close the glaring loophole that enabled Kelley’s access to firearms. The tragedy underscores the moral imperative of rigorous, accountable background‑check systems to protect the public from preventable violence.

END OF SUMMARY.
