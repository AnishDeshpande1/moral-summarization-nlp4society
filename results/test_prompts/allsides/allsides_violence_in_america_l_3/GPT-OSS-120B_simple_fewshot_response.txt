SUMMARY:
Chicago endured one of its bloodiest years on record, with 762 homicides in 2016—the highest in two decades and more than New York and Los Angeles combined—and a surge of 1,100 shooting incidents over 2015. The spike, the largest in 60 years, reflects a flood of illegal firearms (8,300 recovered, a 20 % rise) and a broken deterrent: suspects perceive the criminal‑justice system as a “joke,” often spending more time awaiting trial for minor offenses than gun offenders. The violence is concentrated in five poor, predominantly Black districts on the South and West sides, where most victims have known gang ties. Police Superintendent Eddie Johnson blames lax Illinois gun laws, heightened anger toward police after the Laquan McDonald video, and reduced police activity due to fear of scrutiny. City leaders have responded with promises of 1,000 new officers, expanded street cameras, and gun‑shot detection technology, but critics warn that over‑cautious policing and new paperwork requirements only embolden criminals. The moral imperative is clear: without decisive reforms to gun control, policing practices, and community trust, Chicago’s deadly spiral will continue.  
END OF SUMMARY.
