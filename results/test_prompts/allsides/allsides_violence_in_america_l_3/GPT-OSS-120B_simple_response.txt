SUMMARY:  
Chicago endured one of its bloodiest years, recording 762 homicides in 2016—the highest in two decades and surpassing New York and Los Angeles combined. Shooting incidents rose by 1,100 from 2015, and illegal gun recoveries jumped 20% to 8,300, underscoring a moral crisis of gun proliferation and community neglect. Superintendent Eddie Johnson blamed a loss of public trust after the Laquan McDonald video, arguing that anger toward police and a perception of a “joke” criminal‑justice system have emboldened gangs, especially in the impoverished, majority‑Black South and West Side districts where most victims were already marked by prior arrests. Efforts to restore safety—adding 1,000 officers, installing street cameras, and expanding gun‑shot detectors—face the paradox of police hesitancy after high‑profile misconduct scrutiny and new paperwork‑heavy stop laws. Critics warn that reduced proactive policing lets gangs settle scores unchecked, while the community bears the brunt of a system that appears indifferent to their lives. The data reveal a stark moral failing: a city where illegal guns flow freely, the justice system is viewed as ineffective, and the most vulnerable neighborhoods suffer the greatest loss of life.  

END OF SUMMARY.
