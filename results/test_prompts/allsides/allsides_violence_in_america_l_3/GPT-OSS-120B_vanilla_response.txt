SUMMARY: Chicago experienced one of its most violent years in 2016, recording 762 homicides—the highest in 20 years and surpassing the combined totals of New York and Los Angeles. Shooting incidents rose by 1,100 from 2015, reaching 3,550 incidents citywide, with most violence concentrated in five South and West Side police districts that are poor, predominantly Black, and gang‑afflicted. Homicides jumped from 485 in 2015 to 762, the largest 60‑year increase, while illegal guns recovered rose 20% to 8,300. Police Superintendent Eddie Johnson linked the surge to angry sentiment toward police after the 2015 release of the Laquan McDonald shooting video, perceived leniency in the criminal‑justice system, and lax Illinois gun laws. He noted fewer arrests and cautious policing due to new state reporting requirements and fear of viral incidents. The city responded by adding 1,000 officers and planning more street cameras and gunshot‑detection systems. Critics argue that police hesitation and reduced minor drug arrests have emboldened gangs. END OF SUMMARY.
