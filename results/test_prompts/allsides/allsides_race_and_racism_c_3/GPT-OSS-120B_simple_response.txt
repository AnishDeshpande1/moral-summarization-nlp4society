SUMMARY:  
The article frames George Floyd’s killing as a moral watershed in America’s long history of racial violence. It notes how earlier victims—Emmett Till, Rodney King, Eric Garner, Michael Brown—became symbols of their eras, but Floyd’s death became a catalyst because it was captured in graphic, widely shared video of a police knee on his neck for minutes, exposing the brutality in unmistakable detail. The piece argues that this incident did not occur in isolation; it was the tipping point of accumulated anger over cases like Ahmaud Arbery, Breonna Taylor, and Tony McDade, compounded by pandemic‑driven health and economic inequities that disproportionately harmed Black communities. The moral narrative stresses that systemic racism and police violence have reached a combustible level, igniting nationwide protests and a movement likely to define the decade. Ongoing legal actions against the officers involved and the broader call for justice underscore the ethical urgency of confronting entrenched injustice.  

END OF SUMMARY.
