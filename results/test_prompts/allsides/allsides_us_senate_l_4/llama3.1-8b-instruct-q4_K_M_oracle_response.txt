SUMMARY:

Sen. Al Franken's resignation speech was an opportunity for him to show contrition, but he squandered it by not apologizing or taking responsibility for his actions. Instead, he offered advice on running for office and seemed to take back his earlier apologies, leaving many to wonder if he was insincere from the start. By denying the allegations against him, Franken encouraged voters to doubt and shame his accusers, providing ammunition for those who believe their accounts are part of a right-wing plot. This behavior is in stark contrast to the first step of a helpful public apology: acknowledging the harm done. Franken's failure to do so will likely make it harder to fight the injustices faced by women who have been harassed and abused.

Franken had a choice to leave the Senate in a way that helped the cause he claims to care about - protecting women from men's abuses of power - but instead chose to preserve his own reputation at their expense. By sacrificing his accusers, Franken made it harder for them to speak out against harassment and misconduct, and reinforced the notion that women who report these incidents are often shamed and disbelieved.

END OF SUMMARY.
