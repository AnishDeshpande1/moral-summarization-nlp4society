SUMMARY:  
The author argues that Al Franken squandered a moral opportunity when he resigned from the Senate after multiple women accused him of sexual harassment. Instead of offering a sincere apology that acknowledges harm, admits responsibility, expresses remorse, promises behavioral change, and outlines restitution—as sociologist David Karp’s five‑point checklist prescribes—Franken focused on his personal struggles, gave campaign advice, and issued vague denials that could reinforce victim‑blaming narratives. The piece contends that his retreat from earlier apologies was either a cynical effort to protect his reputation or a half‑hearted concession, but in either case he failed to give the survivors the clear acknowledgment and commitment to change they deserve. By prioritizing his own legacy over the broader fight against men’s abuse of power, Franken not only undermined the momentum of the #MeToo conversation but also bolstered forces that dismiss or shame accusers, making it harder to hold powerful men accountable. The author frames this as a moral failing: Franken chose personal preservation at the expense of justice for his accusers.  
END OF SUMMARY.
