SUMMARY:  
Seattle officials finally moved to dismantle the Capitol Hill Occupied Protest (CHOP) after the city’s police chief condemned the zone as “lawless and brutal.” Wearing helmets and protective gear, officers entered the “autonomous zone,” arrested 31 people for failing to disperse and for alleged assaults, and re‑established control around the East Precinct and Cal Anderson Park. Mayor Jenny Durkan labeled the gathering an “unlawful assembly,” while U.S. Attorney General William Barr praised the police for distinguishing peaceful protest from violent crime and “restoring the rule of law.”  

The article frames the crackdown as a moral correction: the CHOP, once a peaceful response to George Floyd’s murder, had become a haven for shootings, robberies and assaults, according to Police Chief Carmen Best. Former protester Harry “Rick” Hearns, who guarded the zone, echoed this view, blaming “outsiders” for the violence.  

Conservative leaders used the Seattle episode to argue that nationwide protests were not as peaceful as portrayed, while local business owners and attorneys pushed for legal barriers against future “lawless autonomous zones.” Yet activists like Lencho Williams vowed the movement would regroup, insisting that “Black lives matter now and forever.”  

The piece moralizes the tension between the right to protest and the community’s duty to protect safety and order.  
END OF SUMMARY.
