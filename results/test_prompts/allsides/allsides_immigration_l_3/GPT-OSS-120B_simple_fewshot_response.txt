SUMMARY:
Homeland Security Secretary Kirstjen Nielsen told Fox News that, for now, U.S. troops sent to the southern border will not be ordered to shoot migrants, but will be used to apprehend them as President Donald Trump pushes a hard‑line plan to close the border amid a Central American caravan. The administration is preparing to deploy at least 800 active‑duty soldiers—an unusual domestic use of the military—while considering emergency powers that could bar asylum claims altogether, a move likely to be challenged in court. Trump has framed the caravan as a “national emergency” and a threat to the nation, using fear‑mongering rhetoric to energize his base ahead of the November midterms and to portray Democrats as “pro‑illegal immigration.” Migrants, many fleeing violence and poverty in Honduras, are pleading for humanitarian treatment, emphasizing that they seek a better future for their children. The policy shift underscores a moral clash: the government’s militarized, deterrent stance versus the migrants’ right to safety and asylum under U.S. and international law. The deployment highlights how political calculations are driving harsh immigration enforcement at the expense of humanitarian principles. 
END OF SUMMARY.
