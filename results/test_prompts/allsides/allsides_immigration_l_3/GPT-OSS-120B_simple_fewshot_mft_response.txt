SUMMARY:
Homeland Security Secretary Kirstjen Nielsen told Fox News that, for now, U.S. troops “have no intention” to shoot migrants trying to cross from Mexico, but they will be “apprehended” and may use force to protect themselves. The Trump administration is preparing to send at least 800 active‑duty soldiers to the southern border—an unprecedented domestic deployment intended to bolster the National Guard and pressure a caravan of Central‑American families. While the president frames the migrants as a “national emergency” and a threat to American security, officials are also considering an emergency order that would deny asylum applications outright, a move likely to face legal challenges. This strategy leverages federal authority to subvert established legal protections for asylum seekers, raising serious concerns about the fairness of denying due process and the potential harm inflicted on vulnerable families fleeing violence and poverty. Critics note that the aggressive militarization and rhetoric portray migrants as criminals, undermining humanitarian obligations and exploiting fear for electoral advantage, thereby violating principles of fairness and care. The article underscores the tension between the administration’s assertion of authority and the moral costs imposed on people seeking safety.

END OF SUMMARY.
