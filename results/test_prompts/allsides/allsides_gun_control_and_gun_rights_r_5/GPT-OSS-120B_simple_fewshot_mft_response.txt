SUMMARY:
Mayor Michael Bloomberg has spent millions pushing gun‑ban legislation while relying on stop‑and‑frisk—an authority‑driven policing tactic he claims has removed 8,000 illegal guns and halved murders. After a federal judge declared the practice unconstitutional and racially discriminatory, the New York City Council overrode Bloomberg’s veto to create an NYPD inspector general and expand civil suits for profiling. Bloomberg blames the decision on the NRA and the ACLU, arguing that their “ideology” threatens public safety, and accuses them of subverting legitimate authority. Conservatives, including Conservative Party chair Mike Long, defend stop‑and‑frisk as essential care for communities of color, warning that abandoning it will make the city as unsafe as Chicago. The article frames Bloomberg’s appeal as a clash between authority (law‑enforcement power) and subversive judicial rulings, emphasizes the care argument that the tactic protects lives, and portrays the judge’s ruling as unfair cheating of citizens who benefit from reduced crime. It calls for the appeals court to restore the authority‑based, safety‑oriented approach and to reject what it depicts as an unjust, harmful judgment.  
END OF SUMMARY.
