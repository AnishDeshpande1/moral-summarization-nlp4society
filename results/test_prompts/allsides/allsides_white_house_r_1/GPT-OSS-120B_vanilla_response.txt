SUMMARY: President Donald Trump has reacted angrily to multiple leaks of private and classified information that have damaged his image and that of his White House. He ordered FBI and White House counsel investigations into the disclosures, which include top‑secret data, alleged misconduct in a Moscow hotel, partial transcripts of former aide Michael Flynn’s calls with the Russian ambassador, and an intelligence report contradicting his travel‑ban claims. While leaking classified material is a felony, the article notes that leaks can also stem from over‑concealment of intelligence that officials are legally obligated to share with the president, which is also illegal. Additionally, internal White House gossip was leaked, prompting a seizure of staff cellphones—a move questioned under the Fourth Amendment, which protects government employees from warrantless searches. The media, protected by the First Amendment, may publish any material of public interest, including leaks about the president, without facing criminal or civil liability. The piece underscores the tension between national‑security secrecy, presidential authority, and press freedom, citing historical support for a free press. END OF SUMMARY.
