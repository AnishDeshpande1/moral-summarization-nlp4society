SUMMARY:

President Trump has ordered an investigation into leaks of classified information, including allegations about his behavior in a Moscow hotel room and confidential communications between former national security adviser Michael Flynn and the Russian ambassador. The leaks have caused presidential anger, but also raise questions about the duty to reveal intelligence data to superiors and ultimately to the president. Selectively concealing or revealing this type of intelligence can be a felony.

The White House has also been plagued by leaks of Oval Office gossip, leading to the temporary seizure of government-issued cellphones held by staffers. However, these actions may not be criminal, as they are protected by the Fourth Amendment. The media is free to publish leaked data, including top-secret information, as it is considered a material interest to the public.

The First Amendment protects the publication of leaked materials that reflect on the president as a person or government official. Trump's predecessors have recognized the importance of accountability and transparency in government, with Thomas Jefferson stating that he would prefer newspapers without a government to a government without newspapers.
