SUMMARY:
U.S. Treasury Secretary Steven Mnuchin missed the House Ways and Means Committee’s final 5 p.m. deadline to turn over President Donald Trump’s six years of personal and business tax returns, heightening a looming constitutional clash over congressional oversight. Chairman Richard Neal warned he will subpoena the records and may hold Mnuchin or IRS Commissioner Charles Rettig in contempt, a step that could force the dispute to the Supreme Court. Democrats argue the request is a lawful effort to expose possible conflicts of interest and uphold the rule of law, condemning the administration’s repeated refusals as “a contempt for rule of law … without peer” and “corrupt.” Republicans label the push a partisan “fishing expedition.” The Treasury department says it is still consulting the Justice Department because the request is “unprecedented” and must be consistent with the law. The White House maintains the president is not inclined to release the returns while an audit is alleged, despite former lawyer Michael Cohen’s claim the audit is a pretext. Legal experts warn that defying a subpoena could bring penalties or even jail time, underscoring the moral stakes of transparency versus executive resistance.

END OF SUMMARY.
