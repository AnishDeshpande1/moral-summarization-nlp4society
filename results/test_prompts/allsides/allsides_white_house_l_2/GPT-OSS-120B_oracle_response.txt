SUMMARY:
Leaks about former National Security Advisor Michael Flynn’s December call with Russian Ambassador Sergei Kislyak sparked the first Trump‑administration shake‑up and underscored that Washington’s rules of engagement have **not** changed. President Trump vowed to **catch** the “low‑life leakers,” but history shows they rarely are **caught**. Chief of Staff Reince Priebus worries that trying to **stop** leaks only leads to blame‑shifting, **abandonment**, low morale and eventual **breakin**s, echoing the Watergate **quest** that birthed the original **Plumbers**. After Flynn’s resignation the White House could have **regained** focus on **creating** a new government, yet instead it chose to **follow** the Nixon playbook and **treat** leakers as the problem. Trump’s February 16 press conference framed leaks as **criminal acts**, invoking the new Attorney General and **approved** Justice Department action, even as past administrations proved most leaks are not prosecutable. A week later, after Priebus’s own leak to the FBI, Trump **looked** at the FBI as **permeated** by leakers, **given** “devastating” classified info to the media, and ordered “FIND NOW” in all caps. Secretary of State Rex Tillerson’s memo warning that leaks **chill** candid advice was itself **leaked**, highlighting the futility of the current **quest** to **stop** the leak epidemic.  
END OF SUMMARY.
