SUMMARY:

The U.S. Court of Appeals has struck down same-sex-marriage bans in Wisconsin and Indiana, citing "no reasonable basis" for the prohibitions. Judge Richard Posner's unanimous opinion emphasized that the bans deny the welfare of American children, particularly those adopted by homosexual couples who would be better off emotionally and economically if their parents were married. The states' argument that straight couples must be pressured to marry to prevent accidental pregnancies is "full of holes" and ignores the fact that many children are adopted by gay couples. Posner scorned the states' position, saying it rewards heterosexuals for producing unwanted children while denying gay couples the right to marry. He also rejected the argument that the ban helps preserve a centuries-old tradition of marriage, citing the Supreme Court's 1967 decision striking down interracial marriage bans, which held that tradition cannot be a lawful ground for discrimination. The ruling is a striking blow to discrimination, and the case will likely be appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court. END OF SUMMARY.
