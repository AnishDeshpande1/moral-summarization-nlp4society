SUMMARY:

The U.S. Court of Appeals for the Midwest has struck down gay-marriage bans in Wisconsin and Indiana, joining two other federal appeals courts in doing so. Judge Richard Posner wrote that the states had given no reasonable basis for forbidding same-sex marriage, calling their argument "so full of holes that it cannot be taken seriously." He argued that the welfare of American children is at stake, as many are abandoned to single parents or foster care when biological parents aren't married. However, Posner noted that these children would be better off with married adoptive parents, which is often the case for those raised by homosexual couples.

Posner rejected the states' argument that marriage between a man and a woman is necessary to protect children, calling it "belied" by empirical data on out-of-wedlock births. He also struck down the states' claim of tradition as a lawful ground for discrimination, pointing out that this argument runs counter to the Supreme Court's decision striking down interracial marriage bans in 1967.

The court's decision is being appealed to the U.S. Supreme Court, which is expected to take on the issue before its current term ends in June.

END OF SUMMARY.
