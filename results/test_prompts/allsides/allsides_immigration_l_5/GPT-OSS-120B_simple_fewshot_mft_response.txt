SUMMARY:
A new report shows that the Priority Enforcement Program (PEP), touted by ICE as a “common‑sense” effort to detain only the most dangerous “criminal aliens,” is largely misusing federal authority. Half of the immigration “holds” placed on local jails involve people who have never been convicted—many arrested for minor traffic violations or whose charges were dropped—yet they are still swept into federal detention and deportation proceedings. Only about 25 % of those held have committed serious felonies such as murder or sexual assault; the most common offenses are drunk driving and low‑level assaults. Critics argue this subverts the promise of targeting genuine threats, turning the program into a tool that harms vulnerable immigrants and their communities, discouraging victims from reporting crimes for fear of detention. The practice also raises serious fairness concerns, as it punishes individuals without proven guilt and appears to be a rebranded version of the older Secure Communities scheme. ICE officials defend the policy, claiming it focuses resources on true security risks, but advocates and affected residents contend the program betrays both justice and public safety.  

END OF SUMMARY.
