SUMMARY:
A new report reveals that the Priority Enforcement Program (PEP), touted by ICE as a “common‑sense” effort to deport only the “worst of the worst,” is largely missing its mark. An analysis of ICE requests to local jails shows that half of the so‑called “holds” were placed on immigrants who had only been arrested—not convicted—often for minor traffic violations or dropped charges. Only about 25 % faced serious offenses such as murder or sexual assault; the most common “convictions” were DUI, simple assaults and traffic infractions. Despite ICE’s claim that PEP narrows the scope of the earlier Secure Communities program, data indicate the agency now targets people with no criminal record at a slightly higher rate than before. Critics argue the policy harms community trust, deterring crime victims from reporting crimes for fear of detention, and that the new detainer forms amount to a re‑branding of an already‑flawed system. Legal advocates call the practice a “joke” that undermines public safety and immigrant rights.

END OF SUMMARY.
