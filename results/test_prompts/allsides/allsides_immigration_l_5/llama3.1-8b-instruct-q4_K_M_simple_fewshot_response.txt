SUMMARY:

A new report reveals that many immigrants being deported under the Priority Enforcement Program (PEP) have past arrests but no convictions, highlighting the program's failure to prioritize the deportation of "the worst of the worst". Analysis of federal requests for local jails to hold immigrants in custody found that half of the so-called "holds" were placed on people with no criminal conviction. The most common conviction was for drunk driving or simple traffic offenses. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) claims it has narrowed the criteria used under a previous program, but data shows ICE is now targeting people with no record at a slightly higher rate than before. This has led to criticism that the PEP program is simply a "name change" from its predecessor, which contributed to a record number of deportations under Barack Obama.

The report highlights the humanitarian crisis caused by US and Mexico's mass deportations, with many immigrants being caught in the middle. ICE claims it is focusing on convicted criminals and individuals who threaten public safety and national security, but critics argue that the agency's policies are not keeping anyone secure.
