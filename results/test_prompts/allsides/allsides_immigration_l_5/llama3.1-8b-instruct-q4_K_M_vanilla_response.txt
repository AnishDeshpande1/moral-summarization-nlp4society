SUMMARY:

A report by the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse found that half of the "holds" placed on immigrants suspected of violating US immigration law under the Priority Enforcement Program (PEP) had no criminal conviction, but rather past arrests or minor infractions such as traffic violations. Only 25% of the holds were for serious offenses like murder or sexual assault. The report suggests that ICE's criteria has not changed significantly from its predecessor Secure Communities, which led to a record number of deportations under Barack Obama.

ICE claims it has narrowed its focus to "criminal aliens," but data shows it is still targeting people with no criminal record at a higher rate than before. Immigration advocates have sued for more details on how PEP differs from Secure Communities, suggesting that the new program may be just a name change.

The report highlights concerns about community policing and public safety, as some immigrants are hesitant to report crimes due to fear of being detained by ICE. The issue has sparked debate over sanctuary cities and immigration policy in the US.

END OF SUMMARY.
