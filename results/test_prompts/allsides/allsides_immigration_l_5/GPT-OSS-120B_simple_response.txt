SUMMARY:  
A new report reveals that the Priority Enforcement Program (PEP), touted by ICE as a “common‑sense” effort to focus on the “worst of the worst” criminal aliens, is largely missing its moral target. Half of the immigration “holds” placed on local jails involve people who have only been arrested—not convicted—often for minor traffic violations or whose charges were dropped. Only 25 % of those detained have committed serious crimes such as murder or sexual assault; the most common offenses are drunk driving, simple assaults and traffic infractions. Despite ICE’s claim that PEP narrows the criteria of its predecessor, Secure Communities, the data show the agency is now detaining more non‑convicted individuals than before. Critics argue the policy undermines community safety: undocumented victims may avoid reporting crimes for fear of detention, and many jurisdictions have resisted ICE’s requests. Legal advocates suspect PEP is merely a rebranding of an aggressive deportation strategy, with new detainer forms that target recent illegal entrants without criminal records, turning the program into “a joke.” The moral implication is clear: a policy presented as protecting public safety is, in practice, punishing the innocent and eroding trust between immigrant communities and law enforcement.  
END OF SUMMARY.
