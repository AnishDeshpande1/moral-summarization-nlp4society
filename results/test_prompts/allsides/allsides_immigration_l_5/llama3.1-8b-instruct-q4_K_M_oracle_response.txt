SUMMARY:

The Priority Enforcement Program, intended to prioritize the deportation of immigrants who officials call “the worst of the worst,” is missing its target. An analysis found that half of the so-called “holds” placed on people suspected of violating US immigration law had past arrests but no convictions. Many were picked up for minor offenses such as traffic violations or had charges dropped, yet their arrest triggered a process leading to federal immigration detention and deportation proceedings. Only one quarter had committed serious offenses like murder or assault. ICE claims it has narrowed the criteria under PEP, but data shows it now targets people with no criminal record at a higher rate than before. Immigration advocates question whether PEP is just a name change from its predecessor, Secure Communities, which fueled a humanitarian crisis by deporting record numbers of immigrants.

Critics argue that implementing such policies threatens public safety and national security, while also deterring crime victims from reporting crimes for fear of being detained themselves. The agency says it tries to consider community policing needs, but the program's effectiveness is unclear. END OF SUMMARY.
