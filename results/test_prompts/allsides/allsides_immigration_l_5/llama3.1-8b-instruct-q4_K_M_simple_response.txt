SUMMARY:

A new report reveals that the Priority Enforcement Program (PEP), intended to prioritize the deportation of immigrants who officials call "the worst of the worst", is failing in its mission. Analysis of requests for local jails to hold immigrants suspected of violating US immigration law found that half of the so-called "holds" were placed on people with past arrests but no convictions. The most common conviction was for drunk driving, followed by miscellaneous assaults and simple traffic offenses. This suggests that ICE is targeting individuals who are not necessarily a threat to public safety or national security.

Immigration advocates have criticized the program, noting that it may be more about detaining immigrants than prioritizing those who pose a real danger. The report also highlights concerns about the impact of mass deportations on humanitarian crises along the US-Mexico border. ICE claims to have narrowed its criteria since 2008's Secure Communities program, but critics argue that PEP is just a name change and continues to target individuals with no criminal record.

END OF SUMMARY.
