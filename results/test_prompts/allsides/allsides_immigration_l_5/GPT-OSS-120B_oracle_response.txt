SUMMARY: A new report finds that many of the “holds” in ICE’s Priority Enforcement Program (PEP) are missing their intended target. The analysis shows that half of the “holds” were placed on immigrants who had only been arrested, not convicted, for minor violations such as traffic offenses or drunk driving. Only one‑quarter had committed serious offenses like murder or sexual assault, and the most common conviction was for drunk driving, followed by miscellaneous assaults. Despite ICE’s claim that PEP “focuses enforcement resources on convicted criminals and individuals who threaten public safety and national security,” the data reveal that the program continues to target people with no conviction at a slightly higher rate than before. Critics argue the policy is “fueling” a humanitarian crisis, discouraging victims of crime from reporting incidents for fear of detention. Legal advocates suspect the PEP is merely a name change that still allows ICE to request custody of non‑convicted, low‑risk immigrants, turning the enforcement effort into a “joke.” END OF SUMMARY.
