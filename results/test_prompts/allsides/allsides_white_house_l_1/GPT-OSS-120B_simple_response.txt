SUMMARY:  
Retired Marine Gen. John Kelly, who served as President Trump’s chief of staff, announced his departure at year‑end, leaving the White House as chaotic as when he arrived. While his personal discipline contrasted with Trump’s impulsiveness, Kelly embodied the core “Trumpism” ideology: casual cruelty, tolerance for bigotry, and a willingness to defend reprehensible policies. He dismissed criticism from Black Congresswoman Frederica Wilson, praised Confederate leader Robert E. Lee shortly after Charlottesville’s white‑nationalist violence, and defended accused abuser Rob Porter as “a man of true integrity.” Most starkly, Kelly helped design and justify the “zero‑tolerance” family‑separation policy, shrugging off its cruelty. The author argues that Kelly’s veneer of moderation concealed an alignment with Trump’s ethnonationalist worldview, and that a more disciplined Trump could have been even more effective at enacting such hateful policies. The lesson is that the administration’s moral character derives from the president, not from a supposedly “adult in the room” chief of staff.  

END OF SUMMARY.
