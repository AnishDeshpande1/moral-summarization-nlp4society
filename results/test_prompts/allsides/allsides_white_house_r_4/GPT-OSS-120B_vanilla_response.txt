SUMMARY: The article argues that the “Obama Doctrine” lacks a coherent principle, describing it instead as a pattern of selective, short‑term interventions driven by image rather than substance. It notes Obama’s own rhetoric of multilateralism and limited power, but points to contradictory actions: aggressive moves in Libya, limited engagement in Syria and Ukraine, and minimal response to crises in Egypt, Iran, Nigeria, and Iraq. The author claims Obama’s foreign policy is characterized by emotional posturing, brief shows of force, and a reluctance to commit to sustained action, leading to ineffective outcomes and high civilian casualties. The piece concludes that Obama’s focus on popularity and appearances, rather than lasting leadership, makes his doctrine dangerous and ultimately unpopular. END OF SUMMARY.
