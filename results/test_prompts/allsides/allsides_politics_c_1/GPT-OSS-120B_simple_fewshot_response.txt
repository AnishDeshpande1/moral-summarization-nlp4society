SUMMARY:
Former national security adviser John Bolton’s forthcoming memoir accuses President Trump of putting personal and political gain above the nation’s interests, citing alleged requests for election help from China, support for Chinese prison camps, threats to journalists and a detailed account of the Ukraine affair that fueled the impeachment. Trump dismissed the book as “lies” and tried to block its release on national‑security grounds, but a federal judge allowed it to be published. Bolton, who left the White House in 2019 after clashes with Trump, was not subpoenaed by House Democrats or Senate Republicans during the impeachment inquiry, a decision now being revisited as the book’s explosive claims emerge. Critics on the left condemn Bolton for profiting from his insider knowledge, while Trump’s allies attack the memoir’s credibility. Some argue the revelations are too late to affect the impeachment outcome or the current health and racial‑justice crises, yet others contend the allegations could still sway voters and damage Republican senators who refused his testimony. The book hits shelves on June 23, prompting renewed debate over accountability, political self‑interest, and the moral responsibility of former officials to disclose wrongdoing.

END OF SUMMARY.
