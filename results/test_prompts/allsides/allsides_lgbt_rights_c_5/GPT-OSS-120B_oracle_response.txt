SUMMARY:
The Obama administration faces a pivotal, symbolic decision on gay marriage as it prepares to file briefs in two Supreme Court cases, including California’s Proposition 8. The initiative, passed in 2008, banned same‑sex couples from marrying and was later struck down as unconstitutional discrimination. The government must decide whether to argue that the state’s brief window of legal marriage creates a right that cannot be revoked, that denying marriage to samesex couples is discriminatory, or simply challenge the standing of the initiative’s sponsors. President Obama’s rhetoric has shifted from urging states to “legalize” marriage locally to declaring that the nation’s journey is incomplete until gay brothers and sisters are treated equally under the law, echoing the bold, full‑throated language of the 1967 interracial‑marriage ruling. The administration could make a bold claim that the constitutional right to marry extends to samesex couples, or adopt narrower positions about discrimination or standing. Both advocates of marriage equality and traditional heterosexual marriage view the brief as a moral statement, even if its legal impact on the justices may be limited. The choice will signal how the government views the right to marry and the fight against discrimination.  
END OF SUMMARY.
