SUMMARY:
Immigrants are the common denominator of America’s “greatness,” a truth that endures despite Donald Trump’s anti‑immigrant rhetoric. They have sustained the nation’s growth, founding iconic companies such as Budweiser and McDonald’s, and accounting for roughly 40 % of Fortune‑500 origins. Immigrant‑founded firms generate $857 billion in sales and employ nearly 4 million workers. Critics who claim immigrants abuse social programs overlook evidence that undocumented newcomers actually pay $13.8 billion more into Medicare than they receive, while native‑born citizens create a deficit. Correlating immigration with wrongdoing is likewise misguided: a 2008 Public Policy Institute of California study found native‑born males are ten times more likely to be incarcerated than foreign‑born men. The real danger lies in a citizenship process that continues to be bureaucratic and convoluted, incentivizing unlawful, desperate alternatives and fostering a rise in human smuggling. Rounding up 11 million undocumented immigrants is impractical; building a wall would be an irresponsible, taxpayer‑draining project that only prolongs risks. Most immigrants, documented or not, simply overstayed visas in pursuit of freedom and to escape hardship. Native‑born citizens and lawmakers should welcome them rather than disparage them. Immigrants made America great before; they will make it great again.  
END OF SUMMARY.
