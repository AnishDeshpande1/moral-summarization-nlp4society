SUMMARY:
The Senate is set to debate the Born‑Alive Abortion Survivors Protection Act, a bill that would force doctors to give newborns who survive an abortion the same medical care any other infant receives. Proponents argue the measure exposes the “unlimited‑abortion” logic of Democrats, who they say deny the existence of “born‑alive” infants and claim existing laws already protect them. Critics point to cases like Kermit Gosnell and anecdotal evidence that infants do survive late‑term abortions, highlighting the lack of a federal requirement for life‑saving care. While only 33 states have born‑alive statutes, the bill does not restrict abortion or women’s health choices; it simply mandates care for a living child. Opponents label the legislation “anti‑abortion,” suggesting it interferes with doctor‑patient decisions, but supporters contend that this label reveals a willingness to let a newborn die simply because it was once targeted for termination. If any pro‑choice senator backs the bill, they risk showing the inconsistency of granting rights only after birth while denying them in utero. The bill is expected to face Democratic opposition and likely defeat, but its introduction aims to spotlight what supporters view as the dehumanizing core of abortion ideology.  
END OF SUMMARY.
