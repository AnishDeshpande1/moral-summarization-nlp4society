SUMMARY:
A federal judge in New York halted a Trump administration rule that would have let a broad swath of health‑care workers refuse to provide abortions and other services on moral or religious grounds. Judge Paul Engelmayer found the Health and Human Services Department exceeded its authority, relied on a false claim of rising conscience‑based complaints, and created a “solution in search of a problem.” The rule would have expanded conscientious‑objector protections to include anyone who schedules, refers, or even operates an elevator in a medical facility, threatening emergency care, increasing staffing costs, and endangering vulnerable patients—including women, LGBTQ people, and those with HIV/AIDS. Plaintiffs—19 states, D.C., local governments and health groups—argued the policy was discriminatory and would obstruct access to care. Planned Parenthood and the American Psychological Association praised the decision as a block against illegal discrimination, while some Republicans dismissed it as “absurd.” The judge emphasized that any changes to accommodate religious objections belong in Congress, not an overreaching agency rule.

END OF SUMMARY.
