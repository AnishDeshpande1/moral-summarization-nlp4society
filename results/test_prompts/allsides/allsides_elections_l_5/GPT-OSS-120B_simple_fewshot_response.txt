SUMMARY:
The Obama campaign portrayed the Republican National Convention in Tampa as a total failure, claiming the GOP “didn’t lay a glove on their man.” In reality, the convention delivered a more nuanced and dangerous tactic: Republicans adopted the theme of “disappointment,” positioning themselves as former 2008 Obama supporters lamenting his unfulfilled promises. By framing Obama’s record as a let‑down rather than attacking him with overt attacks, they aimed to erode his base’s enthusiasm and shift momentum subtly. Speakers such as Mitt Romney and Marco Rubio warned that Obama’s promises to slow sea‑level rise and heal the planet had faded, while Rubio framed the problem as Obama being a “bad president” rather than a bad person. Even traditionally partisan moments—like Paul Ryan’s tribute to his mother and Rubio’s immigrant‑success story—mirrored Democratic rhetoric. This strategic, low‑key messaging could prove more damaging than the usual “kitchen‑sink” attacks, quietly softening voter sentiment as the campaign heads into its final months. The moral framing suggests that the GOP’s clever reframing of disappointment poses a greater threat to Obama than overt criticism.

END OF SUMMARY.
