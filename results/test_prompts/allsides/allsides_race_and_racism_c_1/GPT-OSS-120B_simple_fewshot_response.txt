SUMMARY:
The article recounts the brutal “Bloody Sunday” clash on March 7, 1965, when 25‑year‑old John Lewis led a peaceful voting‑rights march across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma. Despite a hostile crowd of white onlookers and a line of Alabama state troopers armed with gas canisters, nightsticks and mounted sheriffs, Lewis and fellow activists pressed on toward Governor George Wallace’s segregationist stronghold. When Major John Cloud ordered the march illegal and gave a two‑minute ultimatum, the troopers surged forward, beating and clubbing demonstrators. Lewis was struck in the head, fracturing his skull, while Amelia Boynton Robinson was tear‑gassed and knocked unconscious. The savage attack shocked the nation and helped spur the passage of the federal Voting Rights Act. Lewis later joined Martin Luther King Jr. on the historic 52‑mile march to Montgomery and spent a lifetime championing civil rights, urging others to “speak up, get in good trouble, and help redeem the soul of America.” His death in 2020 marks the loss of a moral beacon who endured violence to advance justice.  
END OF SUMMARY.
