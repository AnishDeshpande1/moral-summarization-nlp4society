SUMMARY:

On March 7, 1965, John Lewis led hundreds of marchers across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama, to demand voting rights for African Americans. As they approached a line of state troopers, tensions escalated, and violence broke out. Troopers armed with gas canisters and nightsticks attacked the peaceful protesters, leaving many injured, including John Lewis, who sustained a fractured skull.

The brutal suppression of the march, known as Bloody Sunday, shocked the nation but ultimately galvanized support for the civil rights movement. The Voting Rights Act was passed in response to the event, and John Lewis became a key figure in American politics, carrying the struggle against racial discrimination from the 1960s into Congress.

In his later years, Lewis continued to advocate for justice and equality, urging people to "get in good trouble" and speak out against injustice. His legacy serves as a powerful reminder of the importance of standing up against oppression and fighting for human rights.

END OF SUMMARY.
