SUMMARY:

John Lewis, a civil rights icon, died at 80, leaving behind a legacy of fighting against racial discrimination. In 1965, Lewis, then 25, led a voting rights march in Selma, Alabama, where he was brutally beaten by state troopers. Despite the violence, Lewis continued to fight for freedom and equality, becoming a hero of the US civil rights movement. The confrontation led to the passage of the federal Voting Rights Act. Lewis's courage and perseverance inspired a nation, and his message of "getting in good trouble" continues to resonate today. He outlived other key players in the movement, including Gov. George Wallace, who had vowed "segregation forever." Lewis's legacy serves as a reminder of the importance of standing up against injustice and fighting for the soul of America.

END OF SUMMARY.
