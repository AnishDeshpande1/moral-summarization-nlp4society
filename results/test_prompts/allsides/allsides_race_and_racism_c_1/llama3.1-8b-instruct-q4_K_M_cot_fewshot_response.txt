SUMMARY:
John Lewis, a hero of the U.S. civil rights movement, died at age 80 after leading voting rights demonstrations in Selma, Alabama, in 1965. On March 7, 1965, Lewis and hundreds of marchers were met with state-sanctioned violence on the Edmund Pettus Bridge, resulting in a brutal confrontation that shocked the nation. The event became known as Bloody Sunday. Despite the brutality, Lewis persevered and continued to fight for justice, eventually leading a group of marchers from Selma to Montgomery. His activism led to the passage of the federal Voting Rights Act. In his later years, Lewis reflected on the significance of his actions, saying "Get in good trouble, necessary trouble, and help redeem the soul of America." His legacy continues to inspire generations to fight for justice and equality. END OF SUMMARY.
