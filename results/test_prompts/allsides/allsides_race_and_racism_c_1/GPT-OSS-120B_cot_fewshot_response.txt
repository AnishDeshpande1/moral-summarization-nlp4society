**SUMMARY:**  
On March 7, 1965, a young John Lewis led a peaceful voting‑rights march toward the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama, confronting a line of state troopers armed with nightsticks, gas canisters and horse‑riding posse members. The troopers unleashed brutal violence, beating Lewis, Amelia Boynton Robinson and dozens of others while nearby whites cheered. Lewis suffered a fractured skull but remained standing, embodying courage and the fight for freedom against a racist, segregationist regime. The state’s illegal, cruel assault shocked the nation and helped propel the Voting Rights Act. Lewis’s heroism endured for decades, inspiring later generations to “speak up, get in good trouble, and help redeem the soul of America.” His sacrifice and the collective struggle against injustice and oppression remain a powerful testament to the ongoing quest for redemption.
