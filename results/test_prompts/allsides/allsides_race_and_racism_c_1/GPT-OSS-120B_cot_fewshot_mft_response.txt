**SUMMARY:**
John Lewis, then a 25‑year‑old civil‑rights leader, faced a line of Alabama state troopers armed with nightsticks, gas masks and gas canisters as he led a voting‑rights march onto the Edmund Pettus Bridge on March 7, 1965. Defying an illegal order from Governor George C. Wallace’s authority, Lewis and fellow activist Hosea Williams attempted dialogue, but Major John Cloud unleashed state‑sanctioned violence. Troopers beat, beat‑up and tear‑gassed marchers, including Amelia Boynton Robinson, in a brutal, racist assault that shocked the nation. Lewis suffered a fractured skull, yet survived to testify before Judge Frank M. Johnson, who overturned Wallace’s order and upheld the demonstrators’ right to march—an act of subversion against oppressive authority. The “Bloody Sunday” violence became a catalyst for the Voting Rights Act, embodying the fight for freedom, justice and the redemption of America’s soul. Lewis later urged people to “speak up, get in good trouble, and help redeem the soul of America.” END OF SUMMARY.
