**SUMMARY:**  
John Lewis, a 25‑year‑old *civil* rights leader, faced a line of Alabama state troopers on the Edmund Pettus Bridge on March 7, 1965. The troopers, armed with gas canisters and nightsticks, unleashed *brutality* on the *peaceful* marchers, beating Lewis, Amelia Boynton Robinson and others while white onlookers *cheered*. Lewis, later celebrated as a *hero* of *freedom* and *justice*, recalled being struck repeatedly, his head protected only by a hand. The violent attack, ordered by segregationist Governor George Wallace, shocked the nation and helped spur the federal Voting Rights Act. After the assault, Judge Frank M. Johnson overturned Wallace’s order, allowing the historic 52‑mile march to Montgomery. Decades later, Lewis urged new generations to “*Speak up, speak out, get in the way*—to get into *necessary* *good trouble* and help *redeem* the *soul* of America.”
