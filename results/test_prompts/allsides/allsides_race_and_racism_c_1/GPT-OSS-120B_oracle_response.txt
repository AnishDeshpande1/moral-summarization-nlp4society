SUMMARY:  
On March 7, 1965, a young John Lewis led a peaceful **voting** **demonstration** across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama, carrying the **struggle** against **racial** oppression toward Governor George C. Wallace’s capital. As Lewis and fellow activist Hosea Williams approached, a line of state troopers, armed with gas canisters and nightsticks, **swung** and **jabbed** at the crowd, launching a brutal **spasm** of state‑sanctioned **violence**. Troopers **hit**, **knocked**, and **beaten** marchers; **whites cheered** while officers **teargassed** and **clubbed** participants, leaving Amelia Boynton Robinson **fractured** and unconscious. The **illegal** attack, later called “**Bloody** Sunday,” shocked the nation and led Judge Frank M. Johnson Jr. to **overturn** Wallace’s order, clearing the way for the historic 52‑mile **march** and **walk** to Montgomery that helped pass the Voting Rights Act. Lewis, who later **refused** to stay silent, urged future generations to “**Speak up, speak out, get in the way**,” urging **necessary trouble** to **redeem** America’s soul.  

END OF SUMMARY.
