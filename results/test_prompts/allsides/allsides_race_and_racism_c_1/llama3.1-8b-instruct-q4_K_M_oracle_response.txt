SUMMARY:

John Lewis, the chairman of the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, led hundreds of marchers on March 7, 1965, to deliver a message of freedom to segregationist Gov. George C. Wallace in Montgomery. Despite being told that their voting rights demonstrations were illegal, Lewis and his fellow marchers refused to back down. The ensuing confrontation sparked a spasm of state-sanctioned violence, with troopers wielding nightsticks and gas masks, and sheriff's posse members on horseback clubbing and beating the peaceful protesters. John Lewis was fractured when he was knocked to the ground by a trooper, but he carried the struggle against racial discrimination forward, testifying in court about the brutality of that day.

Lewis' actions helped lead to the passage of the federal Voting Rights Act and propelled him onto a global stage as a hero of the U.S. civil rights movement. He outlived many key players from Bloody Sunday and continued to march towards redemption, urging others to "get in good trouble, necessary trouble" and help redeem the soul of America.

END OF SUMMARY.
