SUMMARY:  
The article recounts the pivotal “Bloody Sunday” event on March 7, 1965, when 25‑year‑old civil‑rights leader John Lewis led a voting‑rights march across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama. Confronted by Alabama state troopers armed with gas masks, nightsticks and horse‑mounted sheriff’s deputies, Lewis and the peaceful demonstrators were ordered to disperse by Major John Cloud. Troopers violently attacked the marchers, clubbing Lewis—who suffered a fractured skull—and beating others, including Amelia Boynton Robinson, who was tear‑gassed and left unconscious. Lewis testified before U.S. District Judge Frank M. Johnson Jr., who later ruled that Governor George Wallace’s ban on the march was illegal, paving the way for the historic 52‑mile march to Montgomery and the eventual passage of the Voting Rights Act. The piece notes Lewis’s later life, his 2020 death, and the deaths of other key figures such as Wallace, Cloud, Judge Johnson, Hosea Williams, and Robinson, highlighting Lewis’s enduring call for “good trouble” to advance America’s soul.  

END OF SUMMARY.
