SUMMARY:

John Lewis, a hero of the U.S. civil rights movement, died at 80, leaving behind a legacy of fighting against racial discrimination. In 1965, Lewis led a voting rights march in Selma, Alabama, where he was brutally beaten by state troopers, sustaining a fractured skull. Despite the violence, Lewis continued to carry the message of freedom to segregationist Gov. George C. Wallace. The ensuing confrontation helped lead to the passage of the federal Voting Rights Act. Lewis's struggle against racial discrimination propelled him to a global stage, where he advocated for human rights and encouraged others to "get in good trouble, necessary trouble, and help redeem the soul of America." Lewis's courage in the face of brutality and his commitment to the movement inspired generations to come. His legacy will continue to march forward, even in the face of adversity.

END OF SUMMARY.
