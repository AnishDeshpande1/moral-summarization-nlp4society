SUMMARY:
The article recounts the brutal state‑sanctioned assault on civil‑rights leader John Lewis and peaceful marchers on “Bloody Sunday” in Selma, exposing the illegitimate abuse of authority by Alabama officials who ordered the illegal, violent dispersal of a voting‑rights demonstration. By detailing the night‑stick blows that fractured Lewis’s skull, the tear‑gas attack on Amelia Boynton Robinson, and the cheering white crowd, the piece highlights the severe harm inflicted on non‑violent protesters and the moral degradation of a system that protected segregation. It stresses the stark unfairness of Governor George Wallace’s segregationist orders and the subsequent legal victory of Judge Frank Johnson, who restored the right to march, underscoring the pursuit of justice and equal treatment. The narrative honors the courage of Lewis, Hosea Williams, and other activists, framing their non‑violent resistance as a righteous subversion of corrupt authority and a call to “get in good trouble” to redeem America’s soul. END OF SUMMARY.
