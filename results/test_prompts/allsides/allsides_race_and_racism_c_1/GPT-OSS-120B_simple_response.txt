SUMMARY: The article recounts the moral courage of John Lewis on “Bloody Sunday,” March 7, 1965, when he, then 25, led peaceful marchers onto the Edmund Pettus Bridge to demand voting rights in Selma, Alabama. Confronted by armed state troopers and a hostile white crowd, Lewis refused to retreat, embodying non‑violent resistance. Troopers, acting on Governor George Wallace’s segregationist orders, brutally assaulted the demonstrators—Lewis was beaten and suffered a fractured skull, while others, including Amelia Boynton Robinson, were tear‑gassed and beaten. Lewis’s testimony before Judge Frank M. Johnson Jr. helped overturn Wallace’s illegal ban, paving the way for the historic 52‑mile march and the passage of the Voting Rights Act. The piece frames Lewis’s steadfastness as a moral beacon, urging future generations to “speak up, get in good trouble, and help redeem the soul of America.” It notes the deaths of Wallace, the commanding trooper Maj. John Cloud, Judge Johnson, and other civil‑rights figures, underscoring the lasting moral legacy of Lewis’s sacrifice. END OF SUMMARY.
