SUMMARY:
The public‑health community warns that racism is a public‑health crisis, a deadly and cruel force that underlies the police violence killing Black Americans at nearly three times the rate of whites. Attempts to deflect blame onto “underlying health conditions” are ironic and mask the deeper injustice. Structural racism creates stark inequities and disparities in income, housing, education, food, green space and employment, leading to chronic stress, adverse health outcomes and higher mortality. Black communities face higher rates of diabetes, hypertension, asthma and heart disease, compounded by disproportionate exposure to pollution—“I can’t breathe”—and a higher burden as essential workers during COVID‑19, resulting in worse care and six‑fold higher death rates. The pandemic has laid bare these systemic inequities, and, as James Baldwin wrote, “nothing can be changed until it is faced.” We have a responsibility to acknowledge this reality, connect the dots, and press for meaningful policy change toward justice. END OF SUMMARY.
