SUMMARY:
The article argues that racism is a public‑health emergency, evident in the police killings of Black Americans—who die at nearly three times the rate of whites—and in the enduring health disparities rooted in slavery, discrimination, and unequal social determinants. It condemns attempts to blame George Floyd’s death on “underlying health conditions” as a deflection from police culpability, while highlighting that Black communities disproportionately suffer from diabetes, hypertension, asthma, heart disease, obesity, and chronic stress caused by systemic racism. These conditions are amplified by poorer housing, jobs, education, food access, and exposure to pollution, which also heighten COVID‑19 infection and mortality rates. The piece cites evidence that chronic discrimination harms cardiovascular health, accelerates aging, and drives higher maternal and infant mortality. Citing James Baldwin, it calls for confronting and acknowledging these entrenched inequities and urges immediate, meaningful policy action to dismantle structural racism and protect Black health. The moral of the story is that ignoring racism’s deadly impact on health perpetuates injustice; facing and fixing it is a collective responsibility.  
END OF SUMMARY.
