SUMMARY: The article argues that racism is a public health crisis, evident in the disproportionate police killings of Black Americans and the higher prevalence of chronic diseases such as diabetes, hypertension, asthma, and heart disease among them. It links these health disparities to structural racism—unequal job opportunities, income, housing, education, food access, and exposure to pollution—and to the direct physiological stress of discrimination. The piece cites examples like George Floyd’s death, the COVID‑19 pandemic’s outsized impact on Black communities, and the case of Erica Garner to illustrate how racism amplifies health risks. It highlights that Black residents are more likely to live near polluting facilities, work essential jobs, and receive poorer medical care, all contributing to higher infection and mortality rates. The author calls for acknowledgment of systemic racism’s role in health outcomes and urges meaningful policy changes to address these entrenched inequities. END OF SUMMARY.
