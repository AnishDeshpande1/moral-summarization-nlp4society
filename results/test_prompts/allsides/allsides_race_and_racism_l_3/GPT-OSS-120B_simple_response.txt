SUMMARY:  
The author argues that racism is a public‑health crisis, made starkly visible by the police killing of George Floyd and the broader pattern of violence against Black Americans. They contend that attempts to blame “underlying health conditions” for Floyd’s death deflect responsibility and ignore how systemic racism creates those very conditions—higher rates of diabetes, hypertension, asthma, obesity, and stress‑related illnesses among Black people. The piece links structural inequities—poor housing, limited job and educational opportunities, food deserts, and exposure to pollution—to poorer health outcomes, citing data on COVID‑19 infection and mortality disparities, as well as higher asthma death rates tied to proximity to polluting facilities. Chronic stress from discrimination further damages cardiovascular, immune, and aging processes, contributing to maternal and infant mortality. Quoting James Baldwin, the author calls for confronting this “immutable fact” that systemic racism has long oppressed African‑American health, urging individuals and policymakers to acknowledge the evidence and enact meaningful reforms before the moment for justice passes.  
END OF SUMMARY.
