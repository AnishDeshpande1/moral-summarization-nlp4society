SUMMARY:  
The Senate is set to vote Thursday on two bills that could finally end the nation’s longest shutdown, a crisis many lawmakers describe as a moral failure that holds the American people hostage. Senate leaders Mitch McConnell and Chuck Schumer announced a vote on a $5.7 billion border‑wall package that incorporates Trump’s “concessions,” followed—if needed—by a short‑term funding measure to reopen the government until Feb. 8. Critics, including House Speaker Nancy Pelosi and immigration advocates, argue the wall bill is an “unacceptable” scheme that deepens suffering at the border and that Republicans are complicit in a political standoff that endangers citizens.  

Democrats are pushing a separate spending plan that funds ports of entry and immigration judges but excludes wall money, a proposal they expect to die in the GOP‑led Senate. Centrist Democrats have drafted a letter urging a compromise: vote to fund the government now and consider a wall vote later. President Trump continues to demand wall funding, framing it as essential for national security, while immigration groups denounce the wall bill as a “Trojan Horse” that would cripple asylum protections. The outcome hinges on whether 60‑vote supermajorities can be secured amid fierce moral accusations from both sides.  

END OF SUMMARY.
