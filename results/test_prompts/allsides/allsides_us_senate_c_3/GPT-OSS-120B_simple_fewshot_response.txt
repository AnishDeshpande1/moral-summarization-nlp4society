SUMMARY:
The Senate is set to vote Thursday on two bills that could end the longest government shutdown in U.S. history, but both require a supermajority and face partisan roadblocks. One proposal would allocate $5.7 billion for President Trump’s border wall and include immigration concessions that Democrats say amount to a “Trojan Horse” for hard‑line policies, such as limiting asylum for Central American minors and capping refugee admissions. The other is a short‑term funding measure to keep the government open until Feb. 8, which also needs 60 votes and could be vetoed by Trump. Senate leaders Mitch McConnell and Chuck Schumer are urging their colleagues to set aside politics and vote “yes” to reopen the government, while House Speaker Nancy Pelosi condemns any Senate vote that ties funding to the wall, calling the shutdown a hostage‑taking of the American people. Centrist House Democrats are pushing a compromise that funds ports of entry and immigration judges but not the wall, hoping to break the impasse. The moral stakes are clear: the nation’s workers and services are suffering while leaders clash over a wall versus a functioning government, and a failure to compromise prolongs hardship for ordinary Americans.  
END OF SUMMARY.
