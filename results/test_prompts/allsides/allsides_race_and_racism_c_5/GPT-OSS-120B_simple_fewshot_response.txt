SUMMARY:
In the wake of the El Paso and Dayton mass shootings, Democratic frontrunner Joe Biden accused President Donald Trump of stoking the white‑supremacist hatred that fuels such attacks. Biden said Trump’s “clear language and code” have “fanned the flames of white supremacy” and linked the shooter’s anti‑Hispanic manifesto directly to Trump’s rhetoric about an “invasion” of Central Americans. Senator Cory Booker echoed the moral charge, warning that hate is “planted” before it can bloom, and called for concrete action against white nationalism and gun violence. Trump, who denies being racist, condemned “racism, bigotry and white supremacy” but stopped short of major gun reforms, instead proposing mental‑health measures and social‑media monitoring. Democratic candidates, including Biden and Booker, are pressing for stricter gun laws—universal background checks, assault‑weapon bans, and licensing—while framing the issue as a moral imperative to confront and dismantle the hateful ideology that underlies mass shootings. The campaign thus positions Trump’s rhetoric as a dangerous catalyst for violence that must be challenged with decisive policy and moral leadership.  
END OF SUMMARY.
