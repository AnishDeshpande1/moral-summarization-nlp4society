SUMMARY:
The wave of cyber‑attacks surrounding the 2016 campaign has already damaged American democracy, not by breaking voting machines but by sowing fear that the election could be hacked. A Carbon Black survey found 56 % of Americans “concerned” or “very concerned” about election hacking, and 20 % said this might keep them from voting—potentially 15 million voters. After the DNC breach, U.S. officials blamed senior Russian officials, while the mysterious hacker “Guccifer 2.0” resurfaced, urging others to monitor and expose “electoral fraud.” Experts dismiss the threat to actual machines as unlikely, but stress that the real weapon is a disinformation campaign that undermines confidence in the vote. Officials in all 50 states are on heightened alert, scanning systems and preparing for any disruption, from cyber intrusion to mundane sabotage like fire alarms. The moral takeaway is that even unfulfilled threats of digital fraud can erode public trust, depress turnout, and invite prolonged challenges to election legitimacy, threatening the very foundation of democratic governance.  
END OF SUMMARY.
