SUMMARY:  
A wave of cyber‑attacks and warnings about possible election‑day hacking has shaken confidence in the U.S. vote. A Carbon Black survey found 56 % of Americans are concerned or very concerned about the election being hacked, and 20 % say this might keep them from voting—potentially 15 million people. The DNC breach this summer, attributed by U.S. agencies to senior Russian officials, was claimed by the hacker “Guccifer 2.0,” who has now threatened to monitor and expose alleged fraud on Election Day. Experts dismiss the claim as rhetoric but say the fear it generates can suppress turnout and erode trust. DHS has briefed all states on cybersecurity assistance, though actual system compromise is considered unlikely due to the fragmented U.S. voting infrastructure. Russia’s likely goal is a disinformation campaign to undermine confidence rather than direct machine tampering. Officials warn that even non‑technical disruptions (e.g., fire alarms) could affect voting. The pervasive fear of digital sabotage may lead to post‑election challenges and long‑term doubts about legitimacy.  
END OF SUMMARY.
