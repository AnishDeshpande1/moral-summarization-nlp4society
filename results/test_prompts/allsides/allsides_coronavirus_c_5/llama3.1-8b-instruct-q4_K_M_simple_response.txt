SUMMARY:

The Department of Veterans Affairs (VA) has significantly reduced its use of hydroxychloroquine, an unproven malaria drug, on veterans with COVID-19. Initially, the VA used the drug due to limited treatment options and to provide "hope" for patients. However, as studies pointed to possible dangers and other treatments became available, the VA's use of the drug decreased dramatically. According to VA data, 1,370 veterans were prescribed hydroxychloroquine for COVID-19, but only a few prescriptions have been issued in recent weeks.

Veterans Affairs Secretary Robert Wilkie defended the initial use of the drug while also acknowledging the need to follow scientific evidence and expert advice. President Donald Trump has promoted the use of hydroxychloroquine despite lack of scientific evidence, which has raised concerns among veterans' organizations and lawmakers.

The VA is now turning to remdesivir as a treatment option, with preliminary results showing reduced recovery time. The shift away from hydroxychloroquine reflects the importance of prioritizing evidence-based medicine in responding to the pandemic.

END OF SUMMARY.
