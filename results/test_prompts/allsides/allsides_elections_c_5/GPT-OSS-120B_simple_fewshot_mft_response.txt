SUMMARY:
President Obama framed the federal response to Hurricane Sandy as a moral duty of national authority, promising “thoughts and prayers” plus the full resources of the government to protect and heal the millions harmed by the storm. He highlighted FEMA’s professional leadership and pledged to cut red‑tape, even offering a personal hotline for mayors and governors denied aid, underscoring the legitimacy and responsibility of federal power to care for citizens in crisis. By contrast, Republican Mitt Romney emphasized the moral virtue of decentralized, voluntary action, citing a canned‑food drive and a high‑school story about individuals cleaning their own “lane.” While he did not deny the need for government help, Romney argued that disaster relief should be shifted back to states and the private sector, reflecting a subversive stance toward centralized authority. The article notes bipartisan praise from Republican Governor Chris Christie for the federal effort, yet points out that Republicans, wary of federal spending, still target FEMA’s budget for cuts. The moral framing pits the caring, authoritative federal response against a subversive, individual‑focused approach that questions the scope of government aid. END OF SUMMARY.
