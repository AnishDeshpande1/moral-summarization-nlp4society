SUMMARY:
Venezuelans woke to a dramatic power shift that toppled Hugo Chávez’s authoritarian rule, ending a three‑day general strike and renaming the nation from the “Bolivarian Republic” to the plain “Republic of Venezuela.” Business leader Pedro Carmona, installed by the military, declared the strike over and dismissed the Bolivarian symbols, signaling a subversion of Chávez’s imposed authority. The transition was framed as a response to the lethal suppression of a peaceful 100,000‑person protest, where armed Chávez supporters fired on unarmed demonstrators, causing deaths and injuries—an egregious breach of care for citizens. Chávez’s own crackdown on dissent, including shutting down TV channels and filling PDVSA’s board with loyalists, was portrayed as cheating the public out of fair governance. After the army demanded his resignation, Chávez fled, was detained, and the country now faces a power vacuum. Observers note relief that the oppressive regime fell, but also anxiety about how the new leadership will address the deep social wounds and restore fairness, protect citizens, and rebuild legitimate authority.

END OF SUMMARY.
