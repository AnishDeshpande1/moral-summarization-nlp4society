SUMMARY:  
Recent weeks have highlighted a stark moral divide in trans‑Atlantic climate policy. The EU’s environment ministers formally agreed on 4 March to ratify the Kyoto Protocol before 1 June, positioning Europe to lead global climate protection ahead of the August 2002 Johannesburg summit. By contrast, the United States, under President Bush, unveiled a weak climate program that merely seeks an 18 % improvement in energy efficiency by 2012—far below what science demands. Even with this modest gain, U.S. emissions are projected to rise 24.5 % above 1990 levels, violating the responsibility implied by the Kyoto target of a 7 % cut. The IPCC warns that without decisive, coordinated action, temperatures could rise 5.8 °C by 2100, sea levels could climb 90 cm, and extreme weather will intensify. The EU therefore calls on the U.S. to “assume its responsibilities” and stabilize emissions at 1990 levels. Morally, the EU argues that climate leadership is not only an environmental duty but also an economic opportunity, creating jobs and fostering future‑proof technologies. The United States, as the world’s largest emitter, must re‑enter the Kyoto framework; its continued retreat is portrayed as a failure to meet a fundamental global responsibility.  

END OF SUMMARY.
