SUMMARY:  
In recent weeks the EU and the United States have taken divergent steps on climate policy. European environment ministers reached a political agreement on 4 March to ratify the Kyoto Protocol before 1 June, paving the way for the treaty’s entry into force ahead of the August 2002 Johannesburg summit. By contrast, President Bush’s 14 February US climate protection program is widely criticised as weak. The US “withdrawal” from Kyoto and the modest 18 % efficiency target are seen as “unheeded” “warnings” from scientists; even with a 3 % annual growth rate, absolute emissions would rise 24.5 % above 1990 levels, far short of the “decisive” cuts required. The IPCC “estimates” that industrialised nations must cut emissions by about 70 % by 2020 to avoid catastrophic warming. EU leaders therefore call on the United States to “assume” its responsibilities, “strengthen” its actions, and stop the “reticence” that prevents it from “committing” to the level of effort accepted by other industrialised countries. European policymakers argue that robust climate action will “show” economic benefits, creating jobs and fostering renewable‑energy markets, and that the US should not be “relieved” of its duty as the world’s biggest emitter.  

END OF SUMMARY.
