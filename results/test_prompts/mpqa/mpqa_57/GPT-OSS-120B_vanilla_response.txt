SUMMARY: Recent developments highlight a split in transatlantic climate policy. On 4 March, EU environment ministers agreed to ratify the Kyoto Protocol by 1 June, positioning Europe to meet the August 2002 Johannesburg summit. Conversely, US President Bush unveiled a modest climate plan on 14 February, aiming for an 18 % improvement in energy efficiency by 2012, which would still allow emissions to rise 24.5 % above 1990 levels due to economic growth. This falls short of Kyoto targets (EU ‑ 8 %, Japan ‑ 6 %, US ‑ 7 % reductions) and the IPCC’s call for industrialized nations to cut emissions to 70 % of 1990 levels by 2020. EU ministers urged the US to stabilize emissions at 1990 levels and match Kyoto efforts, emphasizing that strong climate policies drive technological innovation, export markets, and job creation. They argue that US participation is essential for global effectiveness and that the Kyoto Protocol should be swiftly implemented and extended beyond 2012, leaving the door open for US re‑entry. END OF SUMMARY.
