SUMMARY: Astronauts attempted the first run of the International Space Station’s permanent rail system on Monday, but the one‑ton railcar repeatedly stalled due to micro‑gravity effects. The car floated slightly off the track, breaking contact between its magnetic sensors and the iron strips in the aluminum rails. After moving only 17 feet at under half an inch per second, the automatic latching system disengaged, requiring ground controllers to manually secure the car. Astronaut Carl Walz issued commands to restart the car, which then rolled 26 feet to a second work station, only to stop again and need manual intervention. A third attempt succeeded in returning the car the same distance, but the latch failed once more. NASA plans software adjustments if the issue persists. The railcar, part of a $190 million system, is secured by rings on the 44‑foot girder and is slated for use later this summer to transport the station’s 58‑foot robotic arm as additional girders are installed. A final spacewalk will complete the girder installation. END OF SUMMARY.
