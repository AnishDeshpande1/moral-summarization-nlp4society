SUMMARY:  
Astronauts rolled the International Space Station’s new railcar down a short track, but a **snag** quickly halted the inaugural run of the first permanent railroad in orbit. NASA **blamed** the issue on weightlessness, suspecting the one‑ton car floated just off the rail, causing magnetic sensors to lose contact with the iron strips. The **problem** surfaced after the empty flatcar inched 17 feet at less than a half‑inch per second, stopped at a work station, and automatically latched itself down. Ground controllers took manual control, latching the car and later commanding astronaut Carl Walz to move it again. Each time the railcar rolled a short distance, the automatic latching **shut down** and controllers intervened. NASA manager Ben Sellari said they are learning how the mobile transporter works in zero‑g and may adjust the software if it becomes a **nuisance**. He assured there is no **danger** of the $190 million railcar rolling off the track, thanks to rings on both ends of the 44‑foot girder. The railcar will later transport the station’s 58‑foot robot arm as more girders are installed.  

END OF SUMMARY.
