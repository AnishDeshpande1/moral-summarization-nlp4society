SUMMARY:  
As Zimbabwe heads to a pivotal election, the nation teeters on the brink of bloodshed. Citizens like barber‑shop patron Wellington Chinyama ask one another whether they are ready for a civil war, reflecting a deep moral dread that the contest between long‑time autocrat Robert Mugabe and opposition leader Morgan Tsvangirai may erupt into violence. Reports of assassinations, police intimidation, and the government's willingness to rig voting rules—by inflating rural polling stations, restricting urban voters, and imposing onerous residency proofs—underscore a systematic effort to silence dissent. Senior ZANU‑PF officials openly declare that a Tsvangirai government would be intolerable, even hinting at supporting a coup. Meanwhile, the state has charged Tsvangirai with treason on dubious evidence, and dozens of MDC supporters have already been killed. International observers warn that both sides appear prepared to reject any result, risking rioting or military crackdown. The moral stakes are stark: a people yearning for democratic change face a regime prepared to sacrifice lives and liberty to cling to power, while the opposition confronts the threat of imprisonment or death. The upcoming vote could either ignite a tragic conflict or, if fairness prevails, pave the way for a less repressive future.  

END OF SUMMARY.
