SUMMARY:
As Zimbabwe heads to the polls, fear of a looming civil war dominates the national conversation. Surveys show opposition leader Morgan Tsvangirai ahead of long‑time autocrat Robert Mugabe, but both sides appear unwilling to accept defeat. Government officials and military leaders have openly declared they would not tolerate an MDC government, even hinting at a coup, while the regime has been accused of escalating political violence that has already claimed dozens of lives, mostly opposition supporters. Election‑law changes – more rural polling stations, a new residency requirement, and the exclusion of independent monitors – raise serious doubts about a free and fair count. Mugabe’s allies have charged Tsvangirai with high treason, and the army has reportedly been placed on “high alert.” Both camps warn that a contested result could trigger riots, martial law, or a crackdown, leaving the country on the brink of chaos. The election thus threatens not just a political transition but the very stability of Zimbabwe.

END OF SUMMARY.
