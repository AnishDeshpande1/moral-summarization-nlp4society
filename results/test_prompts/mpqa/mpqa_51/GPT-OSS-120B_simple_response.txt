SUMMARY: On February 14, President George W. Bush unveiled a voluntary, tax‑incentive‑driven alternative to the Kyoto Protocol, claiming it would cut U.S. greenhouse‑gas intensity by 18 % in ten years while preserving economic growth. The plan places no binding obligations on industry, relying on “goodwill” and offering $4.6 billion in incentives, and promises to respect the development rights of China and India while urging them to share the climate burden. The White House hailed the proposal as “the most audacious program in American history” and a cost‑saving “yardstick for progress.”  

Environmental groups reacted with moral outrage, denouncing the plan as a “Valentine’s Day gift for polluters.” Sierra Club director Carl Pope accused the administration of siding with the energy industry’s polluting agenda, while Greenpeace and the National Environment Trust warned that voluntary measures will not compel polluters to act responsibly. Critics argue the scheme sacrifices ethical responsibility for the climate in favor of short‑term economic interests, undermining global efforts to curb warming.  

END OF SUMMARY.
