SUMMARY:
President George W. Bush unveiled a voluntary, tax‑incentive alternative to the Kyoto Protocol, claiming it would cut U.S. greenhouse‑gas intensity by 18 % in ten years while preserving economic growth. The plan places no binding obligations on industry, relies on goodwill from polluters who generate over a third of global emissions, and offers $4.6 billion in incentives for renewable energy. Bush argues the approach respects developing nations’ right to grow and urges China and India to share the burden, while promising the United States will not block Kyoto ratifiers. Environmental groups slammed the proposal as a “Valentine’s Day gift for polluters,” noting it lacks enforceable limits, depends on doubtful corporate cooperation, and would achieve little real emission reduction. Critics from the Sierra Club, National Environment Trust and Greenpeace warned that the voluntary scheme abandons the sensible, health‑protecting steps needed to combat climate change and moves the U.S. opposite to the international community’s effort under Kyoto.

END OF SUMMARY.
