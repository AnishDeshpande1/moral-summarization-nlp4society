SUMMARY: On February 14, President George W. Bush unveiled a voluntary, tax‑incentive‑based alternative to the Kyoto Protocol, rejecting the binding emissions limits adopted by over 170 countries. The plan aims to cut U.S. greenhouse‑gas intensity by 18% within ten years, targeting a reduction from 183 to 151 tonnes per million dollars of GDP by 2012, and offers $4.6 billion in incentives for renewable energy. Bush emphasized that the approach respects developing nations’ right to economic growth while urging China and India to share responsibility for emissions. The White House promoted the proposal as a cost‑saving, growth‑compatible strategy, but environmental groups slammed it as ineffective and industry‑friendly. Critics from the Sierra Club, National Environment Trust, and Greenpeace argued that without mandatory limits the plan will do little to curb pollution and diverges sharply from the global consensus embodied in Kyoto. END OF SUMMARY.
