SUMMARY:
Pakistan’s foreign ministry dismissed the U.S. State Department’s latest human‑rights country report as “factually inaccurate and unwarranted,” arguing that it ignores a series of government initiatives aimed at advancing human rights. The spokesperson highlighted reforms such as the Juvenile Justice System Ordinance, a permanent commission on the status of women, police and jail reforms, a national plan to eliminate child labour, a fund for rehabilitating working children, and amendments to the 1951 Citizenship Act allowing women to pass Pakistani nationality to children of foreign husbands. Pakistan also noted its ratification of ILO conventions on equal pay and the worst forms of child labour, its signing of optional protocols to the Convention on the Rights of the Child, and the introduction of joint electorates for minorities. Despite these steps, the U.S. report still judged Pakistan’s human‑rights record “poor,” which the ministry called a gross misstatement. Pakistan rejected the assessment, urging states to cooperate rather than judge one another, and reaffirmed its commitment to human‑rights promotion, welcoming assistance from friendly nations.

END OF SUMMARY.
