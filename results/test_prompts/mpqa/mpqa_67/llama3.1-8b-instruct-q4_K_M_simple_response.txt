SUMMARY:

The Namibian government has dismissed a US human rights report that lists several human rights violations committed by security forces in the country. The report, which covers 2001, alleges extra-judicial killings, deaths in custody, torture, and arbitrary arrest. However, the government's Permanent Secretary, Mocks Shivute, responded by accusing the US of being a worse human rights violator due to its detention of over 300 people at Guantanamo Bay without trial.

Shivute claimed that the report contained factual inaccuracies and was based on unverified reports. He also pointed out that similar actions by the US were not considered human rights abuses just because they were committed by the US. The government's response has been met with criticism, with some accusing it of attempting to deflect attention from its own human rights record.

The report highlighted several cases of abuse by Special Field Force members and criticized the government for failing to account for the whereabouts of people detained by security forces. The government's dismissal of the report raises questions about its commitment to upholding human rights in Namibia.

END OF SUMMARY.
