SUMMARY:
Namibia’s government publicly dismissed a U.S. human‑rights report that documented serious abuses by Namibian security forces, including extrajudicial killings, torture, disappearances and unlawful detentions in 2001. Information Permanent Secretary Shivute deflected criticism by accusing the United States of being the world’s worst rights violator, pointing to Guantanamo Bay and post‑9/11 detention practices, and claimed the Namibian report was riddled with factual errors and unverified claims. While the report highlighted constitutional violations—failure to inform detainees of reasons for arrest, denial of timely magistrate hearings, and targeting of suspected UNITA and CLA sympathizers—Shivute dismissed it as “little value” and a “mere reproduction of unsubstantiated reports.” The moral framing underscores a double standard: Namibia rejects external scrutiny of its own human‑rights record while deflecting to U.S. controversies, thereby evading accountability for documented abuses by its security forces. This response raises concerns about hypocrisy and the unwillingness to address systemic violations at home. END OF SUMMARY.
