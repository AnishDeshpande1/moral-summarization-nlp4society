SUMMARY:  
The Namibian government publicly dismissed a U.S. human‑rights report that accused its security forces of extra‑judicial killings, torture, disappearances and other abuses in 2001. Information Permanent Secretary Shivute counter‑attacked, labeling the United States the world’s worst human‑rights violator for holding detainees at Guantanamo Bay without trial and for post‑9/11 detention camps in Afghanistan. He suggested that the same acts the U.S. commits would be ignored as “acceptable” because they are American. Shivute also claimed the U.S. report was riddled with factual errors—misidentifying the party newspaper Namibia Today as government‑owned and confusing the affiliation of the National Union of Namibian Workers—thereby deeming it “little value.” While the report highlighted constitutional violations such as failure to inform arrestees of reasons for arrest, denial of timely magistrate hearings, and targeting of suspected UNITA and CLA supporters, the Namibian response framed the criticism as hypocritical American bias and unreliable journalism rather than a moral indictment of its own security forces.  

END OF SUMMARY.
