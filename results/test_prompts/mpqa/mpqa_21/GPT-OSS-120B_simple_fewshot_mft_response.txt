SUMMARY:
A NASA‑led study shows that the world has already begun to cool modestly thanks to declining greenhouse‑gas emissions since the late 1980s, a trend the researchers attribute in part to the authoritative action of the 1987 Montreal Protocol, which successfully phased out ozone‑depleting chlorofluorocarbons—powerful heat‑trapping gases. By contrast, the politically dominant Kyoto Protocol, which focuses narrowly on carbon‑dioxide cuts, faces resistance because it threatens major economic interests tied to coal, oil and gas. Hansen and Sato argue that policymakers should respect the proven authority of scientific evidence and broaden their regulatory scope to include other pollutants such as black carbon, tropospheric ozone, and methane. Reducing methane by 30 % would deliver climate benefits comparable to the ambitious CO₂ reductions envisioned by Kyoto, effectively subverting the narrow, contentious focus on carbon alone and demonstrating that a wider, scientifically grounded approach can achieve meaningful climate progress. Their findings call for a shift in authority from politically charged negotiations toward evidence‑based, comprehensive pollutant control.  
END OF SUMMARY.
