SUMMARY: A NASA GISS study finds that declining greenhouse‑gas emissions since the late 1980s have slowed the atmosphere’s heat‑retention rate, partly due to the 1987 Montreal Protocol’s phase‑out of chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs), which are potent greenhouse gases. If 1980‑level emissions had continued, temperatures by 2050 would match those projected for 2100 under doubled CO₂. The slowdown also reflects slower growth of methane and CO₂, possibly from increased plant and ocean uptake, though the reasons remain unclear. Researchers James Hansen and Makiko Sato suggest that targeting pollutants other than CO₂—such as black carbon, tropospheric ozone, and methane—could yield climate benefits comparable to those expected from the Kyoto Protocol’s CO₂ cuts. For example, a 30 % reduction in methane emissions could match the climate impact of Kyoto’s first‑period CO₂ reductions. The study, published Dec. 18, 2001 in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, highlights alternative mitigation pathways alongside Kyoto. END OF SUMMARY.
