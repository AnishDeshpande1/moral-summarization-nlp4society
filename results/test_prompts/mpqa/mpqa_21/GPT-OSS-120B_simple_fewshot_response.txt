SUMMARY:
A new NASA study shows that the world is already cooling slightly thanks to declining greenhouse‑gas emissions since the late 1980s, a trend linked to the 1987 Montreal Protocol’s phase‑out of ozone‑depleting chlorofluorocarbons, which are also potent heat‑trappers. Had emissions continued at their 1980 peak, temperatures by 2050 would have risen to levels now expected only by 2100. While the slowdown also reflects unclear reductions in methane and carbon dioxide—possibly from greater plant and ocean uptake—the researchers argue that focusing solely on CO₂, as the Kyoto Protocol does, is politically fraught and economically painful. They propose a broader, morally responsible strategy: targeting additional pollutants such as black‑carbon soot, lower‑atmosphere ozone, and methane from waste and energy production. Cutting methane by 30 % would deliver climate benefits comparable to the CO₂ cuts envisioned for Kyoto’s first commitment period (2008‑2012). The study suggests that diversified, science‑driven policies can achieve meaningful climate mitigation without the heavy economic sacrifices tied to carbon‑only approaches.  

END OF SUMMARY.
