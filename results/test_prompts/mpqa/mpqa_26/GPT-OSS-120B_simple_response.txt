SUMMARY:  
The article frames the United States’ refusal to ratify the Kyoto Protocol as a morally reprehensible act that betrays global responsibility and undermines Japan’s pride as an environmental steward. It highlights the stark climate threats—dying coral reefs, disappearing polar bears, starving migratory birds—and argues that the 1997 Kyoto agreement was the world’s moral promise to combat them. Japanese voices, epitomized by Mie Asaoka of the Kiko Network, condemn the “greatest gas‑emitting country” for turning its back on this promise, calling the move “absolutely inadmissible.” The piece casts the Bush administration’s “do‑it‑our‑way” alternative as unilateral, self‑serving, and likely to encourage other nations to abandon Kyoto, creating a two‑tier system that lets the US and a few allies evade stricter limits. While some Japanese industry warns that stricter policies could hurt competitiveness, the dominant moral narrative is that the US’s stance is an unjust, selfish betrayal of a collective ethical duty to protect the planet.  

END OF SUMMARY.
