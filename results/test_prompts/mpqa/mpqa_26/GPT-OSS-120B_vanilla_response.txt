SUMMARY:  
The article reports Japanese frustration with the United States’ decision not to ratify the Kyoto Protocol, which was signed by 150 nations in 1997 to combat global warming. Japanese environmental groups, represented by the Kiko Network, criticize the move as unacceptable for the world’s largest greenhouse‑gas emitter and view it as a sign of growing U.S. unilateralism after 9/11. Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi expressed disappointment during President George Bush’s visit, while U.S. officials offered an alternative “Clear Skies” plan that promises market‑based cuts but is seen by critics as allowing continued emissions growth. The divergence could create a two‑tier system, with the U.S., Australia and Canada setting looser targets while other countries adhere to stricter Kyoto limits. Some Japanese business leaders argue that further emission cuts would harm industrial competitiveness, noting Japan’s own energy‑saving initiatives. Overall, the U.S. stance is straining relations with Japan and other allies, raising doubts about the Kyoto Protocol’s effectiveness.  
END OF SUMMARY.
