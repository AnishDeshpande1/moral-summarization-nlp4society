SUMMARY:  
The United States released its 2001 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices to a chorus of scorn, as journalists and critics highlighted glaring double standards. While the report painstakingly details abuses in democratic allies such as India, it glosses over the egregious violations of strategic partners like Saudi Arabia, Egypt and Turkey, allocating them only brief, superficial coverage. Officials defended the exercise as “even‑handed,” even refusing to critique the U.S. itself as “unfair.” In a terse briefing, Assistant Secretary Lorne Cramer offered vague, non‑committal answers about concrete actions in Saudi Arabia, deflecting to generic “talks” and unrelated examples from Uzbekistan or Kyrgyzstan. When pressed for tangible progress, he could point only to reforms in Pakistan and could not name any specific initiatives for Saudi Arabia, acknowledging that the impact of U.S. engagement there remained unknown. The episode underscored a moral indictment: the human‑rights report has become a ritualistic tool that castigates less‑important nations while excusing the abuses of key allies, revealing a hypocrisy that fuels domestic criticism of America’s own civil‑liberty erosion.  

END OF SUMMARY.
