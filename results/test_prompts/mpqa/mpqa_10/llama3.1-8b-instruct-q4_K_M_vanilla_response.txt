SUMMARY:

The US State Department's annual human rights report has been met with skepticism and scorn due to its perceived hypocrisy in criticizing some countries while turning a blind eye to the abuses of others, particularly allies such as Saudi Arabia and Pakistan. Critics argue that the report is a meaningless ritual, and that Washington's kid-glove treatment of its allies undermines its credibility. The report itself acknowledges human rights violations in India, but devotes only 32 pages to Saudi Arabia, one of the world's least free countries.

During a briefing to release the report, US Assistant Secretary Lorne Craner struggled to defend American policy, citing examples of programs aimed at promoting democracy and human rights in other countries, but failing to provide concrete evidence of such efforts in Saudi Arabia. Critics pressed for tangible results, with Craner ultimately admitting that he didn't know the effect of these efforts.

The report's credibility has been further eroded by its failure to address human rights abuses within the US itself, with officials arguing that it wouldn't be fair to do so.
