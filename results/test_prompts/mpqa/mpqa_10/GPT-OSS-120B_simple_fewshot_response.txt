SUMMARY:
The United States released its 2001 Country Reports on Human Rights Practices to a chorus of scorn, as journalists and observers highlighted the stark double standards in the report. While the lengthy dossier on democratic India details countless abuses, the entry on repressive Saudi Arabia is a perfunctory 32‑page note, and the report omits any assessment of U.S. civil‑rights violations. Critics seized on the administration’s vague answers, noting that Assistant Secretary Lorne Cramer could cite tiny projects in Uzbekistan and Kyrgyzstan but offered no concrete actions in Saudi Arabia, the nation with arguably the worst record. When pressed, Cramer deflected, comparing Saudi Arabia to Iraq, North Korea and Libya, and reduced the U.S. role to “talking” at unspecified levels, without tangible outcomes. The episode underscored accusations of U.S. hypocrisy: harshly condemning lesser‑aligned states while shielding strategic allies such as Saudi Arabia, Egypt and Turkey, thereby eroding the credibility of the annual human‑rights exercise. END OF SUMMARY.
