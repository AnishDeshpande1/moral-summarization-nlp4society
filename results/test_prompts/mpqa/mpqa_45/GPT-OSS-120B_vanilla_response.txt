SUMMARY:  
On May 4, 2002, a coalition of 32 NGOs announced a 100‑day boycott of Malaysia’s Human Rights Commission (Suhakam), protesting the government’s failure to act on the commission’s recommendations, including its maiden and freedom‑of‑assembly reports. The NGOs, led by Hakam president Ram Das Tikamdas, criticized the dismissal of the Kesas report that condemned police excesses, the continued use of the Internal Security Act, bans on assemblies, restrictions on speech, and undemocratic election law amendments. They also highlighted the government’s refusal to ratify key international human‑rights treaties. Concerns were raised over Suhakam’s independence after former Attorney‑General Abu Talib Othman was appointed chair and three commissioners were not re‑appointed, perceived as retaliation for the Kesas report. New commissioners, largely retired civil servants, were questioned for their ability to critique executive policies. The NGOs pledged to cease dialogue, memorandums, and complaints to Suhakam during the boycott, monitoring its compliance with past recommendations instead. Suhakam commissioner Prof Hamdan Adnan called the boycott “unfair,” urging NGOs to cooperate. The boycott will be reassessed after the 100‑day period.  

END OF SUMMARY.
