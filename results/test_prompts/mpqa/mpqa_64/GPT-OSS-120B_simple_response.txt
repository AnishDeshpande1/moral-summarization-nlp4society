SUMMARY:  
Robin Cook announced that regular British troops will not be deployed to fight the Taliban, stressing that Britain has not placed its soldiers in a civil war and sees no reason to change that stance. While SAS and U.S. forces continue to support the Northern Alliance, Cook hailed the recent advances – the surrender of Kunduz and ongoing negotiations for Kandahar – as a moral victory, arguing that the lack of a British ground presence is “an advance, not a retreat.” He noted that troops remain on a short‑term standby, but the situation is “better than anyone could have hoped for.” The article also reports that humanitarian conditions remain precarious: the UN refugee chief and Oxfam warn that Afghanistan is still insecure and lacking food aid, while a prison riot killed many captured foreign fighters. Prime Minister Tony Blair’s spokesman echoed the optimistic tone, saying the campaign’s military, diplomatic and humanitarian progress is moving in the right direction, but any future British military action will require clear evidence. The overall moral narrative frames restraint, humanitarian urgency, and cautious optimism as the guiding principles.
