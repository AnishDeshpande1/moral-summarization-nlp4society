SUMMARY:
British Foreign Secretary Robin Cook signalled that regular troops will not be put into a new war in Afghanistan, noting that the recent collapse of Taliban control in Kunduz and the crumbling of their northern stronghold have reduced the need for British forces. SAS and American units continue fighting alongside the Northern Alliance, but Cook stressed that Britain remains on 48‑hour standby while the situation improves. Negotiations are under way to avert further bloodshed as Taliban commanders discuss the surrender of Kandahar, their last major stronghold, after 70 tribal leaders called for peace. The fall of Kunduz was followed by a riot in a prison near Mazar‑e‑Sharif where hundreds of foreign fighters were killed, highlighting ongoing instability. UN refugee chief Ruud Lubbers warned that aid assistance is still insecure and that much work remains to create a safe environment for humanitarian efforts. Prime Minister Tony Blair’s spokesman praised the Alliance’s military success as the best way of getting food to the population, while reiterating that any future anti‑terrorism action must be based on solid evidence. The campaign against terrorism continues, but Britain will not commit regular troops at this stage.  
END OF SUMMARY.
