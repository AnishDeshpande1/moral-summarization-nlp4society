SUMMARY:

British troops will not be sent to fight the Taliban, according to Robin Cook, as the militia's control crumbled in their final northern stronghold of Kunduz. The city's top Taliban commander was among thousands who surrendered after days of talks interspersed with fighting and a prison riot that killed hundreds of foreign fighters captured during the siege of Mazar-e-Sharif. The fall of Kunduz leaves Kandahar as the Taliban's last major stronghold, prompting calls from 70 Afghan tribal leaders to avert bloodshed through surrender negotiations.

The situation is "better than anybody could have hoped for," according to Cook, who said British troops remained on standby but would not be put into the ground civil war. The Prime Minister's official spokesman echoed this sentiment, saying the progress made on the military, diplomatic, and humanitarian fronts was a success, but that the campaign was far from over.

The UN Commissioner for Refugees, Ruud Lubbers, highlighted the need to create a secure environment for aid agencies to operate in, citing instability across the country as a major obstacle. The British government has stressed that any future military action would have to be based on evidence and part of a multi-faceted campaign against terrorism.

END OF SUMMARY.
