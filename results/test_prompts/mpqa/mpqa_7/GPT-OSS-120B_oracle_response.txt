SUMMARY:  
A new study by Alfredo Keller confirms the dramatic decline in President Hugo Chávez’s popularity, mirroring recent polls that show a nation engulfed in crisis. Among 1,000 adults surveyed across major Venezuelan cities, more than half say Chávez must be removed to solve the country’s problems, with 49 % favoring institutional replacement and 5 % endorsing forceful means. Approval has fallen from 84 % in April 1999 to just 33 % in November, while disapproval rose to 58 %, reflecting growing insecurity and unemployment. The myth of a rich‑poor polarization has collapsed; 78 % now see many poor opposing Chávez. A strong call for a constitutional referendum (75 % support) underscores the demand for a solution to the unresolved crisis. Chávez’s perceived incapacity and shrinking ability to inspire hope are highlighted by analysts, who note that two‑thirds of Venezuelans hold him responsible for the nation’s problems. Despite this, fragmented opposition keeps him in first place, with 25 % still preferring him over other candidates.  

END OF SUMMARY.
