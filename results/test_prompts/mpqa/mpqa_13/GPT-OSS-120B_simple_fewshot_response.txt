SUMMARY:
U.S. and Japanese officials wrapped up two days of climate talks in Tokyo, focusing on joint research and technology cooperation to combat global warming. The dialogue marked the first high‑level meeting since President George Bush announced a new U.S. approach that favors voluntary greenhouse‑gas limits tied to economic growth rather than the mandatory targets of the 1997 Kyoto Protocol. The United States pledged to boost R&D on climate solutions, while Japan reaffirmed its intention to ratify Kyoto despite Bush’s earlier rejection of the treaty, which he argued would impede U.S. economic expansion. The talks highlighted a moral clash: the U.S. prioritizes economic growth over binding emissions commitments, whereas Japan stresses adherence to international environmental agreements. Both sides left the conference with a commitment to continue scientific collaboration, but the underlying disagreement over voluntary versus mandatory climate action remains unresolved.

END OF SUMMARY.
