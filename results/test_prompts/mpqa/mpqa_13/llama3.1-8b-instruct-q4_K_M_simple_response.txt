SUMMARY:
Representatives from Japan and the United States concluded two days of climate talks in Tokyo, focusing on cooperation in science and technology to combat global warming. This meeting marked the first between the two countries since President Bush unveiled a new US plan for voluntary greenhouse gas emissions limits tied to economic growth, rather than mandatory targets. The US emphasized its commitment to research and development in addressing climate change, while Japan reaffirmed its intention to ratify the Kyoto protocol, which was rejected by President Bush last year due to concerns it would hinder US economic growth.

This development highlights a significant divide between the two nations on climate policy. While Japan remains committed to international cooperation on reducing emissions, the US is pursuing a more voluntary approach that may not be sufficient to address the urgent need for action on global warming. This raises questions about the moral obligation of nations to work together to mitigate the impacts of climate change and protect the planet for future generations.

END OF SUMMARY.
