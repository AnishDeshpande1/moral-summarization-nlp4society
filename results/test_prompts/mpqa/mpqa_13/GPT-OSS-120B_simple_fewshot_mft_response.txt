SUMMARY:
U.S. and Japanese officials wrapped up two days of climate talks in Tokyo, focusing on joint science and technology cooperation to curb global warming. The dialogue marked the first high‑level encounter since President George Bush unveiled a new U.S. climate strategy that favors voluntary emission limits tied to economic growth, deliberately sidestepping the mandatory targets of the 1997 Kyoto Protocol. By rejecting Kyoto as a barrier to U.S. prosperity, Bush positioned the United States as an authority challenging established international norms. Japan, in contrast, reaffirmed its commitment to ratify the Kyoto agreement, upholding the authority of the global treaty framework. The talks therefore highlighted a clash of authority: the U.S. asserting a subversive, growth‑first approach, while Japan defended the established international climate order. Both sides pledged to promote research and development on climate change, but the underlying power dynamic underscored a contest between unilateral U.S. policy and multilateral environmental authority.  
END OF SUMMARY.
