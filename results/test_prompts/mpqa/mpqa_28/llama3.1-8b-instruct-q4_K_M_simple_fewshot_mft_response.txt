SUMMARY:

The U.S. naval base at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, may soon become a prison for captured al Qaeda terrorists and Taliban fighters. Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld has announced plans to house these prisoners on the island's isolated eastern coast, citing its "strategic importance" as a detention center. The move has been met with support from some lawmakers, including Rep. Ileana Ros-Lehtinen, who sees it as an opportunity to underscore the base's significance and potentially even return the territory to Cuba. However, concerns about the potential for human rights abuses and the base's isolation have also been raised.

Rumsfeld has downplayed these worries, stating that there are no plans to stage military tribunals or trials at the base. The biggest challenge will be transporting prisoners from South Asia to the Caribbean. The move raises important questions about the balance between national security and human rights, as well as the potential consequences for the detainees.

The decision to use Guantanamo Bay as a detention center is morally significant, as it involves the subversion of international law and potentially infringes on the human rights of the prisoners. It also raises concerns about care and harm, particularly given the base's isolated location and history of housing refugees.
