SUMMARY:
The Pentagon, led by Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld, has decided to use the Guantánamo Bay naval base in Cuba to detain captured al Qaeda terrorists and Taliban fighters, asserting the island’s “least worst” status for holding dangerous enemies far from U.S. soil. By placing the prisoners on a base that is not U.S. territory, the administration maintains legal authority over the detainees while avoiding the obligations of residency and citizenship, reinforcing governmental control and subverting calls to return the base to Cuba. Supporters such as Rep. Ileana Ros‑Lehtinen praise the move for reinforcing strategic authority and giving “new life” to the facility. The plan also reflects a concern for national security and public safety, emphasizing the need to protect Americans from the potential harm these terrorists pose. Logistical challenges of transporting prisoners from Afghanistan are acknowledged, but officials stress that past experience with refugee detainees provides a ready framework. The decision underscores the government’s authority to secure the nation while addressing the moral imperative to shield citizens from the threats posed by extremist violence.

END OF SUMMARY.
