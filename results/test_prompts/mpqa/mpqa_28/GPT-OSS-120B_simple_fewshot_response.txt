SUMMARY:
The Pentagon plans to use the Guantánamo Bay naval base in Cuba to house captured al Qaeda terrorists and Taliban fighters, a shift from its historic role detaining Cuban and Haitian migrants. Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld called the remote island “the least worst” option despite the logistical challenge of moving dozens of high‑risk detainees thousands of miles from Afghanistan. Rep. Ileana Ros‑Lehtinen praised the move, arguing it underscores the base’s strategic value and revives “Gitmo” at a time when some lawmakers urge returning the territory to Cuba. Because Guantánamo is not U.S. soil, detainees would not receive residency or citizenship rights, raising moral questions about due‑process protections. No military tribunals are planned, and the facility, which can hold about 100 people, currently houses roughly 45 prisoners. The proposal revives old contingency plans from 1999 and highlights the tension between national security imperatives and the ethical duty to treat even the most dangerous suspects with legal safeguards. The decision also risks diplomatic friction with Cuba, though officials downplay any expected retaliation from Fidel Castro.

END OF SUMMARY.
