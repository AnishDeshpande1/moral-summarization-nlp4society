SUMMARY:
U.S. Secretary of State Colin Powell’s criticism of President Hugo Chávez’s foreign policy sparked a forceful backlash from Venezuela’s ruling party, which condemned the remarks as a breach of national sovereignty and a violation of the principle of non‑intervention. Party leaders such as Guillermo Garcia Ponce, Cilia Flores, and Noheli Pocaterra stressed that Venezuela’s domestic and external decisions are the exclusive concern of Venezuelans and must be respected by all nations, including the United States. They argued that Chávez’s trips to Cuba, Libya and Iraq are legitimate efforts to strengthen trade and OPEC ties, not invitations for foreign approval. Opposition deputies linked Powell’s comments to a broader U.S. smear campaign, accusing journalists of fabricating evidence of terrorist links to justify intervention. Even some pro‑government deputies warned that such external criticism harms Venezuela’s international image, deters investment and risks a diplomatic breakdown with its main trade partner. Across the board, the response framed Powell’s statements as unjustified meddling that threatens Venezuela’s right to self‑determination and peaceful sovereign governance.  
END OF SUMMARY.
