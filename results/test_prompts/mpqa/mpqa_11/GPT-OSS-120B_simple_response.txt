SUMMARY:  
U.S. Secretary of State Colin Powell’s criticism of President Hugo Chávez’s foreign policy sparked a swift backlash from Venezuela’s ruling party, which framed the episode as a breach of national sovereignty and a violation of the principle of non‑intervention. Guillermo Garcia Ponce of the Political Command of the Revolution called Powell’s remarks “unusual,” insisting that Venezuela’s domestic and external policies are exclusively Venezuelan matters mandated by the constitution. Deputies Cilia Flores and Noheli Pocaterra echoed this moral stance, condemning U.S. meddling and demanding equal respect for Venezuela’s sovereign right to choose its allies and travel destinations, especially to OPEC and trade partners.  

Opposition deputy Pedro Carreno linked Powell’s comments to a smear campaign involving a disputed video allegedly showing ties to Colombian rebels, accusing journalists of treason and accusing the U.S. of seeking intervention or blockade. Conversely, some opposition figures (e.g., Alejandro Arzola, Claudio Fermin, Julio Montoya) used the incident to moralize about Venezuela’s harmful governance, warning that Chávez’s “aggressive” and ambiguous policies erode investor confidence and strain relations with the United States, Venezuela’s principal trade partner.  

The discourse thus pivots on a moral contest: Venezuelan officials defend sovereignty and non‑interference, while critics argue that Chávez’s conduct threatens democratic norms and economic stability.  
END OF SUMMARY.
