SUMMARY: U.S. Secretary of State Colin Powell’s criticism of President Hugo Chávez’s foreign policy—particularly visits to countries on the U.S. terrorism blacklist and questions about Venezuela’s democratic credentials—prompted sharp rebukes from Venezuela’s ruling party. Leaders such as Guillermo Garcia Ponce (CPR), Deputy Cilia Flores (MVR), and National Assembly Vice‑President Noheli Pocaterra condemned the remarks as violations of sovereignty and non‑intervention, emphasizing that Venezuela’s domestic and foreign policies are solely its own concern. Deputy Pedro Carreno linked Powell’s comments to a recent scandal involving a video allegedly showing Chávez’s ties to Colombian rebels, accusing opposition journalists of a smear campaign aimed at inviting U.S. intervention or blockade. While some opposition figures (e.g., Alejandro Arzola, Claudio Fermin, Julio Montoya) agreed that Chávez’s actions harm Venezuela’s international standing and oil sales to the United States, the government officials uniformly rejected any U.S. interference and defended their diplomatic choices. END OF SUMMARY.
