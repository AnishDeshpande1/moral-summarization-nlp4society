SUMMARY:

Venezuela's President Hugo Chavez was ousted for two days in April, highlighting the country's struggle to move beyond coups and tyrannical tactics. Chavez, a former Army coup-meister, has abused his popular mandate with heavy-handed measures and rewritten the Constitution to suit his interests. His economic promises to the poor have been broken, and his diplomatic flirtation with leftist world dictators has raised concerns about his intentions for more power. The ousting was sparked by Chavez's attempt to stack the board of the state-owned oil company in his favor, which led to a massive protest involving business groups, labor unions, and possibly portions of the military. International pressure and domestic support forced the generals to let Chavez take office again, tarnishing the US role as a champion of democracy. The incident highlights the need for Venezuela to adopt constitutional and nonviolent means to resolve conflicts, rather than relying on coups and tyranny.
