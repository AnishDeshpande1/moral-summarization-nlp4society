SUMMARY:
Venezuela’s brief ouster of President Hugo Chávez last week exposed the futility of fighting tyranny with more tyranny. Chávez, a former coup‑leader who rose to power through elections three years ago, has been abusing his mandate with heavy‑handed, constitution‑rewriting measures and broken promises to the poor, while courting leftist dictators to consolidate personal power. When he attempted to rig the board of the state oil company, executives rebelled, sparking a massive, largely peaceful protest by business groups and labor unions that drew on covert military support. After gunfire killed dozens during a march on the presidential palace, military leaders compelled Chávez to step aside, only to restore him under international pressure and renewed domestic backing. The episode shows that Venezuela—once a key oil giant in a continent of democracies—can and should remove a tyrant through constitutional, non‑violent means rather than coups. The U.S. Bush administration’s tacit approval of the brief coup further blemishes America’s reputation as a defender of democracy and underscores the need for genuine, peaceful opposition to authoritarian rule.

END OF SUMMARY.
