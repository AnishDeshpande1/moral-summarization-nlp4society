SUMMARY: The article critiques Venezuelan President Hugo Chávez, describing his rule as tyrannical despite his electoral mandate. It claims Chávez rewrote the constitution, sought to extend his term beyond 2020, and failed to deliver on economic promises to the poor while aligning with other leftist dictators. Recent unrest began when Chávez attempted to appoint allies to the board of the state oil company, prompting executives to threaten an oil shutdown. This sparked a broad coalition of business groups, labor unions, and parts of the military to protest, culminating in a violent march on the presidential palace that left over a dozen dead. Military leaders briefly forced Chávez to step down, but international pressure and renewed domestic support led to his reinstatement. The piece also alleges the U.S. Bush administration tacitly supported the coup, damaging its democratic credibility. The author argues that Venezuela can remove Chávez through constitutional, non‑violent means, emphasizing that Latin America has moved beyond coups. END OF SUMMARY.
