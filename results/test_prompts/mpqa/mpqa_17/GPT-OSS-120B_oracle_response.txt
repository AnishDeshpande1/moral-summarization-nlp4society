SUMMARY:  
The recent turmoil in Venezuela shows how “tyranny” and “coup‑plotting” can backfire. President Hugo Chávez, once a coup‑master himself, has “abused” his popular mandate with heavy‑handed “measures” and a Constitution largely “rewritten” by his allies. His promises to the poor are in “shreds,” and his diplomatic “flirtation” with leftist dictators reveals a desire to “gain” more power. Yet Latin America has moved beyond “coups,” and the nation’s “means” to “remove” Chávez lie in constitutional, non‑violent avenues. The crisis began when Chávez tried to “stack” the board of the state oil company; the executives “revolted,” threatening a “cutoff” of oil. This sparked a broad “opposition” as business groups and labor unions “joined” a massive “protest.” When “gunshots” rang out during a march on the presidential palace, killing more than a dozen, military leaders “forced” Chávez to step aside. International pressure and renewed domestic support then allowed the generals to let him “take” office again, while the Bush administration “welcomed” the “coup,” further “tarnishing” the U.S. democratic image.  

END OF SUMMARY.
