SUMMARY:
Thousands of Cubans took to the streets to celebrate Venezuelan President Hugo Chávez’s reinstatement, drawing a direct parallel between the thwarted coup in Caracas and the historic U.S.-backed Bay of Pigs invasion that attempted to topple Fidel Castro in 1961. The crowd—workers, students, housewives and retirees—spoke of “dignity and honor” in defending a fellow revolutionary, emphasizing the solidarity between Cuba’s socialist government and Chávez’s left‑wing regime, both seen as bulwarks against American imperialism. A senior Cuban Communist Party official called the gathering a “beautiful historic coincidence,” underscoring the moral triumph of popular resistance over foreign‑engineered regime change. While the Bush administration dismissed claims it had quietly supported Chávez’s removal, the celebration highlighted a shared narrative of anti‑U.S. struggle and the legitimacy of popular movements that reject external interference. The article frames the events as a moral victory for socialism and a rebuke of past U.S. attempts to undermine leftist governments in the hemisphere. END OF SUMMARY.
