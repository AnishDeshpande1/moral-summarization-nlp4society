SUMMARY:
South Africa and Nigeria have taken a bold, moral stand against Zimbabwe’s rigged presidential election, suspending the country from the Commonwealth and signaling that aid, trade and investment will flow to Africa only when good governance and democratic standards are met. By backing the New Partnership for Africa’s Development, Presidents Thabo Mbeki and Olusegun Obasanjo demonstrate that African leaders can hold each other accountable and align with international norms, reinforcing the idea that without rule of law no development assistance can succeed. Their decisive action pressures President Robert Mugabe to cease repression, stop unlawful land grabs and negotiate a genuine, free election, echoing the wishes of Zimbabwe’s opposition and striking trade unions. While some African governments have been reluctant to criticize Mugabe, Mbeki’s shift from quiet diplomacy to outspoken condemnation offers hope for a more democratic, prosperous continent and underscores the moral imperative of linking aid to democratic accountability. This stance strengthens the prospects for Africa’s renewal at the upcoming UN development conference.

END OF SUMMARY.
