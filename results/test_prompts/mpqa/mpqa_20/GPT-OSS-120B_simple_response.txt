SUMMARY:  
The article praises the “courageous” decision by South Africa’s Thabo Mbeki and Nigeria’s Olusegun Obasanjo to suspend Zimbabwe from the Commonwealth after its rigged presidential election, framing it as a moral stand for democracy and good governance. It argues that such principled action is essential for the promised “grand bargain” – increased aid, trade and investment from wealthy nations contingent on African governments respecting the rule of law. The piece commends Mbeki’s shift from “quiet diplomacy” to outspoken criticism of Robert Mugabe, urging African leaders to pressure Mugabe to cease repression, stop unlawful land seizures, and agree to a fair new election. While noting that many African regimes have been lenient toward Mugabe, the article stresses that without transparent elections no amount of aid will lift Africa out of poverty. The author portrays Mugabe’s retaliatory accusations against opposition leader Morgan Tsvangirai as desperate and indefensible, and presents the South African‑Nigerian alliance as a hopeful model for African renewal and democratic progress.  

END OF SUMMARY.
