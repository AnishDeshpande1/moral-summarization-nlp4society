SUMMARY:  
At the UN General Assembly’s Third Committee, delegations warned that poverty, hunger and poor health make it impossible for many people to truly enjoy their human rights. They framed the right to development as a moral prerequisite for universal human‑rights protection, alongside the need for international peace and security. Thailand stressed that human rights and development are “two sides of the same coin” and must put people at the centre of social and economic progress, shielding them from disease, drugs, conflict and organized crime. Eritrea warned that unchecked globalization threatens to marginalize the developing world further, while China’s Shen Guofang argued that underdevelopment cripples countries’ ability to safeguard rights. The United States linked the fight against terrorism to the protection of human rights, insisting there can be no compromise or complacency. Representatives from Cuba, Egypt, Morocco, Japan, Venezuela, Mali, Greece and Indonesia echoed these concerns. The debate underscored a shared moral conviction: narrowing development gaps and upholding human dignity are essential to achieving lasting peace and universal rights.  

END OF SUMMARY.
