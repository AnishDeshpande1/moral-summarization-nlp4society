SUMMARY:
Foreign multinationals unleashed a frantic lobbying campaign in Argentina, desperate to shield billions of dollars in profits from a government plan that would devalue the peso and end the long‑standing dollar peg. U.S. banks, Spanish telecoms, transport and energy firms flooded President Eduardo Duhalde’s office with calls, fearing losses of over $3 billion if the emergency package passed. The legislation, approved by the lower house and poised to sail through the Senate, aims to relieve an angry, heavily indebted middle class by converting dollar‑denominated loans into pesos and redirecting gas‑export revenues to prop up banks. While Argentina’s economy teeters—unable to pay pensions, workers or creditors—the foreign lobby argues that breaking contract promises would set a dangerous precedent for other nations. Critics warn the move will trigger price hikes on food, medicine and utilities, sparking further social unrest. The episode highlights the clash between profit‑driven foreign interests and a sovereign government trying to protect its citizens from a crushing debt burden.  
END OF SUMMARY.
