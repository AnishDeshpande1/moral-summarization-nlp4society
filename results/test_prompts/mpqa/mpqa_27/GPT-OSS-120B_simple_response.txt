SUMMARY:  
Foreign multinationals are frantically lobbying Argentine legislators to block President Eduardo Duhalde’s emergency devaluation package, fearing billions in losses. U.S. and Spanish banks, telecoms, transport and energy firms argue that scrapping the peso‑dollar convertibility—a promise that once guaranteed their dollar‑valued profits—will ruin their investments and set a dangerous precedent for contract renegotiation worldwide. Yet the government, pressed by an exhausted middle class drowning in dollar‑denominated debt, is pushing the package to devalue the peso, convert consumer loans into pesos and redirect gas‑export revenues to shore up banks. While foreign firms demand price‑indexing to recoup expected cost gaps, analysts warn the devaluation could spark price hikes on food, medicine and utilities, potentially sparking street unrest. The moral clash is stark: multinational lobbyists seek to protect their profits, whereas Duhalde’s administration strives to alleviate the suffering of ordinary Argentines and preserve the nation’s financial stability.  

END OF SUMMARY.
