SUMMARY:
Foreign multinationals launched an intense lobbying campaign in Argentina to stop President Eduardo Duhalde’s emergency package that would devalue the peso and end the long‑standing dollar convertibility. U.S., Spanish, and other banks, telecom, transport and energy firms warned they would lose billions and demanded the government keep the dollar‑linked pricing that has protected their profits. Duhalde, confronting a collapsed economy, a cash‑withdrawal limit and a mounting debt crisis that harms the country’s middle class, rejected the pressure and pressed ahead, arguing that maintaining the convertibility scheme would continue to punish ordinary Argentines whose debts are mostly dollar‑denominated. The legislature approved the measure, giving Duhalde sweeping powers to restructure debt, redirect gas export revenues to banks and allow a 30 % peso devaluation that will ease the burden on consumers but strip foreign firms of promised contract protections. Critics warn this subversion of established contracts threatens fairness, could set a cheating precedent for other nations, and may trigger price hikes that harm citizens further.  
END OF SUMMARY.
