SUMMARY:  
Foreign multinationals frantically lobbied Argentine lawmakers as President Eduardo Duhalde pushed an emergency package to devalue the peso and **break** a decade‑long dollar parity. Companies fear **pain** from losing billions if the **crisis** measures pass, while Argentina’s government is **broke** and unable to **pay** pensioners, state workers, or $132 billion in foreign debt. The plan would convert most dollar‑denominated consumer debt into pesos, slashing the dollar value of loans for the middle class, and redirect gas‑export revenues to shore up banks. Critics warn the devaluation could spark **violence** on the streets as prices for food, medicine and services rise. Foreign banks and Spanish‑based telecom, transport and energy firms argue that allowing the **break** of contract‑based dollar billing would set a dangerous precedent for other nations. Despite intense pressure, Duhalde’s legislation advanced through the lower house and is expected to clear the Senate, aiming to ease the **pain** of an angry middle class while confronting the nation’s fiscal **crisis**.  

END OF SUMMARY.
