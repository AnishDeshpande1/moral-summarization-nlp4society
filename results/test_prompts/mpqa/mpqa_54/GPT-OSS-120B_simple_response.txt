SUMMARY:  
The article condemns the United States’ post‑Cold‑War foreign policy as a blend of **pragmatism** and **unilateralism** that serves its own super‑power ambitions at the expense of international norms. It argues that President Bush used the National Missile Defense (NMD) program to flaunt allies, dismissing the 1972 ABM Treaty as obsolete while continuing missile tests despite a UN resolution upholding the treaty. The piece portrays the 1 April Sino‑U.S. spy‑plane collision as a stark example of U.S. double standards: the U.S. claimed the right to spy, blamed China for the crash, refused an apology until forced, then threatened retaliation on unrelated issues.  

The author further denounces Bush’s abandonment of global environmental and arms‑control treaties—the Kyoto Protocol, the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, and the Biological Weapons Convention—citing domestic oil and coal interests and a refusal to bind U.S. freedom.  

After 9/11, the U.S. framed the “war on terror” as a universal cause, demanding all nations choose “us or the terrorists,” and labeling any state that harbors militants as hostile, while selectively rewarding allies like Pakistan for strategic convenience. The overall moral judgment is that the U.S. manipulates multilateral rhetoric to legitimize unilateral actions, prioritizing its security and economic interests over global cooperation.  

END OF SUMMARY.
