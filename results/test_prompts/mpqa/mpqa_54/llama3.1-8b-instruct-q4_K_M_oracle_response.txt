SUMMARY:

The Bush administration's foreign policy is characterized by pragmatism and unilateralism, which it has consistently deployed in its diplomatic efforts. Despite international opposition, the United States went ahead with deploying the National Missile Defense System (NMD), persisting in carrying out tests even after the adoption of a resolution to maintain the Anti-Ballistic Missile Treaty. The US seeks to persuade the international community to accept the NMD and revise the ABM treaty, but its actions have triggered widespread condemnation.

The administration's treatment of China following the Sino-US plane collision event is illustrative of its pragmatism: it initially refused to apologize but later did so after being deterred by China's firm stance. The US has also adopted a unilateralist position on international treaties, refusing to enforce the Kyoto Protocol and asking for revision of other agreements that restrict its freedom.

The Bush administration views itself as a major force against terrorism, but its actions are often guided by pragmatism rather than principle. It seeks to use multilateral diplomacy to enhance its unilateralism, but its treatment of countries like Pakistan reveals a more nuanced approach.

END OF SUMMARY.
