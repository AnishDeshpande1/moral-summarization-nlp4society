SUMMARY:
The article argues that since the Cold War’s end the United States has embraced a pragmatic‑unilateral foreign policy that serves its own super‑power status at the expense of international norms. Under President Bush, the U.S. pushed ahead with the National Missile Defense system despite global and allied opposition, dismissed the 1972 Anti‑Ballistic Missile Treaty as obsolete, and continued missile‑interception tests even after a UN resolution urging compliance. The piece cites the 1 April Sino‑U.S. plane collision as an example of American double standards, noting that the U.S. blamed China, refused an apology, and later threatened retaliation over unrelated issues. It also highlights Bush’s unilateral withdrawals from or obstruction of treaties such as the Kyoto Protocol, the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, and the Biological Weapons Convention, portraying these moves as driven by domestic industry interests rather than global responsibility. After 9/11, the U.S. framed the “war on terror” as a universal cause, demanding that nations either support America or be labeled hostile, while selectively applying pragmatism—e.g., rewarding Pakistan when convenient. The moral of the story is that U.S. pragmatism often masks unilateralism, allowing America to dictate terms, ignore multilateral agreements, and pursue security and economic goals without regard for global fairness or the rights of other states.  
END OF SUMMARY.
