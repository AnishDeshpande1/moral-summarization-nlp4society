SUMMARY: The article argues that since the Cold War’s end, the United States has acted as the sole super‑power, increasingly relying on a blend of pragmatism and unilateralism in its foreign policy, especially under President Bush. It cites the controversial deployment of the National Missile Defense (NMD) system—pursued despite opposition from allies and the UN, and used to pressure Russia to amend the 1972 Anti‑Ballistic Missile Treaty. The piece highlights the U.S. handling of the April 1 Sino‑U.S. plane collision, portraying the U.S. as blaming China while later threatening retaliation over unrelated issues. It notes Bush’s refusal to honor or ratify several international treaties, such as the Kyoto Protocol, the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, and the Biological Weapons Convention, emphasizing a preference for agreements that serve U.S. security and economic interests. After 9/11, the U.S. framed the “war on terror” as a global effort, demanding universal alignment and labeling non‑cooperating states as hostile, yet pragmatically supported Pakistan to secure its anti‑terrorism objectives. Overall, the article depicts U.S. diplomacy as using multilateral rhetoric to further unilateral goals. END OF SUMMARY.
