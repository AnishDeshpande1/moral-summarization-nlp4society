SUMMARY:
The article argues that, since the Cold War’s end, the United States has embraced a pragmatic and unilateral foreign policy that subverts international authority and treats other nations as subordinate. Under President Bush, the U.S. pushed the National Missile Defense system despite global and allied opposition, dismissed the 1972 ABM Treaty as obsolete, and continued testing, showing a willingness to breach established agreements for strategic advantage—an act framed as cheating fair multilateral rules. The U.S. response to the 1 April Sino‑U.S. plane collision exemplifies this pattern: it refused responsibility, demanded apologies only under Chinese pressure, and then threatened retaliation on human‑rights, trade and Olympic issues, demonstrating contempt for fair diplomatic conduct and a readiness to harm other states to preserve dominance. Likewise, the Bush administration abandoned the Kyoto Protocol, stalled the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, and opposed the Biological Weapons Convention, prioritizing domestic oil and coal interests over global environmental and security concerns. After 9/11, the U.S. framed the “war on terror” as a universal moral crusade, demanding that all nations choose between alignment with America or being labeled hostile, thereby using multilateral rhetoric to legitimize unilateral coercion. The piece portrays these actions as a systematic abuse of power that harms other countries and undermines fairness in international relations.  
END OF SUMMARY.
