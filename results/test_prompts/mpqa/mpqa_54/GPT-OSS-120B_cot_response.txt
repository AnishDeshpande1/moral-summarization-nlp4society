**SUMMARY:**  
After the Cold War the United States emerged as the world’s **sole super power**, advancing a foreign policy defined by **pragmatism** and **unilateralism**. President Bush pushed the National Missile Defense (NMD) despite widespread **opposition** from allies, claiming it would not **attack** others but only **prevent** attacks, even threatening to **scrap** the 1972 Anti‑Ballistic Missile Treaty. The U.S. continued missile tests after the UN resolution urging respect for the treaty, illustrating its **unilateral** stance. The April 1 Sino‑U.S. plane collision highlighted this double standard: the U.S. demanded the right to spy while denying the host nation the right to defend its own airspace, refusing an **apology** until pressured, then **retaliating** with threats over **human rights**, **trade**, and the **Olympics**. Bush also **condemned** the Kyoto Protocol and the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, favoring agreements that serve U.S. **freedom**, security, and economic interests while **rejecting** those that limit them. Following 9/11, the U.S. framed the “war on **terrorism**” as a **world war**, labeling any state that **harbors** terrorists as a **hostile** **enemy** and demanding universal **support**. Pakistan was treated **pragmatically**, shifting from sanctions to aid, leaving its future **responsibility** uncertain.
