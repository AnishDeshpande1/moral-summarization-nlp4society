SUMMARY:  
The Organization of African Unity (OAU) and the Southern African Development Community (SADC) declared Zimbabwe’s March 9‑11 presidential election “free, fair, transparent and credible,” praising it as a genuine “reflection” of the people’s will and a legitimate “mandate” for President Robert Mugabe. African leaders—including Tanzania’s Benjamin Mkapa, Kenya’s Daniel ar​ap Moi and South Africa’s observer mission— hailed the vote as a defence of Zimbabwe’s “freedom” and “democracy,” warning that external interference would be a tragedy.  

Conversely, several Western governments condemned the poll as “flawed.” The United States warned of further sanctions, Britain accused Mugabe of a systematic campaign of “violence and intimidation” designed to “steal” the vote, and France, Germany and Denmark called the election not substantially free or fair, announcing cuts to aid, the possible shutdown of embassies and “toughening” of sanctions.  

The final tally gave Mugabe 56.2 % of the vote against Morgan Tsvangirai’s 41.96 %, with about 3.4 million of 5.6 million registered voters participating.  

END OF SUMMARY.
