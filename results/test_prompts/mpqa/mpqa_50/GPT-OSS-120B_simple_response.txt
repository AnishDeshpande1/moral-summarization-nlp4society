SUMMARY:  
The March 9‑11 presidential election in Zimbabwe, which returned Robert Mugabe to office with 56 % of the vote, sparked a stark moral divide. African bodies and leaders hailed the poll as a legitimate expression of the people’s will. The OAU observer team called it “transparent, credible, free and fair,” and Tanzania’s President Benjamin Mkapa lauded Mugabe as a “champion of democracy,” warning that external interference would be a tragedy. Kenya’s President Daniel arap Moi and the Southern African Development Community likewise described the result as a “true reflection” of Zimbabweans’ confidence, while South Africa deemed the process conflict‑free and legitimate.  

Conversely, Western governments condemned the vote on moral grounds. The United States labeled it “flawed” and threatened sanctions; Britain’s Foreign Secretary accused Mugabe of a systematic campaign of violence and “stealing” the vote; France, Germany and Denmark echoed concerns that the election was not substantially free or fair, with Denmark announcing aid cuts and possible embassy closure.  

Thus, the election is portrayed as a triumph of African self‑determination by regional actors, yet as a breach of democratic standards and human rights by Western critics.  

END OF SUMMARY.
