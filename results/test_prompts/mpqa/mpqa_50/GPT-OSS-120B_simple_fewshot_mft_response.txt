SUMMARY:
The Organization of African Unity and regional bodies hailed President Robert Mugabe’s March 9‑11 re‑election as a legitimate expression of Zimbabwean sovereignty, emphasizing the authority of African institutions to judge their own affairs. OAU observers called the vote “transparent, credible, free and fair,” and leaders such as Tanzania’s Benjamin Mkapa and Kenya’s Daniel ar‑ap Moi praised Mugabe as a “champion of democracy” and a testament to the people’s confidence, reinforcing respect for established authority and the rightful outcome. The Southern African Development Community likewise declared the results a true reflection of the voters’ will and urged acceptance, underscoring the moral duty to uphold the authority of the electoral process.

Conversely, Western officials framed the election as a breach of fairness, accusing the Mugabe regime of cheating and subverting democratic norms. The U.S. labeled the poll “flawed,” Britain’s foreign secretary warned of “systematic violence and intimidation” designed to secure power “at all costs,” and France, Germany, and Denmark called the vote not substantially free or fair, threatening sanctions and aid cuts. The contrasting reactions illustrate a clash between African assertions of legitimate authority and Western claims of electoral unfairness and subversive manipulation.  

END OF SUMMARY.
