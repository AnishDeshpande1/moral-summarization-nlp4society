SUMMARY:
A recent Canadian Manufacturers and Exporters study warns that implementing the 1997 Kyoto treaty would cost the nation 450,000 jobs and up to $40 billion, a figure drawn directly from government documents. Prime Minister Jean Chrétien dismisses the estimate as “frightening” and claims the facts are unclear. Yet the National Air Issues Coordinating Committee on Climate Change predicts a $40‑billion loss in 2010, roughly $1,100 per capita, for “hitting” Canada’s growth. The author argues that governments must **protect** the national economy and should deviate from that mission only for truly crucial interests, such as **fighting** **terrorism**. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change finds that rising temperatures will actually **benefit** Canadian farmers and everyday citizens, while the global benefit of Canada’s Kyoto compliance would be minimal—a 1/200th reduction in world emissions. Even if all signatories ratified the treaty, worldwide emissions would still rise, and the 8 % emission dip would cost about $2 trillion in lost GDP. Instead of trying to **cap** emissions—a costly and ineffective burden—the author urges **abating** damage in vulnerable nations through **assistance**, including **vaccinate** programs and relocation efforts, which would cost a fraction of Kyoto’s price tag.  
END OF SUMMARY.
