SUMMARY:

A recent study by the Canadian Manufacturers and Exporters found that implementing the Kyoto global warming treaty would cost Canada's economy 450,000 jobs and up to $40-billion. However, Prime Minister Jean Chretien disputes these figures, saying they are exaggerated. The study, however, is based on government documents from 2000, which estimated a loss of approximately $40-billion in annual economic output by 2010.

The article argues that the costs of implementing Kyoto outweigh its benefits. Rising temperatures pose little threat to Canada's economy and environment, but would require significant sacrifices from Canadians. Meanwhile, the expected reduction in global greenhouse gas emissions is minimal, with the treaty only accounting for about 1/200th of the world's projected output.

The article concludes that instead of trying to cap carbon dioxide emissions, Western countries should focus on helping at-risk nations mitigate the effects of global warming, which would be more cost-effective and beneficial. This approach could include providing assistance for new crops, vaccinations, and coastal town relocation.

END OF SUMMARY.
