SUMMARY:  
The article argues that Canada should reject the Kyoto Protocol on moral and economic grounds. It cites a Canadian manufacturers’ study claiming Kyoto would cost 450,000 jobs and $40 billion, figures the author says are taken from official reports. The author frames the government’s duty to protect the national economy as a moral obligation, suggesting that only existential threats (e.g., terrorism) justify sacrificing growth. He asserts that climate change poses little danger to Canada—farmers may even benefit, and everyday Canadians would enjoy milder winters. Moreover, Canada’s $40 billion contribution would shrink global emissions by a negligible 1/200th of the world total, while the treaty would barely curb worldwide carbon growth (26 % vs. 34 % by 2010). The moral alternative, the author contends, is to spend the money on direct aid to vulnerable developing nations—helping with agriculture, disease control, and relocation—rather than imposing costly domestic caps that have little global effect. In this view, protecting Canadian prosperity is the ethical priority, and humanitarian assistance abroad is a more justifiable use of funds.  
END OF SUMMARY.
