SUMMARY:  
Robert Mugabe was declared the winner of Zimbabwe’s fifth presidential term in an election widely condemned as rigged, marked by violence, intimidation and manipulation of electoral laws. The official result gave Mugabe 56 % of the vote versus Morgan Tsvangirai’s 41 %, a victory that Tsvangirai called “the biggest electoral fraud” and “daylight robbery.” The West, led by George Bush and Colin Powell, denounced the flawed poll and warned of new sanctions, while Britain and Canada hesitated, awaiting a Commonwealth observer report. In stark contrast, African neighbours, including South Africa’s observer team, praised the win as legitimate, blaming low turnout in Harare on administrative oversight. Domestic observers and the Crisis in Zimbabwe Committee labeled the election fraud‑tainted, urging peaceful protest despite security laws that forbid strikes. Mugabe’s forces have taken the streets, setting roadblocks, keeping the military on alert, and ripping up opposition newspapers. Opposition supporters, threatened and prevented from campaigning, remain angry but reject rebellion, while the country grapples with an imploded economy, soaring unemployment, hyper‑inflation and hungry citizens fearing war and turmoil.  
END OF SUMMARY.
