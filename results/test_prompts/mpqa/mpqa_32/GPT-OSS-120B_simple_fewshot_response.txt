SUMMARY:
Robert Mugabe was declared the winner of Zimbabwe’s fifth‑term presidential election, a result hailed by his Justice Minister as a “runaway victory” but denounced by opposition leader Morgan Tsvangirai as “the biggest electoral fraud” he has ever seen. Western leaders – President George Bush, Secretary of State Colin Powell and Britain’s Foreign Secretary Jack Straw – condemned the vote and warned of sanctions, while Canada’s Prime Minister Jean Chrétien adopted a cautious stance pending a Commonwealth report. In stark contrast, African observers, including a South African delegation and the Organization of African Unity, called the election “transparent, credible, free and fair,” attributing irregularities to mere administrative oversights. The opposition, facing intimidation and low urban turnout, remains fearful and disorganized, rejecting violent rebellion but calling for peaceful protest. Meanwhile, Zimbabwe’s dire economy and massive unemployment fuel regional concerns, especially for South Africa, which prioritizes stability over democratic standards. The article frames the episode as a moral clash: the West exposing a rigged election and demanding accountability, while African actors downplay flaws to preserve regional order and avoid refugee flows.

END OF SUMMARY.
