SUMMARY:  
The election that returned Robert Mugabe to a fifth term in Zimbabwe has exposed a stark moral divide. Western leaders—President George Bush, Colin Powell and Britain’s Jack Straw—condemned the vote as a “systematic campaign of violence” and “the biggest electoral fraud,” refusing to recognise the result and threatening further sanctions. Canada’s Jean Chretien, while more cautious, warned that action would follow an observer report, prompting criticism from opposition allies.  

In sharp contrast, African neighbours, led by South Africa’s Sam Motsuenyane, declared the poll “legitimate” and praised its administration, while the OAU observers called it “transparent, credible, free and fair.” This regional endorsement reflects a pragmatic moral stance prioritising stability over democratic ideals, especially given Zimbabwe’s collapsed economy, soaring unemployment and the risk of refugee flows into South Africa.  

Opposition leader Morgan Tsvangirai denounced the outcome as a rigged “daylight robbery,” yet he ruled out armed rebellion, urging peaceful, constitutionally‑acceptable protest despite intimidation, arrests and a militarised security response. The country remains on edge, caught between Western calls for accountability and African acceptance of a contested, yet politically expedient, status‑quo.  

END OF SUMMARY.
