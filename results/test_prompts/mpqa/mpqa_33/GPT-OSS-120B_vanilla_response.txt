SUMMARY: The U.S. State Department’s 2001 Annual Human Rights Report has drawn widespread international condemnation, with critics labeling it inaccurate, biased, and an intrusion into other nations’ affairs. Nations across Asia, Europe, Africa, and Latin America have rejected its findings, accusing the U.S. of politicizing human‑rights assessments to serve its own interests. A Chinese parliamentary report highlighted alleged U.S. abuses, citing high murder rates, instances of torture, wrongful death sentences, child poverty, and domestic violence. Analysts note the report’s emphasis on countries like North Korea and Iraq while overlooking U.S. violations, suggesting evaluations depend on political relations. Reuters pointed out inconsistencies, such as the omission of criticism toward Egypt’s military courts when the U.S. itself plans similar tribunals for al‑Qaida and Taliban detainees. Domestic voices, including a Washington Times op‑ed, defended Vietnam’s religious freedoms and disputed the report’s claims. Overall, the 2001 U.S. human‑rights report is being portrayed as fabricated, selective, and driven by geopolitical motives.  
END OF SUMMARY.
