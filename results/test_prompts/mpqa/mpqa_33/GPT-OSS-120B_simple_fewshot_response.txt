SUMMARY:
The U.S. State Department’s 2001 Annual Human Rights Report has drawn widespread condemnation as a “brazen distortion” and an illegitimate intrusion into other nations’ affairs. Governments across Asia, Europe, Africa and Latin America, as well as international media, have denounced the report’s inaccuracies and its selective focus on countries that suit U.S. political interests. A Chinese parliamentary study highlighted stark domestic abuses—high murder rates, routine torture and coerced confessions, wrongful death‑penalty convictions, over 12 million children living in poverty, and a woman beaten every 15 seconds—arguing that the United States ignores its own grave human‑rights failures while publishing critiques of others. Analysts note that the U.S. report emphasizes allies such as North Korea and Iraq while omitting criticism of its own use of military courts for terrorism suspects, revealing a double standard driven by strategic goals. Even within the United States, voices like Andrew Welsdan of the U.S. Fund for Reconciliation and Development dispute the report’s claims about Vietnam, insisting on religious freedom there. Overall, the report is portrayed as inaccurate, fabricated, and motivated by political manipulation rather than genuine concern for human rights.  
END OF SUMMARY.
