SUMMARY: The U.S. State Department’s 2001 Annual Human Rights Report has been widely denounced as a dishonest, politically‑driven document that distorts reality and interferes in other nations’ affairs. Nations across Asia, Europe, Africa and Latin America have called the report a “brazen distortion” and an illegitimate intrusion. A Chinese parliamentary study highlighted severe U.S. abuses—high murder rates, routine torture and coerced confessions, wrongful death‑penalty convictions, over 12 million children living in poverty, and a woman beaten every 15 seconds—arguing that these violations have been ignored for years while the U.S. critiques others. Analysts note that the U.S. selectively criticizes countries based on strategic interests, praising allies and omitting criticism when convenient (e.g., the shift on Egypt’s military courts). The report’s focus on North Korea and Iraq further exposes its bias. Domestic voices, such as Andrew Welsdan of the U.S. Fund for Reconciliation and Development, dispute the report’s claims about Vietnam, emphasizing religious freedom there. Overall, the publication is portrayed as inaccurate, fabricated, and morally compromised, prompting global protest.  
END OF SUMMARY.
