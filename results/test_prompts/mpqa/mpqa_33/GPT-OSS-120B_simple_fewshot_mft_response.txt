SUMMARY:
The U.S. State Department’s 2001 Annual Human‑Rights Report has been denounced worldwide as a politically motivated distortion that subverts the authority of other nations while ignoring America’s own abuses. Critics from Asia, Europe, Africa and Latin America accuse the United States of using its annual “human‑rights” assessments as a tool of power, praising allies and condemning rivals only when it serves U.S. interests. The Chinese People’s Congress released a counter‑report documenting severe harm inside the United States: a murder rate of 5.5 per 100,000, routine torture and coerced confessions, wrongful death‑penalty convictions, over 12 million children living in poverty, and a woman beaten every 15 seconds. These figures illustrate systemic care‑related violations and a justice system that cheats ordinary citizens of fairness. Media outlets such as Reuters note the inconsistency of U.S. criticism—Egypt was singled out in 2000 but omitted in 2001 when the U.S. planned to try al‑Qaida and Taliban detainees in military courts. Even American voices, like Andrew Welsdan of the U.S. Fund for Reconciliation and Development, dispute the report’s claims about Vietnam, underscoring the report’s lack of credibility. The global outcry frames the U.S. report as an illegitimate, self‑serving exercise of authority that harms vulnerable peoples and cheats the standards of genuine human‑rights fairness. 
END OF SUMMARY.
