SUMMARY: The Pakistani government rejected a U.S. State Department human‑rights report as “factually inaccurate and unwarranted,” accusing it of unfair judgment. In a foreign office statement, officials highlighted numerous reforms and initiatives, including the Juvenile Justice System Ordinance, a permanent commission on the status of women, police and jail reforms, a national plan to eliminate child labour, a fund for rehabilitating working children, amendment of the 1951 Citizenship Act to allow women to pass nationality to children of foreign husbands, and a mass awareness campaign marking the year 2000 as the Year of Human Rights and Human Dignity. Pakistan also cited ratification of ILO Conventions 100 and 182, signing of the Optional Protocols to the Convention on the Rights of the Child, and the introduction of joint electorates for minorities. Despite these points, the U.S. report concluded that Pakistan’s human‑rights record remained poor. The Pakistani government called the assessment a gross misstatement and urged states to cooperate rather than judge each other. END OF SUMMARY.
