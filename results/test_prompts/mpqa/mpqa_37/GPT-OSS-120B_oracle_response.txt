SUMMARY:
The Pakistani government rejected the U.S. State Department’s human‑rights report as “factually inaccurate and unwarranted,” arguing that the assessment fails to acknowledge the country’s extensive measures and initiatives to promote and protect human rights. It listed achievements such as the promulgation of the Juvenile Justice System Ordinance, the establishment of a permanent commission on the status of women, police and jail reforms, a national plan for the elimination of child labour, and a fund for the rehabilitation of working children. The government also noted the amendment of the 1951 Citizenship Act to enable women of Pakistani descent to claim nationality for children born to foreign husbands, the ratification of ILO Conventions 100 and 182, and the signing of optional protocols to the Convention on the Rights of the Child. A mass awareness campaign marked the Year 2000 as the year of Human Rights and Human Dignity, and a system of joint electorates was introduced respecting minority wishes. Despite these developments, the report claimed Pakistan’s human‑rights record remained poor, a misstatement the government rejects, calling for cooperation rather than judgment among states in promoting and protecting human rights. END OF SUMMARY.
