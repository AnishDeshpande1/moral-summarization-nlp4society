SUMMARY:
Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld fiercely defended the United States’ treatment of the 158 detainees at Guantanamo Bay, dismissing worldwide outrage as “breathless arm‑chair hyperbole.” He claimed, after “secure video” inspections, that no evidence showed inhumane conduct and argued that restraints were a necessary, humane safeguard against suicide or attacks during transport. Rumsfeld portrayed the detainees as dangerous terrorists whose primary purpose was to be interrogated for intelligence, insisting that labeling them prisoners of war would set a dangerous legal precedent for terrorist groups.  

In stark moral contrast, international bodies and human‑rights groups condemned the practices as humiliating, psychologically abusive, and potentially illegal under the Geneva Conventions. The International Committee of the Red Cross warned that publishing the photos created a “spectacle” of prisoners; Amnesty International denounced sensory deprivation, shackling, and forced shaving as classic tactics to break spirits. The EU, the Netherlands, and several governments demanded POW status to uphold “our norms and values,” while Britain and Spain defended the U.S. stance. Legal scholars highlighted the detainees’ “legal limbo” and called for swift classification. The moral debate thus framed the U.S. actions as a necessary fight against terror versus a violation of humanitarian principles.  
END OF SUMMARY.
