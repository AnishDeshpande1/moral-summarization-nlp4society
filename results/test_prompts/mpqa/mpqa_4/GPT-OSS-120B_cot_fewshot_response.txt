**SUMMARY:**
Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld defended the United States’ treatment of Guantanamo detainees, dismissing mounting international **criticism** as “breathless arm‑chair **hyperbole**.” He claimed no evidence of mistreatment and insisted that restraints were necessary for safety, arguing that locking prisoners during transport is not **inhumane**. Critics, including the Red Cross and Amnesty International, highlighted **sensory** **deprivation**, **humiliation**, **restraint**, **incommunicado** detention, and tactics that create **isolation**, **domination**, and **futility**, warning they breach Geneva Convention norms. European officials urged granting **legal** **prisoner‑of‑war** status, calling the current **limbo** a threat to shared **values**. While Britain and Spain backed the U.S., the U.S. maintained that detainees are “committed **terrorists**” whose primary purpose is intelligence extraction, not formal **legal** classification. A federal judge questioned his own jurisdiction over a challenge to the detainees’ status.
