SUMMARY:

Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld defended the US treatment of detainees in Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, amidst international outcry over photographs showing prisoners in restraints. Rumsfeld claimed that the prisoners were treated humanely and that the photographs were taken just before their restraints were removed. Critics, including Amnesty International and several governments, accused the US of using sensory deprivation and psychological control techniques to weaken the prisoners.

The US has not officially designated the detainees as prisoners of war, despite being urged by international organizations and countries to do so. Rumsfeld argued that the Geneva Conventions give protection only to prisoners from countries, not terrorist organizations like Al Qaeda, which some of the prisoners belong to. The legal status of the detainees remains uncertain, with a federal judge in Los Angeles questioning his authority to consider a challenge to their detention.

The debate highlights the moral implications of treating individuals as enemy combatants rather than prisoners of war, raising questions about the US commitment to upholding human rights and international law.
