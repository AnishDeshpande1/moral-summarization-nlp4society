SUMMARY:  
Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld defended the United States’ treatment of the 158 detainees at Guantanamo Bay, insisting that no evidence showed they were anything other than treated humanely. He attempted to “tap” down the international “hyper‑bole” after photographs were “distributed” and “published” showing prisoners kneeling, shackled, masked and blind‑folded. Critics argued the images created a “spectacle” that violated Geneva Convention norms and that the “use” of “humiliation” tactics – including “shaving,” sensory deprivation and “restraining” – amounted to classic “atrocities.” European leaders warned that “changing” our values would be a terrorist “victory,” urging the U.S. to “uphold” its norms by granting prisoner‑of‑war status. Rumsfeld said the “control” exercised was merely “imposing” “positive control” to “establish” security, arguing restraints were necessary to prevent detainees from “killing” Americans during transport. He noted the U.S. had not yet “designated” the captives as POWs and that “determining” their status was delayed, while the Pentagon’s priority remained to “prevent” future “attacks.” Amnesty International and the Red Cross warned that “keeping” prisoners incommunicado and the “use” of psychological pressure could breach international law, calling for lawful “interrogation” and legal review.  
END OF SUMMARY.
