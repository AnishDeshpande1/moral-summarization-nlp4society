**SUMMARY:**  
Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld defended the United States’ treatment of Guantanamo detainees, dismissing international **criticism** as “breathless armchair **hyperbole**.” He insisted that no evidence suggested anyone had been treated anything other than **humane**, despite photographs showing prisoners in **restraint**, **sensory** **deprivation**, **humiliation**, and **shame**‑inducing conditions. Rumsfeld argued that **restraint** during transport is necessary, not **inhumane**, to prevent attacks, and that the detainees—labeled **“terrorists”**—are in **legal limbo** rather than granted **“prisoner of war”** status. Critics, including the Red Cross and Amnesty International, warned that such tactics amount to **domination**, **isolation**, **futility**, and a breach of **fairness** under the Geneva Conventions. European leaders called the practice a threat to shared **values**, while Britain and Spain voiced support for the U.S. stance. A federal judge expressed **grave doubts** about his jurisdiction to hear a challenge to the detentions. Rumsfeld concluded that the priority remains extracting intelligence, not granting legal protections.
