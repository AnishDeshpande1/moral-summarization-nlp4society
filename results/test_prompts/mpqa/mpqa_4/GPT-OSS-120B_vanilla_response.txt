SUMMARY: Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld defended the U.S. treatment of 158 detainees at Guantanamo Bay, rejecting international criticism as “hyperbole.” He claimed no evidence of inhumane treatment despite released photos showing prisoners shackled, blindfolded and masked. Critics—including the International Committee of the Red Cross, Amnesty International, the EU, the Netherlands, and several governments—accused the U.S. of violating Geneva Convention rules, using sensory deprivation, and denying legal rights. Britain and Spain defended the U.S. stance. Camp X‑Ray warden Col. Terry Carrico emphasized “positive control,” while a retired Army general described the tactics as psychological isolation. Rumsfeld said restraints were necessary for safety and that the detainees were “committed terrorists” whose primary purpose was intelligence gathering. He admitted uncertainty about legal classifications, noting that Geneva protections apply to state actors, not terrorist groups, and that detainees’ varied nationalities complicate matters. Legal scholars and a federal judge highlighted the “legal limbo” and questioned jurisdiction over detainee rights. The Pentagon has not designated the captives as prisoners of war, nor allowed lawyers or interrogations, prompting calls for judicial review. END OF SUMMARY.
