SUMMARY:

Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld defended the treatment of detainees at Guantanamo Bay, Cuba, amidst international criticism. Critics accuse the US of using sensory deprivation and psychological control techniques to weaken prisoners for interrogation. Amnesty International and several governments called for the prisoners to be granted prisoner-of-war status under the Geneva Convention, which would accord them higher protections.

Rumsfeld dismissed the criticism as "breathless armchair hyperbole" and claimed that the prisoners are being treated humanely. He argued that restraining prisoners during transport is necessary to prevent escape attempts. The US has not officially designated the detainees as prisoners of war, despite Rumsfeld's statement that they are being treated as such.

Criticism from abroad continues to grow, with several governments calling for the US to recognize the detainees' rights under the Geneva Convention. A federal judge in Los Angeles questioned his authority to consider a challenge to the detention of the prisoners in Cuba. The controversy highlights the complex and contentious issue of how to treat enemy combatants captured during the war on terror.

END OF SUMMARY.
