SUMMARY:
Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld defended the United States’ treatment of 158 Guantánamo detainees, dismissing international outcry as “arm‑chair hyperbole.” He claimed no evidence of inhumane conduct, arguing that restraints during transport are necessary for security and not a violation of dignity. Yet the released photographs—showing prisoners shackled, blindfolded, gagged and humiliated—prompted accusations of sensory deprivation, psychological abuse and a degrading spectacle that may breach the Geneva Conventions. Human‑rights groups (Amnesty International, the Red Cross) and several governments demanded fair treatment, legal status as prisoners of war, and access to counsel, invoking care for the detainees’ wellbeing and fairness in applying humanitarian law. Allies Britain and Spain defended the U.S. stance, while the EU and the Netherlands warned that abandoning moral standards would reward terrorism. Critics highlighted the moral degradation of treating captives as “legal limbo” and the subversion of established authority by ignoring established norms. The debate underscores a clash between national security authority and the ethical imperatives of preventing harm, ensuring fairness, and preserving human dignity.  

END OF SUMMARY.
