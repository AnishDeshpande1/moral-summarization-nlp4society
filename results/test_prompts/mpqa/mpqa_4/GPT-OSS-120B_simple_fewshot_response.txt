SUMMARY:
Secretary of Defense Donald Rumsfeld spent an hour defending the United States’ treatment of the 158 detainees at Guantanamo Bay after international outrage over disturbing photographs showing prisoners in shackles, masks and sensory‑deprivation gear. Rumsfeld dismissed the criticism as “arm‑chair hyperbole,” insisting no evidence of inhumane treatment had been found and arguing that restraints are necessary for security when moving “dangerous, suicidal” terrorists. Human‑rights groups and the Red Cross condemned the images as potentially violating Geneva Convention rules, calling the tactics “classic methods to break the spirit” of detainees and demanding legal status and access to counsel. European leaders urged Washington to grant prisoner‑of‑war protections, while Britain and Spain expressed support for the U.S. stance. Legal experts highlighted the “legal limbo” of the captives and the need for a clear determination of their status, noting the difficulty of applying Geneva protections to non‑state terrorist actors. A federal judge questioned his own jurisdiction over a challenge to the detentions, underscoring the unresolved moral and legal controversy surrounding the base’s secretive practices.

END OF SUMMARY.
