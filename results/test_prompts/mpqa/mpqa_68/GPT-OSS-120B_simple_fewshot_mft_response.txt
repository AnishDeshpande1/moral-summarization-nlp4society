SUMMARY:
Japanese Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi and U.S. President George W. Bush clashed over who should set the rules for combating global warming, underscoring a struggle over authority. Bush reaffirmed his rejection of the 1997 Kyoto Protocol, arguing that mandatory limits would undermine U.S. economic leadership, and promoted a new voluntary‑limits plan that places decision‑making power in the hands of American companies. Koizumi, invoking Japan’s commitment to the Kyoto framework, praised the U.S. proposal as “constructive” only if Washington shows greater willingness to follow established international standards, urging the United States not to subvert the global agreement. Both leaders agreed to cooperate on technology development and to work with other nations, but the dialogue highlighted a clear contest between U.S. sovereign authority and the collective authority embodied in the Kyoto Protocol. The exchange frames the U.S. stance as a potential subversion of agreed‑upon global governance, while Japan appeals to the legitimacy of existing international authority. END OF SUMMARY.
