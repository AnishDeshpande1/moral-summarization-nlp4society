SUMMARY:  
Canadian editorialists used the recent political shake‑up and the Guantanamo detainee issue to weigh moral responsibilities. The Ottawa Citizen, the Sudbury Star and other papers argued that even “thugs” deserve humane treatment and that Canada must press the United States to grant a competent tribunal under the Geneva Conventions, stressing that “laws keep us from behaving like them.” In contrast, the Timmins Daily Press defended harsh U.S. tactics as a matter of security, warning that “civil‑rights advocates” could be aiding Osama bin Laden.  

The same moral lens was turned on former cabinet minister Brian Tobin, whose abrupt exit provoked scathing condemnation. Commentators labelled his departure a “third consecutive betrayal of voters,” mocked his family‑leave excuse, and described him as “flighty, opportunistic, unstable” and unfit for public trust. By contrast, John Manley was praised as a “sensible, serious” and “competent and scrupulously honest” leader whose new deputy‑prime‑minister role promises responsible stewardship of national security.  

Thus, the editorials juxtapose a call for legal‑moral standards abroad with harsh moral judgement of domestic politicians, rewarding those deemed ethical and condemning those seen as self‑serving.  

END OF SUMMARY.
