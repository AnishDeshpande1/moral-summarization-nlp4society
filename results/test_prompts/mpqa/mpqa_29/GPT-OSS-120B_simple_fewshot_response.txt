SUMMARY:
The Ottawa press corps spent a week dissecting two very different moral questions. On the one hand, editorials warned that Canada must not abandon the rule of law in demanding a competent tribunal for the al‑Qaida detainees at Guantanamo Bay, arguing that even the worst enemies deserve humane treatment and that “laws keep us from behaving like them.” The Ottawa Citizen and the Sudbury Star called for a UN‑run tribunal, while the Timmins Daily Press warned that soft‑handing the prisoners could endanger American lives, framing the issue as a clash between compassion and security.

On the other hand, the same papers unleashed scathing moral condemnation of former minister Brian Tobin’s abrupt exit from politics. Columnists portrayed his departure as a betrayal of voters, a flighty cash‑grab, and an opportunistic abandonment of public duty, using sarcasm and vivid imagery to underscore his alleged lack of commitment. By contrast, John Manley, newly elevated to Deputy Prime Minister, was lauded as a “sensible, serious, honest” manager whose steady hand on national‑security matters promises stability for Canada.

Thus, the moral narrative praises adherence to legal principles and steady leadership while castigating political opportunism and abandonment of public responsibility. END OF SUMMARY.
