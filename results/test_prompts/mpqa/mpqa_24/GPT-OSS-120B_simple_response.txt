SUMMARY:
The article frames NASA’s next chapter as a moral test of responsibility and credibility. After a decade under Daniel Goldin—who championed “faster, better, cheaper” and expanded unmanned missions—NASA now faces a credibility crisis over the runaway costs of the International Space Station, which have swelled from $17.4 billion to over $30 billion. President Bush’s nomination of Sean O’Keefe, a seasoned budget‑official rather than a space enthusiast, is presented as an ethical pivot: the agency must first “clean up its act” by reigning in expenses before it can pursue grand visions again. O’Keefe’s financial expertise is portrayed as the necessary virtue to restore fiscal discipline, negotiate with international partners, and rescue the station’s future. The piece suggests that, like past “numbers men” such as James Webb, O’Keefe could steer NASA back to trustworthy stewardship, implying that fiscal prudence is the moral prerequisite for any future exploration ambition.  

END OF SUMMARY.
