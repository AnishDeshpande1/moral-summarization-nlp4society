SUMMARY:

NASA's space program is facing a credibility crisis, with soaring costs and a dysfunctional management structure. Administrator Daniel Goldin's resignation marks the end of an era, and President Bush has nominated Sean O'Keefe to take his place. O'Keefe, a seasoned bean counter, may be what the agency needs to restore its credibility and get back on track. The space station project, in particular, is a disaster waiting to happen, with costs ballooning far beyond original estimates from $17.4 billion to over $30 billion.

The task-force report has sparked opposition from international partners, who argue that scaling back the station's scope would violate agreements and limit its use. To fix the problem, O'Keefe will need not only accounting savvy but also deft diplomacy. His appointment sends a clear message: NASA must clean up its act before it can pursue new and exciting projects.

The agency's objectives have become less visionary since its Apollo-program heyday, when numbers men like James Webb and Richard Truly led the way. O'Keefe's mandate is to fix the space station, but some analysts hope that under his leadership, NASA can once again become a beacon of innovation and progress.

END OF SUMMARY.
