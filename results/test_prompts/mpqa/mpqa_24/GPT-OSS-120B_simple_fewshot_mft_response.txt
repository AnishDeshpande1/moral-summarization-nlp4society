SUMMARY:
NASA’s long‑time administrator Daniel Goldin has stepped down after a decade, and President Bush has tapped Sean O’Keefe—a seasoned budget chief and former Pentagon CFO—to take the helm. The appointment signals a clear subversion of the agency’s traditional “space‑cadet” culture in favor of strict fiscal authority. Goldin, once praised for expanding unmanned missions and a “faster‑better‑cheaper” ethos, presided over a soaring International Space Station budget that ballooned from $17.4 billion to over $30 billion, prompting questions about NASA’s credibility. O’Keefe, described as a “bean counter” rather than a space enthusiast, is being brought in to impose financial discipline, trim the station’s scope, and restore governmental control over a program many view as dysfunctional. Critics warn that scaling back the ISS could breach international agreements, but supporters argue that authoritative budget oversight is essential before NASA can pursue any visionary projects again. O’Keefe’s nomination thus frames the agency’s future as a battle between entrenched aerospace ambitions and the need for top‑down fiscal authority.

END OF SUMMARY.
