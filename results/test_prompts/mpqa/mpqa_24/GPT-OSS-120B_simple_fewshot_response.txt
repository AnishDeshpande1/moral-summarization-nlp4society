SUMMARY:
NASA’s next chapter may be defined not by daring rockets but by tough financial discipline. After a decade‑long tenure, administrator Daniel Goldin stepped down, and President Bush nominated Sean O’Keefe—a seasoned budget chief and former Pentagon CFO—to steer the agency back to fiscal credibility. Goldin’s “faster, better, cheaper” era expanded unmanned missions, yet the agency’s reputation has been bruised by runaway costs on the International Space Station, which has ballooned from $17.4 billion to over $30 billion. A recent task‑force report warned that NASA’s own cost estimates for completing the “core” station are unrealistic, sparking diplomatic friction with partners like Canada. O’Keefe’s appointment signals a clear moral message: before NASA can chase bold new ventures, it must first clean up its accounting, restore trust, and respect international commitments. If he can balance the budget and negotiate the station’s scope, the agency may regain the credibility needed for future visionary projects.

END OF SUMMARY.
