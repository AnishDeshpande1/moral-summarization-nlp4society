SUMMARY:  
NASA is at a crossroads as longtime administrator Daniel Goldin departs after a record ten‑year tenure. President Bush has tapped Sean O’Keefe, a seasoned budget chief, to replace the “space cadet” with a “bean counter” in hopes of steering the agency away from the **ballooned** costs of the International Space Station and restoring its credibility. Goldin’s era saw a surge in unmanned missions and a “faster, better, cheaper” philosophy, but the manned program has been plagued by a **disaster**‑like cost escalation—from $17.4 billion to over $30 billion—prompting calls to **clean** up the budget and scale back the station’s scope. O’Keefe will inherit a fiscal morass that demands both accounting skill and diplomatic finesse, especially as partners fear that trimming the station could breach international agreements. Analysts compare his challenge to past “numbers guys” like James Webb, noting that each era’s mandate has shifted: from moon‑landing ambition to post‑Challenger recovery, to budget cuts, and now to fixing the space station. The appointment signals a clear message: NASA must **clean** its act before pursuing the “neat stuff” of future exploration.  
END OF SUMMARY.
