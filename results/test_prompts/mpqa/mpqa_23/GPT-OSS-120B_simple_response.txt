SUMMARY:  
President Robert Mugabe was sworn in for a fifth term in a ceremony marked by triumphalist rhetoric against “imperialism” and “the West.” While African heads of state attended, 57 opposition MPs and representatives of the United States, EU, New Zealand and Canada boycotted, decrying a vote marred by political violence, last‑minute legal changes and the disenfranchisement of tens of thousands of voters in Harare. Mugabe, a former rebel who led Zimbabwe to independence, portrayed his victory as a moral strike against colonial powers and pledged to accelerate land‑reform, seizing white‑owned farms for landless blacks. The government’s actions have already prompted donor cut‑backs, deepening recession and food shortages. Regional leaders such as South Africa’s Thabo Mbeki and Nigeria’s Olusegun Obasanjo are urging Mugabe to consider a unity government to stave off further sanctions and Commonwealth suspension, while Western observers await a clear denunciation of the disputed election. The episode underscores a clash between Mugabe’s anti‑colonial moral narrative and widespread accusations of electoral fraud and authoritarian rule.  

END OF SUMMARY.
