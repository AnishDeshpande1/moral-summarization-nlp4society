SUMMARY:
Zimbabwe’s president Robert Mugabe was sworn in for a fifth term in a ceremony attended by a handful of African heads of state, while the entire opposition bloc and Western governments boycotted it in protest of a vote widely denounced as rigged, violent and marred by voter disenfranchisement. Mugabe framed his victory as a triumph over “imperialism” and “whites,” using the occasion to boast about his land‑reform seizures from white farmers and to blame criticism on racism, even invoking Britain’s Tony Blair. The moral indictment is clear: a leader who clings to power through intimidation, electoral fraud and reckless expropriation, while the nation suffers a deep recession, food shortages and international sanctions. Opposition parties, the United States, the EU, New Zealand and Canada condemned the process, and regional powers such as South Africa and Nigeria urged Mugabe toward a national‑unity government to ease economic collapse and restore democratic legitimacy. The episode underscores the ethical cost of abandoning fair elections, the rule of law and respect for all citizens’ rights in favor of personal and partisan power.

END OF SUMMARY.
