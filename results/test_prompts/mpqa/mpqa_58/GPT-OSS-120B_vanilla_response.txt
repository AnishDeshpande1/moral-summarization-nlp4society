SUMMARY:  
Russia is set to discuss ratifying the Kyoto Protocol, an international treaty aimed at cutting greenhouse‑gas emissions. A recent expert panel, convened by the Foundation for Integrated Applied Research, debated Russia’s role in global climate strategy. Some scientists, invoking early Soviet work by Mikhail Budyko, argued that warming could turn permafrost into fertile land, suggesting Russia could profit by selling excess emission “quotas” and benefiting from higher gas demand. Others, like physicist Sergey Kapitsa, warned that climate models are uncertain and emphasized the need for paleoclimatology insights. The panel highlighted that while Russia’s emissions have fallen below 1990 levels, making its Kyoto obligations modest, the country could become a major “hot‑air” seller under the protocol’s market mechanism. Foreign Ministry adviser Nikolay Ratsiborinskiy cautioned that relying on quota trading alone would hinder technological development and EU integration, urging full ratification and participation in broader conservation efforts. Background information outlined the Kyoto Protocol’s origins, Russia’s emissions rank, and potential economic gains from carbon‑credit sales, while also noting the unpredictable regional impacts of climate change on Russian infrastructure and ecosystems.  

END OF SUMMARY.
