SUMMARY:
Tomorrow the Russian government will decide whether to ratify the Kyoto Protocol, an international treaty that aims to reduce greenhouse‑gas emissions and to solve the global climate threat. EU nations announced their intentions to join the ratification, and scientists have tried to define Russia’s role in a worldwide climatic strategy. Experts argue that Russia could control a large share of emission quotas, gaining economic benefit by selling “hot air” while complying with the protocol’s limits. Others, like Sergey Kapitsa, warn that the warming trend is uncertain and that relying on models alone is unwise; they stress the need to learn from paleoclimate data and to invest in nature‑conservation technologies rather than merely buying foreign quotas. The Ministry of Foreign Affairs described the Kyoto mechanism as costly but essential, recommending that Russia move to ratify and integrate its efforts with the EU, thereby providing an impetus for rational development. The treaty assigns each country specific reduction targets, requires monitoring and inventorying of emissions, and creates a market for trading surplus allowances. If Russia does not behave responsibly, the protocol could die, and the nation would miss the chance to protect its environment while contributing to global stabilization. END OF SUMMARY.
