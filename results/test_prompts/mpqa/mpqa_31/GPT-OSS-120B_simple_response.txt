SUMMARY:  
The article portrays Zimbabwe’s first contested presidential election as a moral test of courage and patience for its citizens. Martha, a ZANU‑PF official who secretly backs MDC candidate Morgan Tsvangirai, embodies the hidden dissent of many who fear repression yet yearn for food, health care and education. Voters like Janet and Colin endure hours‑long, sun‑baked queues, while the ailing Edward Murchabaiw wheels himself to the polls, believing his vote matters even as he faces death. Despite massive intimidation—reduced urban polling stations, last‑minute legal changes, police tear‑gas, and outright ballot‑rigging—the populace persists, driven by a deep faith in democracy. Observers and MDC activists report systematic fraud: displaced rural agents, forced voting by ZANU‑PF officials, and media bans that cripple independent voter education. Yet stories of ordinary people risking hunger, injury, and even their lives to cast a ballot highlight a collective moral resolve that the authoritarian regime cannot easily crush. The hopeful tone suggests that the sheer number of determined voters may finally tip the balance against Mugabe’s rule.  

END OF SUMMARY.
