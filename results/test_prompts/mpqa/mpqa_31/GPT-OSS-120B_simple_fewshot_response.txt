SUMMARY:
Zimbabwe’s first contested presidential election in 22 years turned into a stark test of citizens’ patience and courage. Ordinary people like Martha—a ZANU‑PF official who secretly backs the MDC—risked intimidation to vote for a better life, while others, such as Janet and Colin, endured 27‑hour and 12‑hour lines in scorching heat, believing their ballots could end Mugabe’s rule. The ruling party deliberately skewed the process: urban polling stations were scarce, rural stations plentiful, opposition agents were harassed or expelled, and state media silence crippled voter education. Police resorted to tear‑gas and rubber bullets when crowds grew restless. Yet, despite these abuses, voters like Edward, dying of AIDS, and Mary, coerced by party officials, still cast their votes, clinging to the moral belief that participation matters. International monitors warned that pre‑election cheating had already doomed a free outcome, but the sheer turnout and stories of personal sacrifice underscore a lingering hope that democracy can finally break the cycle of oppression in Zimbabwe.  

END OF SUMMARY.
