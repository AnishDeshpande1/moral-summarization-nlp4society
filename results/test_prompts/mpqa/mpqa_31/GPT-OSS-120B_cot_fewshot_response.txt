**SUMMARY:**  
Zimbabwe’s first contested presidential election in 22 years became a stark test of patience and moral courage. Many voters like Martha, a ZANU‑PF official who secretly supports the MDC, endured **fear** and **intimidation** to cast their ballots for change. Long lines, tear‑gas, and rubber‑bullet attacks turned polling stations into arenas of **frustration**, while the government’s reduction of urban polling sites and the surge of **rigging** and **fraud** signaled an **unfair** attempt to **disenfranchise** opposition supporters. Rural agents reported **abuse**, with ZANU‑PF officials coercing illiterate voters and even marking ballots for Mugabe. Yet despite **oppression**, the electorate’s **hope** persisted; voters such as Edward Murchabaiw and Colin Chipepera expressed joy at finally being heard, believing the vote could be the “final nail on the government’s coffin.” International monitors warned that **cheating** had begun long before election day, but the sheer number of people who braved hardship suggests a deep faith that democracy may still prevail.
