SUMMARY:  
The article describes Zimbabwe’s first contested presidential election in 22 years, pitting ruling ZANU‑PF’s Robert Mugabe against opposition leader Morgan Tsvangirai of the MDC. Voters like Martha, a ZANU‑PF official who secretly supports the MDC, and others endure long waits, intimidation, and scarce polling stations—urban areas face overcrowded stations while rural pro‑Mugabe stations are plentiful. Reports of voter‑rigging include reduced urban polling sites, lack of independent media, bans on opposition rallies, and coercion of illiterate voters. Police used tear gas and rubber bullets against restless crowds, injuring at least 12. International and local monitors claim the election was heavily compromised before voting even began. Despite these obstacles, many citizens, including a terminally ill AIDS patient, expressed hope that their votes could bring change. The MDC alleges that over half of its rural poll agents were intimidated or blocked, allowing ZANU‑PF officials to influence ballots. While the final tally remains pending, the turnout and determination of voters suggest a significant challenge to Mugabe’s rule.  

END OF SUMMARY.
