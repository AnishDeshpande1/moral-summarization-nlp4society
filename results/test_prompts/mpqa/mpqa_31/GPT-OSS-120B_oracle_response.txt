SUMMARY:  
In Zimbabwe’s first contested presidential vote in 22 years, citizens endured **fear**, **intimidation**, and **rigging** as they queued for hours under the scorching sun, hoping for **food**, **hospitals**, **school** fees and a better **life**. The ruling ZANU‑PF **slashed** urban polling stations while **boosting** rural ones, a **change** that many see as an attempt to **disenfranchise** opposition supporters. Reports of **fraud**, **abuse**, and **cheating**—including **abducted**, **chased** agents and denied **accreditation**—suggest the **rig** was **thorough** and **continued** long before election day, leading some observers to declare hopes for a free **election** **dead**. Police used **gas** and **bullets**, **injuring** dozens as crowds grew angry at the **tricks** used to stall voters. Yet ordinary people like Martha, Janet, and Edward voted despite the hardships, driven by a deep **faith** that their ballots could bring **change**. The MDC claims the **attempts** at **rigging** have not **succeeded** and that the sheer number of voters may be the “final nail” on the government’s coffin, showing that even amid **abuse**, the desire for a fair future persists.  

END OF SUMMARY.
