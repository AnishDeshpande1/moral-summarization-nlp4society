SUMMARY:
The United States launched its first airlift of 20 heavily guarded Taliban and al‑Qaeda detainees from Kandahar to the Guantanamo Bay detention facility, marking the start of a planned transfer of hundreds of prisoners. The captives were shackled, hooded and possibly sedated for the 20‑hour flight, sparking criticism from Amnesty International, which warned that such treatment could breach international standards against “cruel, inhuman or degrading” treatment. Pentagon officials said the detainees are not classified as prisoners of war but will receive Geneva Convention protections and will be held at the makeshift Camp X‑Ray, where they will face intensive interrogation and possible military tribunals. The flight was briefly threatened by small‑arms fire near the runway, though no injuries were reported. In related news, the Pentagon announced a pending deployment of over 100 U.S. soldiers to train Filipino forces against the Abu Said Group, and President Bush signed a $318 billion defense spending bill, emphasizing the need to equip the military for continued counter‑terrorism operations. The article juxtaposes U.S. security actions with concerns over detainee rights, highlighting the moral tension between aggressive anti‑terrorism measures and adherence to humanitarian standards.  
END OF SUMMARY.
