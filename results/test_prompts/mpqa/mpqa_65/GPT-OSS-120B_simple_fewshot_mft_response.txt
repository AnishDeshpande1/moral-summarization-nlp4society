SUMMARY:
The Pentagon launched the first airlift of 20 heavily guarded Taliban and al‑Qaeda detainees from Kandahar to Guantanamo’s Camp X‑Ray, asserting that—even though they are not classified as prisoners of war—they will receive Geneva Convention protections. Over 40 specially trained military police oversaw the shackled, hooded prisoners on the 20‑hour flight, with officials hinting that some may be sedated, prompting Amnesty International to condemn such treatment as potentially “cruel, inhuman or degrading.” Defense Secretary Donald Rumsfeld defended the strict security measures, citing past violent uprisings and emphasizing the authority of the U.S. military to prevent chaos. At the same time, the article notes the broader U.S. commitment to combat terrorism, including plans to send Special Operations forces to the Philippines to train troops against the Abu Said group and ongoing bombings of al‑Qaeda camps in Afghanistan. The piece highlights the tension between exercising authority for security, ensuring humane care for detainees, and upholding fairness by adhering to international legal standards.  
END OF SUMMARY.
