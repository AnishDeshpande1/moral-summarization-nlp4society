SUMMARY:  
A U.S. C‑17 departed Kandahar carrying 20 heavily guarded Taliban and al‑Qaeda detainees to Guantanamo’s Camp X‑Ray, marking the first wave of a planned hundreds‑strong transfer. Prisoners were shackled, hooded and possibly sedated for the 20‑hour flight, prompting Amnesty International to condemn the measures as potentially “cruel, inhuman or degrading” under international standards. The Pentagon insisted the detainees, though not classified as POWs, would receive Geneva Convention protections and defended the restraints by citing past violent uprisings in Afghan and Pakistani prisons.  

Simultaneously, the U.S. prepared to deploy over 100 soldiers, including Special Operations, to train Philippine forces against the Abu Said group, a Muslim militant faction linked to al‑Qaeda that holds two American hostages. President Bush signed a $318 billion defense spending bill and reiterated commitments to “high state of readiness,” while continued air strikes targeted al‑Qaeda training sites in Afghanistan.  

The article frames these actions within a moral narrative of necessary security measures, juxtaposed with criticism that the harsh treatment of detainees may breach humanitarian norms.  

END OF SUMMARY.
