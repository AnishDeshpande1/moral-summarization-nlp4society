SUMMARY:
The U.S. State Department’s annual human‑rights report on China is portrayed by Chinese scholars and foreign observers as a politically motivated smear campaign that violates the UN principle of non‑interference. Experts at a China Society for Study of Human Rights seminar argued the report is riddled with falsehoods, exaggerations and “fabricated” anecdotes, using vague citations such as “credible reports” to justify unfounded accusations about Tibet and Xinjiang. They highlighted firsthand visits by Tibetan scholars and an Italian senator who praised China’s investment in Tibetan cultural heritage and religious freedom, contrasting sharply with the report’s claims of repression. The dossier’s case against a Hong Kong businessman for smuggling “heretical” Bibles and its allegation that China exploits anti‑terrorism to target Uyghurs were denounced as baseless and as echoing U.S. double standards in its own counter‑terrorism actions. The moral takeaway presented is that the United States, by publishing a misleading human‑rights assessment, is unjustly tarnishing China’s image and breaching international norms of respect for sovereign affairs.  
END OF SUMMARY.
