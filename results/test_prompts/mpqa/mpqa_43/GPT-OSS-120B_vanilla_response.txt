SUMMARY:  
At a seminar hosted by the China Society for Study of Human Rights, experts condemned the U.S. State Department’s 2001 annual human‑rights report on China as inaccurate and politically motivated. Speakers, including Tibetan historian Xirao‑Nima, former UN official Jin Yongjian, and CSSHR president Zhou Jue, argued the report violates UN principles of non‑interference and contains fabrications, especially regarding Tibet and Xinjiang. They cited personal visits and statements from Italian senator Renzo Gubert, who praised China’s investment in Tibetan cultural sites and religious freedom, contrasting sharply with U.S. claims of repression. The report’s alleged evidence was described as vague (“credible reports,” “various sources”). Specific accusations—such as the arrest of Hong Kong businessman Li Guangqiang for smuggling Bibles and alleged anti‑terrorism crackdowns on Uyghurs—were dismissed as unjustified, with Chinese officials emphasizing that terrorism, not ethnicity, is the target. Overall, the participants asserted the U.S. report aims to tarnish China’s image rather than reflect factual human‑rights conditions.  

END OF SUMMARY.
