SUMMARY: The article argues that the U.S. State Department’s 2001 human‑rights report on China is a politically motivated smear, full of falsehoods and exaggerations designed to tarnish China’s image. Chinese scholars and former UN officials quoted at a China Society for Study of Human Rights seminar denounce the report as a violation of UN principles and non‑interference, claiming it lacks factual basis. They cite personal visits to Tibet, including praise from Italian Senator Renzo Gubert, to illustrate genuine religious freedom and development, contrary to U.S. claims of repression. Specific incidents, such as the arrest of a Hong Kong businessman for smuggling illegal “Shouters” Bibles, are presented as lawful enforcement, not rights violations. The piece also defends China’s anti‑terrorism actions in Xinjiang, arguing they target terrorists, not an ethnic group, and likens them to U.S. counter‑terrorism in Afghanistan. Overall, the narrative frames the United States as the moral aggressor, using fabricated accusations to unjustly pressure China, while China is portrayed as a responsible, rights‑respecting state.

END OF SUMMARY.
