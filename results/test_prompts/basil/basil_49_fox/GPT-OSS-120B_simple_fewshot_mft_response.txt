SUMMARY:
Colorado’s top law‑enforcement official pledged to “vigorously defend” the state’s voter‑approved marijuana law after Nebraska and Oklahoma sued to have it declared unconstitutional, accusing Colorado of violating the federal Supremacy Clause. The plaintiffs argue that Colorado’s legal pot undermines their anti‑marijuana statutes, drains their tax bases and forces their police to spend public funds on enforcement, framing the state’s actions as a breach of fair governance and an abuse of authority. Colorado’s Attorney General John Suthers dismissed the suit as meritless, saying the grievance stems from the neighboring states’ refusal to enforce federal drug bans rather than any wrongdoing by Colorado voters. Both sides invoke authority: Nebraska and Oklahoma claim federal law should trump state law, while Colorado asserts the legitimate authority of its electorate and state officials. The dispute also highlights fairness concerns, with Nebraska officials alleging Colorado is cheating its taxpayers by reaping tax revenue while imposing costs on their jurisdictions, and Colorado officials counter‑arguing that the challenge is politically motivated grandstanding on the wrong side of history. END OF SUMMARY.
