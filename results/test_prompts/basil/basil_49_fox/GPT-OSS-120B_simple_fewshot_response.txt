SUMMARY:
Colorado’s top law‑enforcement official vowed to “vigorously defend” the state’s historic marijuana‑legalization law after Nebraska and Oklahoma asked the U.S. Supreme Court to strike it down as unconstitutional. The neighboring states argue that Colorado’s Amendment 64, approved by voters in 2012, creates a “dangerous gap” that lets pot flow across state lines, undermining their anti‑marijuana statutes, draining their treasuries and overburdening police resources. Colorado Attorney General John Suthers called the suit “without merit,” saying the grievance stems from the federal government’s non‑enforcement rather than Colorado voters’ choices. Colorado points to more than $60 million in tax revenue from legal weed and contends that the lawsuit lacks concrete data on out‑of‑state trafficking. Law‑enforcement leaders in Nebraska and Oklahoma decry the perceived injustice of enforcing one set of laws while ignoring another, framing the issue as a moral duty to uphold the rule of law. Colorado officials, including Governor John Hickenlooper, suggest the lawsuit is an unconstructive, politically‑driven attack that places the state on the “wrong side of history” as more states legalize cannabis.  
END OF SUMMARY.
