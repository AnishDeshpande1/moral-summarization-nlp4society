SUMMARY: Colorado’s top law‑enforcement official has vowed to defend the state’s voter‑approved marijuana law against a lawsuit filed by Nebraska and Oklahoma. The two neighboring states ask the U.S. Supreme Court to block Amendment 64, arguing that Colorado’s legal weed drifts across borders, undermining their anti‑marijuana statutes, burdening taxpayers, and violating the federal supremacy clause. Colorado Attorney General John Suthers dismisses the claim as meritless, noting it merely reflects frustration over the federal government’s lax enforcement of drug bans. While Colorado has collected over $60 million in marijuana taxes, the plaintiffs allege the influx of “pot contraband” strains their law‑enforcement resources. Data on actual cross‑state flow are thin, though seizures have risen modestly. Sheriff Mark Overman of Nebraska decries the potency and profit of Colorado weed, urging stronger action, while Governor John Hickenlooper questions the lawsuit’s constructiveness. Legal scholars call the case unprecedented and note the uncertain federal stance on state‑level drug experiments. Colorado legalization advocates label the suit political grandstanding and argue the state is on the right side of history, urging critics to address their grievances through federal channels instead. END OF SUMMARY.
