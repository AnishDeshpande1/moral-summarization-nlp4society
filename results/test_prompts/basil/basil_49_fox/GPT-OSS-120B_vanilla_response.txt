SUMMARY: Colorado’s top law‑enforcement official vowed to defend the state’s 2012 Amendment 64 marijuana legalization after Nebraska and Oklahoma sued the U.S. Supreme Court to declare it unconstitutional, arguing that Colorado’s legal weed is flowing into their states and undermining their anti‑marijuana laws. The plaintiffs claim the measure violates the Supremacy Clause because it conflicts with federal drug bans, and they cite increased law‑enforcement costs and drug seizures. Colorado Attorney General John Suthers called the suit meritless, noting the grievance stems from non‑enforcement of federal law rather than voter choice. Colorado has collected over $60 million in marijuana taxes, while neighboring states report higher arrests and seizures. A federal Justice Department memo acknowledges limited resources to enforce marijuana bans and lists keeping marijuana in‑state as a priority—something the suing states say Colorado fails to do. Legal experts say the case is unprecedented and its outcome uncertain. Colorado Governor John Hickenlooper questioned the lawsuit’s constructiveness, and other states have not joined the action. Advocates argue the challenge is political grandstanding and that many states have adopted medical marijuana without federal overturns. END OF SUMMARY.
