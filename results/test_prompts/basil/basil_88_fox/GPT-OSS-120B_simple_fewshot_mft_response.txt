SUMMARY:
President Trump declared that “birthright citizenship” will be ended “one way or another,” claiming the policy is “unfair” to American citizens and costs the nation billions. He argued the 14th Amendment does not apply because of the phrase “subject to the jurisdiction thereof,” and invoked former Senate leader Harry Reid’s 1993 remarks to portray the rule as a loophole that rewards illegal entry. Trump’s push—initially hinted at via an executive‑order threat—was framed as a correction of a system that, in his view, cheats American taxpayers and grants an undeserved privilege to children of undocumented immigrants. Republicans largely rebuked the idea of using an executive order, citing constitutional authority: Speaker Paul Ryan said the amendment is “pretty clear,” and Sen. Chuck Grassley called for congressional debate, while Sen. Lindsey Graham announced legislation to curb what he called a “magnet for illegal immigration.” Democrats uniformly condemned the move as an unlawful subversion of constitutional authority and an attack on the fairness owed to all born on U.S. soil, with Nancy Pelosi labeling Trump’s claims as beyond his legal power. The debate thus centers on the tension between perceived legal authority and claims of fairness versus cheating the system.  
END OF SUMMARY.
