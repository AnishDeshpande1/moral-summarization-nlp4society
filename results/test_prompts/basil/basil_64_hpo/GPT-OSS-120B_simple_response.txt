SUMMARY:  
Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg’s recent jokes about moving to New Zealand if Donald Trump becomes president have sparked an ethics outcry. She told the *New York Times* and *AP* that she can’t imagine the country or the Court under Trump, and hinted she’d prefer a Hillary Clinton appointment. Legal scholars argue her candor threatens the Court’s integrity, urging recusal from any Trump‑related case. Critics like Josh Blackman call the remarks “beyond the pale,” while Rick Hasen warns a “reasonable person” might doubt her impartiality. Professor Dmitry Bam says the comments create an appearance of bias, violating the principle that justices must step aside when impartiality could be questioned. Although recusals for political views are rare, the Supreme Court’s own rules and past practice (including Ginsburg’s participation in high‑profile cases despite calls to step aside) make this episode morally contentious. Some, however, contend that acknowledging judges’ personal beliefs may be more honest than pretending total apoliticism, noting the Court’s historically partisan decisions. The debate centers on whether Ginsburg’s outspoken stance breaches judicial ethics or simply reflects the inevitable political reality of the judiciary.  
END OF SUMMARY.
