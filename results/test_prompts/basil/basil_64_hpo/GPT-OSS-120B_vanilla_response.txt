SUMMARY: Supreme Court Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg recently joked about moving to New Zealand if Donald Trump becomes president and expressed reluctance to consider a Trump‑appointed court, prompting criticism from legal scholars. Commentators such as Josh Blackman, Rick Hasen, and Dmitry Bam argue her remarks threaten the Court’s appearance of impartiality and may warrant recusal from Trump‑related cases. Federal law requires justices to step aside when “impartiality might reasonably be questioned,” yet recusals for political bias are rare. Past instances show justices, including Ginsburg, have participated in politically charged cases despite calls for recusal. Critics contend her comments cross ethical lines, while others suggest candidness about personal biases reflects the inevitable political nature of the judiciary. The debate raises questions about judicial ethics, the need for transparency, and whether the Court should maintain a pretense of apoliticism.  
END OF SUMMARY.
