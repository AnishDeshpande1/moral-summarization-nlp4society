SUMMARY:
Supreme Court Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg recently warned that a Donald Trump presidency would be “unimaginable,” even joking she might move to New Zealand. Her frank criticism of the presumptive Republican nominee has sparked a firestorm among legal scholars, who argue that such public commentary jeopardizes the Court’s appearance of impartiality. Critics like law professors Josh Blackman, Rick Hasen and Dmitry Bam contend that Ginsburg may need to recuse herself from any Trump‑related cases, noting that federal law requires judges to step aside when “impartiality might reasonably be questioned.” While recusals for political bias are rare, past instances—such as the justices’ participation in same‑sex‑marriage cases despite calls for recusal—show the Court’s reluctance to withdraw. Conservative commentator Ed Whelan calls Ginsburg’s remarks “remarkable indiscretions,” stressing that ethics concerns exist regardless of the candidate’s merits. Some observers suggest the episode reveals a deeper truth: judges are inevitably influenced by personal beliefs, and pretending otherwise may do more harm than good. The moral framing emphasizes the need for judicial restraint and the protection of the Court’s integrity above partisan expression.

END OF SUMMARY.
