SUMMARY:  
Supreme Court Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg’s recent candor about a possible Donald Trump presidency – joking she might flee to New Zealand and refusing to contemplate Trump as “appointer‑in‑chief” – has sparked a debate over judicial ethics. Legal scholars such as Josh Blackman and Dmitry Bam say her comments “crossed” an ethical line, arguing she should tell the court to quiet down or even step aside. Rick Hasen warns the remarks could lead a reasonable person to question her impartiality and might require her to recuse from any Trump‑related case. While federal law obliges justices to step aside when impartiality could be reasonably questioned, recusals for political bias are exceedingly rare; past instances show justices have declined to recuse even in highly politicized matters. Critics like Ed Whelan label Ginsburg’s statements “indiscretions,” yet some argue the pretense of total judicial neutrality does more harm than good, noting that judges often take positions influenced by personal beliefs. The controversy raises whether the court should avoid the appearance of bias or accept candid disclosures of bias as part of a realistic view of the judiciary.  
END OF SUMMARY.
