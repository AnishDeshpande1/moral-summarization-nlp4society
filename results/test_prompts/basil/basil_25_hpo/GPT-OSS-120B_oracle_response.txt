SUMMARY:  
The latest Romney‑Obama Medicare showdown pits two rival “harm” narratives. The Republican plan would turn Medicare into a quasi‑voucher system, letting recipients “fund” private coverage, while the Romney camp accuses President Obama of having “stolen” $700 billion from Medicare – a charge that carries “blood” on his hands, they claim. Fact‑checkers note the Obama‑era savings come from “limiting” waste, “fraud” and “abuse” and are “budgeted” to “help” subsidize insurance and lower prescription costs. The Obama campaign previously used a similar “characterization,” saying McCain’s proposal would “cut” benefits, raise “higher” premiums and “copays,” turning seniors’ health into a “gamble.” Those ads were later rated “false,” with analysts pointing out the plan was about “eliminating” inefficiency, not slashing care. Both parties have been accused of “refusing” to “specify” financing, “weighted” toward tax cuts, and of “demagogued” attacks that “complicate” honest debate. The dispute illustrates how political rhetoric can “contribute” to confusion over real Medicare reform, rather than focusing on genuine “savings,” “reforming,” and improved “access to care.”  
END OF SUMMARY.
