**SUMMARY:**  
The NRA‑backed task force released a 225‑page report urging that every American school employ armed police officers, security guards, or staff members, arguing that an armed presence adds a layer of security and reduces response time to danger. Citing a 1997 Mississippi incident where an assistant principal retrieved a .45‑caliber pistol and stopped a shooter, the report frames armed personnel as protectors who can safeguard vulnerable children. Critics decry the plan as fear‑mongering, accusing the NRA of turning schools into armed fortresses and intensifying the crisis of gun violence. Democratic leaders label arming teachers a reaction to past tragedies and question whether more weapons truly protect students. One Newtown parent, however, supports the recommendation, saying it offers real solutions to keep kids safe. States such as Indiana are already considering legislation to require armed “protection officers” in schools, while Congress prepares to debate broader gun‑control measures, including expanded background checks and assault‑weapon bans. The debate pits arguments over safety, responsibility, and the right to bear arms against concerns about escalating fear and lethal threats in educational settings.
