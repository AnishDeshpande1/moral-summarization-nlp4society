SUMMARY: Gov. Chris Christie launched his presidential bid with swagger and a self‑styled moral crusade, promising blunt honesty (“I say what I mean and I mean what I say”) and a return of “strength and decision‑making” to the Oval Office. He painted himself as the sole Republican who can speak uncomfortable truths, contrasting his gubernatorial experience—including the 2010 fiscal crisis and Hurricane Sandy—with Senate rivals who have never run a state. Yet the article moralizes Christie’s candidacy as a desperate reliance on charisma to mask a deteriorating record: credit downgrades, weak job growth, a collapsing pension system, and scandals that have tainted his once‑admired ethics. Voters largely reject him (55 % of Republicans say they cannot envision voting for him), leaving only Donald Trump as less palatable. Christie’s strategy now hinges on his “magnetic” personality, town‑hall rallies, and a narrative of personal redemption, even as the piece warns that his moral authority is shaky and his appeal may not survive a field of better‑funded, more popular contenders. END OF SUMMARY.
