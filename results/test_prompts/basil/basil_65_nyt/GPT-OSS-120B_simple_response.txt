SUMMARY:  
Michael Bloomberg’s fiery DNC attack on Donald Trump paints the two billionaires as moral opposites rather than long‑standing rivals. Bloomberg is depicted as a modest philanthropist, arts patron, and technocratic financier who has generally kept a courteous distance from Trump. By contrast, Trump is described as ostentatious, crass, and reckless, a “public menace” whose business‑style politics are portrayed as dangerous. Their limited interactions—golf outings, brief TV appearances, and polite Twitter exchanges during Bloomberg’s mayoralty—show a superficial civility that evaporated once Trump entered the 2016 race. Bloomberg’s brief exploratory independent bid was framed as a principled effort to stop “crazy” Trump, while Trump’s later denials of friendship and attacks on Bloomberg’s wealth emphasize the moral breach. The article thus uses their personal histories to underscore a larger ethical judgment: Bloomberg embodies responsible, community‑focused leadership; Trump embodies self‑serving, unstable behavior that threatens the public good.
