SUMMARY: Outgoing White House economic adviser Larry Summers warned that blocking the bipartisan tax‑cut compromise could trigger a double‑dip recession, urging Congress to act quickly. The deal, negotiated by President Obama and Vice‑President Biden, extends two‑year tax cuts for the wealthy and the middle class and lowers the estate tax—measures liberals denounce as giveaways to the rich that do little for ordinary Americans. In exchange, the agreement promises extended unemployment benefits and a temporary cut in Social Security payroll taxes to boost take‑home pay. While Senate Republicans are ready to bring the bill to a vote, Senate Democrats have quietly demanded changes, and House Democrats, led by figures such as Rep. Peter DeFazio, are mobilizing to block the legislation, accusing the White House of repeatedly “jamming” unpopular deals through the speaker. Obama defended the pact, citing economist forecasts of stronger job growth, but House Speaker Nancy Pelosi and other Democrats called the estate‑tax concession “a bridge too far.” The moral clash centers on whether extending tax breaks for the affluent is a responsible stimulus or an unfair transfer of wealth that betrays progressive values.  
END OF SUMMARY.
