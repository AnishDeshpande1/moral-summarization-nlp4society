SUMMARY:
Outgoing White House economic adviser Larry Summers warned that failing to approve a new tax‑cut compromise could raise the risk of a double‑dip recession, urging Congress to pass the deal quickly. The agreement, struck by President Obama with Republicans, extends tax cuts for both the wealthy and the middle class and lowers the estate tax, drawing sharp criticism from liberal Democrats. Senate Republicans, led by Sen. Jon Kyl, say the bill could reach the floor soon, though the Joint Committee on Taxation’s cost estimate is not yet ready. Senate Democrats, however, have asked the administration for changes, while House Democrats, led by figures such as Rep. Peter DeFazio and Rep. Jim McDermott, are drafting a letter to block the bill and seek a caucus vote. Vice President Biden met with House Democrats who argue the deal gives too much to the rich, despite promises of extended unemployment benefits and a temporary cut to Social Security payroll taxes to boost spending. President Obama defended the deal, citing economist forecasts of stronger job growth, but House Speaker Nancy Pelosi and other Democrats remain opposed, calling the estate‑tax provision “a bridge too far.”
END OF SUMMARY.
