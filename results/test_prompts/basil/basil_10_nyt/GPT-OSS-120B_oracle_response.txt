SUMMARY:  
On the first day of the 112th Congress, House members began a live reading of the United States Constitution on the floor, a new rule that requires every bill to cite its constitutional authority. The session started with a partisan clash after Rep. Jay Inslee demanded clarification on which passages would be read and which would be omitted, prompting a debate over language that foreshadowed future battles over the document’s meaning. After the procedural dispute was settled, Speaker John A. Boehner read the preamble, followed by Rep. Nancy Pelosi and other leaders, with members reading the text sentence by sentence in diverse accents that reflect districts that did not exist when the Constitution was written. At one point a protester was **removed** by the sergeant‑at‑arms. The reading, conceived by Rep. Robert W. Goodlatte, aims at **making** legislators and voters more aware of constitutional limits, echoing Tea Party calls for strict adherence to the founding charter. It coincided with Republicans launching their effort to repeal the health‑care law, which they argue lacks constitutional basis. This was only the third recorded instance of the Constitution being entered into the Congressional Record and the first time it was read aloud on the House floor.  
END OF SUMMARY.
