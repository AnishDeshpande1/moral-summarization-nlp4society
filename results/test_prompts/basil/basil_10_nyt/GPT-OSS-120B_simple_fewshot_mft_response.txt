SUMMARY:
On the first day of the 112th Congress, House members read the Constitution aloud to satisfy a new rule requiring every bill to cite its constitutional authority. The session quickly turned into a showdown over procedural authority: Democrat Jay Inslee demanded clarification on which passages could be read, prompting a partisan clash that foreshadowed future battles over the document’s meaning and the limits of legislative power. Republicans framed the reading as a corrective to perceived federal overreach, stressing fairness by reminding lawmakers and voters that many constituents believe Congress has exceeded its constitutional authority, especially in the ongoing effort to repeal the health‑care law. The event was presented as a bid to restore respect for legitimate authority while exposing what Republicans view as Democratic subversion of constitutional limits. The reading, organized by Rep. Robert Goodlatte, was also a symbolic nod to the Tea Party’s emphasis on constitutional fidelity, positioning the ceremony as a fair, transparent reminder that legislative action must stay within the bounds set by the nation’s founding charter. END OF SUMMARY.
