SUMMARY:

In an effort to set the tone for the 112th Congress, members of the House of Representatives began reading the United States Constitution aloud from the chamber's floor on Thursday morning. However, before the reading could begin, Jay Inslee questioned the procedure, sparking a partisan battle over language and interpretation. Once settled, Representative John A. Boehner read the preamble, followed by other leaders and members who wished to participate. The reading of the document was part of the new rules package, intended to instruct members on their constitutional authority and inform voters. As Representative Robert W. Goodlatte explained, the goal was to "send a message" that the Constitution is an important document, particularly in light of ongoing debates about federal powers.

The decision to read the Constitution aloud was also seen as a nod to the Tea Party movement, which cites the document as the basis for their political theory. Despite some disruptions and yelling from protesters, the reading continued, with members making references to the constitutional language that reflects the diverse districts they represent. The event marked a rare occurrence in Congressional history, with only two prior instances of the Constitution being inserted into the Congressional Record.

END OF SUMMARY.
