SUMMARY:  
On the opening day of the 112th Congress, members of the House fulfilled a new rule requiring every bill to cite its constitutional authority by reading the United States Constitution aloud on the chamber floor. The proceedings began with a partisan clash when Democrat Jay Inslee demanded clarification on which passages would be read, leading to a decision to omit any text superseded by amendments—thereby avoiding references to slavery and obsolete provisions like prohibition. After the debate was settled, Speaker John A. Boehner recited the preamble, followed by Nancy Pelosi, Eric Cantor and other leaders, then a diverse lineup of representatives—including women and African‑American members—who read the document sentence by sentence, echoing the voices of districts that did not exist when the Constitution was drafted. The reading, conceived by Rep. Robert W. Goodlatte, was framed as a moral reminder to legislators and citizens that Congress must respect its limited powers and to signal solidarity with the Tea Party’s constitutional‑strict stance, especially as House Republicans launched efforts to repeal the health‑care law. Historically rare, this was only the third time the full Constitution had ever been entered into the Congressional Record and the first time it was spoken aloud on the House floor.  

END OF SUMMARY.
