SUMMARY: The National Rifle Association framed its push for more armed school personnel as a moral imperative to protect children, arguing that “the only thing that stops a bad guy with a gun is a good guy with a gun.” After a weeks‑long review sparked by the Newtown tragedy, the NRA urged state legislatures to adopt a model law permitting specially trained staff to carry weapons on campus, proposing a 40‑60‑hour training program and emphasizing that teachers should focus on teaching, not fighting. Former Arkansas Rep. Asa Hutchinson softened the “armed officer in every school” stance, noting decisions should be locally made and budget‑dependent, but still called for at least one armed protector per school and for safety assessments and mental‑health pilots. The NRA’s moral narrative contrasts with Democratic‑led gun‑control efforts—such as assault‑weapon bans and stricter background checks—by claiming schools deserve the same security afforded to banks and Congress. Even a victim’s father praised the review, underscoring the charged moral debate over how best to safeguard students.  
END OF SUMMARY.
