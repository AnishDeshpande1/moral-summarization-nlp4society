SUMMARY:
Governor Asa Hutchinson, the Republican leader of Arkansas, warned that the controversial “religious freedom” bill—criticized for potentially sanctioning discrimination against LGBTQ people—faces internal family opposition. His son, Seth Hutchington, a labor organizer and self‑described “black sheep,” wrote to his father urging him to reconsider, arguing the measure could harm the state’s reputation and economy. Their dispute reflects a long‑standing, love‑filled but politically divided relationship: Seth grew up on campaign trails, but early work experiences and witnessing the marginalization of gay workers reshaped his sense of justice. He became active in gay‑rights and labor causes, joining Young Democratic Socialists and later moving to Texas as a union activist. Despite their sharp disagreements, the Hutchinsons maintain close ties, meeting regularly and debating issues openly. The governor now says he will ask legislators to recall or rewrite parts of the bill, acknowledging pressure from all sides, though Seth declines credit for the shift. The story frames Seth’s dissent as a moral stand rooted in empathy and fairness, contrasting with his father’s traditional conservative stance.

END OF SUMMARY.
