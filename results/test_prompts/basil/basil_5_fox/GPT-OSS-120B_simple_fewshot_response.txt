SUMMARY:
The NAACP adopted a unanimous resolution at its Kansas City convention condemning “racist elements” within the Tea Party movement and urging its leaders to repudiate bigotry, including racism, anti‑Semitism and homophobia. President Benjamin Todd Jealous emphasized support for free assembly but warned that the Tea Party’s tolerance of hate threatens civil‑rights progress. The NAACP cited a March Capitol‑Hill incident in which protesters allegedly shouted racial slurs at Black lawmakers, though Tea Party members dispute the claim and say no evidence exists. In response, Tea Party figures such as Dallas founder Phillip Dennis and St. Louis organizers accused the NAACP of playing the “race card,” calling the civil‑rights group a partisan “attack dog” and even urging the IRS to review its tax‑exempt status. They argue the movement welcomes minorities and that its fiscal‑conservative principles could help address problems in Black communities. The dispute highlights a broader moral imperative: civil‑rights advocates demand accountability for hate, while Tea Party supporters defend their right to organize and reject accusations of racism. Both sides frame the conflict as a test of responsibility, integrity and the proper role of political activism in a democratic society.  
END OF SUMMARY.
