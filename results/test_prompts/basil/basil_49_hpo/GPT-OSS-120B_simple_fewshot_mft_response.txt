SUMMARY:
Nebraska and Oklahoma have taken the extraordinary step of filing a federal lawsuit in the U.S. Supreme Court, arguing that Colorado’s voter‑approved legalization of recreational marijuana violates the Supremacy Clause because federal law still bans the drug. The plaintiffs, led by Nebraska Attorney General Jon Bruning and Oklahoma Attorney General Scott Pruitt, claim Colorado’s policy harms their states by allowing illegal marijuana to cross borders and burdening their law‑enforcement resources, framing the suit as a defense of constitutional authority and public safety. Colorado Attorney General John Suthers rebuffs the claim, insisting the lawsuit is baseless and that the real grievance is the other states’ refusal to respect Colorado voters’ choices—a subversion of state sovereignty and fairness. Advocates on both sides invoke care concerns: Nebraska and Oklahoma stress the damage to their citizens, while Colorado supporters warn that the suit threatens the fair, democratic will of Colorado’s electorate and could jeopardize legalization efforts nationwide. The clash pits competing visions of lawful authority, the responsibility to protect neighboring populations, and the equitable treatment of states pursuing divergent drug policies.  
END OF SUMMARY.
