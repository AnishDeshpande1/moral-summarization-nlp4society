SUMMARY:

Larry Summers, outgoing chairman of the National Economic Council, warned that failure to pass the tax deal would significantly increase the risk of a double-dip recession. President Obama disputed criticism from fellow Democrats, saying the more who look at it, the more will say this makes sense. He urged Congress to examine the agreement and take action, emphasizing that the American people are watching and expecting results.

The administration has been pushing back against liberal outrage over the deal, highlighting endorsements from private-sector forecasters and Democratic officeholders. Vice President Biden is set to meet with House Democrats in an attempt to sell the compromise. Despite this, many Democrats remain firmly opposed to the president's negotiated deal.

Economists have upwardly revised their forecasts for growth after the tax deal was announced, projecting increases in economic growth next year by at least 0.5 percentage points and the creation of 1.6 million jobs. However, liberal critics continue to express outrage over what they see as a betrayal by the president, with some calling the deal "fiscally irresponsible" and "grossly unfair."

END OF SUMMARY.
