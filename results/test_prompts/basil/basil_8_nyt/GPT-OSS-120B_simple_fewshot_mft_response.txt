SUMMARY:
Outgoing NEC chair Larry Summers warned that blocking President Obama’s tax‑cut package would raise the risk of a double‑dip recession, underscoring the administration’s view that the deal is the correct exercise of executive authority. Obama and senior aides, including VP Biden, framed the compromise as a responsible, bipartisan response to economic danger, urging Congress to respect the president’s leadership and “get this done” for the American people. In contrast, a growing coalition of liberal Democrats portrayed the agreement as a betrayal of party loyalty, labeling it fiscally irresponsible and unfair to working‑class voters. House members such as Peter Welch and Barney Frank signed letters denouncing the deal, while Senate Democrats remained firmly opposed despite Biden’s lobbying. The White House countered with endorsements from economists, Democratic officials, and the DNC, attempting to reinforce authority and rally loyal supporters. The moral clash centers on whether adherence to presidential authority and party unity outweighs the perceived subversion and betrayal by those who resist the tax compromise.  
END OF SUMMARY.
