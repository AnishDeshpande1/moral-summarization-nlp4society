SUMMARY:
Senator Marco Rubio used a Council on Foreign Relations speech to lay out a “Rubio Doctrine” that frames American power as a moral duty to keep the world safe. Citing John F. Kennedy, he argued that a stronger military and assertive U.S. leadership deter war, whereas President Obama’s “soft‑on‑adversaries” approach invites danger. Rubio stressed three guiding principles – American strength, protection of the U.S. economy in a globalized market, and moral clarity about core U.S. values. He attacked Obama’s moves to normalize relations with Cuba, warned against Iran nuclear talks, backed rebels in Syria, and pushed for more aid to Ukraine to counter Russian aggression. He also denounced Hillary Clinton’s tenure as Secretary of State as “ineffective at best and dangerously negligent at worst.” When pressed about invoking Kennedy, Rubio said the message is timeless, and he clarified he would not have supported the 2003 Iraq invasion had he known WMDs were absent. The speech positions Rubio as a hawkish, principled alternative to what he portrays as a weak, overly diplomatic Obama administration.  
END OF SUMMARY.
