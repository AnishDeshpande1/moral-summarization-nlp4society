SUMMARY:
Sen. Marco Rubio used a Council on Foreign Relations speech to assert a strong, authoritative U.S. role abroad, arguing that American power is the best safeguard against global harm. Invoking President Kennedy, Rubio claimed that “strength prevents war” and warned that President Obama’s softer, “nation‑building at home” approach weakens authority and invites danger. He outlined his “Rubio Doctrine” – American strength, protection of the U.S. economy, and moral clarity – positioning himself as a defender of national security and a counter‑weight to what he frames as Obama’s subversive retreat from leadership. Rubio criticized the Obama administration’s diplomatic overtures to Cuba and Iran, and attacked Hillary Clinton’s record as “ineffective at best and dangerously negligent at worst,” reinforcing his stance against perceived subversion of U.S. authority. He also linked a robust military budget to protecting American citizens and allies from harm, while noting he would not have supported the 2003 Iraq invasion had he known the WMD claim was false, signaling a concern for avoiding unnecessary bloodshed. The speech thus blends a call for decisive authority with a promise to shield the nation from threats and suffering.  
END OF SUMMARY.
