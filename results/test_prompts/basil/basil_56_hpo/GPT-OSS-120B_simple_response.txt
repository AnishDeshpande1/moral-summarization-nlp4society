SUMMARY:
Sen. Marco Rubio used a Council on Foreign Relations speech to cast his foreign‑policy vision as a moral imperative, invoking President John F. Kennedy to argue that American strength prevents war and that U.S. weakness invites danger. He condemned President Obama for “softening” America’s stance, claiming the president’s retreat from global leadership harms both U.S. security and world peace. Rubio outlined his “Rubio Doctrine”—American strength, protection of the U.S. economy, and moral clarity about core values—and framed each as ethical duties to safeguard freedom. He attacked the Obama administration’s outreach to Cuba, its Iran nuclear talks, and its limited support for Syrian rebels and Ukraine, portraying these policies as naïve and morally hazardous. Rubio also denounced Hillary Clinton’s tenure as Secretary of State as “ineffective…dangerously negligent.” When pressed on the Kennedy reference, he insisted the message is “timeless,” not retrograde. He distanced himself from the 2003 Iraq invasion, saying he would not have supported it without evidence of WMDs, contrasting his stance with Jeb Bush’s recent defense of the war. Rubio’s rhetoric frames a robust, value‑driven U.S. role abroad as the ethical path to global stability. END OF SUMMARY.
