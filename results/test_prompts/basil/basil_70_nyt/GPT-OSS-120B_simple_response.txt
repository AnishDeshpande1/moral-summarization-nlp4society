SUMMARY:  
Donald J. Trump’s inauguration was cast as a moral crusade against “American carnage.” In a rain‑soaked address, the outsider‑president vowed to rescue a nation he described as ravaged by poverty, factory loss, drugs and crime, promising to “fight for you with every breath” and return jobs, borders, wealth and dreams to the people. He framed his rise as a populist uprising against a corrupt, self‑servicing elite in Washington, declaring that the era of empty talk was over and that power must be returned to ordinary citizens. The ceremony combined traditional rites—oaths on historic Bibles, joint coffee with former presidents—with starkly grim rhetoric, contrasting his anti‑establishment stance with the modest turnout and protests that marked the day. While Trump pledged protectionist policies, border security and the eradication of Islamic terrorism, he also claimed there was “no room for prejudice.” Democrats and many Washington residents responded with disappointment and unrest, underscoring the deep moral and political divide his inauguration opened. The event thus signaled, in the author’s view, a moral reset: a hostile takeover of the capital aimed at overturning a perceived system of inequality and restoring a mythic American greatness.  
END OF SUMMARY.
