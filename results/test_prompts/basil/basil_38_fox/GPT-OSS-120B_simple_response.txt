SUMMARY:  
The IRS and Treasury announced that legally married same‑sex couples can now file federal joint tax returns, extending the same tax benefits to them that heterosexual couples enjoy. This policy follows the Supreme Court’s decision striking down part of the Defense of Marriage Act and applies nationwide, regardless of a couple’s state of residence, though it does not cover domestic partnerships or civil unions. Treasury Secretary Jacob Lew framed the ruling as a matter of fairness, saying it gives “all Americans” the benefits, responsibilities, and protections they deserve under federal tax law. Human Rights Campaign President Chad Griffin echoed this moral view, calling the change a correction of “discriminatory” treatment and a step toward equality for “committed and loving” gay and lesbian families. In contrast, Brian Brown, president of the National Organization of Marriage, condemned the decision as part of a “pattern of lawlessness,” accusing the Obama administration of imposing same‑sex “marriage” on an unwilling public and arguing that only Congress should set tax policy.  

END OF SUMMARY.
