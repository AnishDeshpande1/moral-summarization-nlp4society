SUMMARY:

Lawmakers’ attempt to read the Constitution on the House floor turned into a partisan showdown over what counts as “sacred” text. Republicans, led by Rep. Bob Goodlatte, skipped portions of the original document that have been superseded by amendments—such as the three‑fifths clause and the original prohibition amendment—arguing that obsolete language need not be recited. Democrats blasted the move as a moral failure, insisting that omitting amended sections erases the struggle of African Americans, women and other groups to improve an imperfect founding charter. Rep. Jesse Jackson Jr., Rep. Elijah Cummings, and Rep. Jerrold Nadler condemned the “ritualistic” reading as propaganda that worships a document the founders themselves would not have treated as divine. Supporters, however, hailed the historic first reading as a symbolic reminder that the United States is a government of laws, not men, and urged a return to the Constitution’s original limits on federal power. Historians and libertarian scholars echoed the view that revisiting the full text is needed to curb today’s expansive government. The episode highlights a moral clash: whether the Constitution should be honored in its entirety, including its flawed origins, or selectively highlighted to serve contemporary political aims.  

END OF SUMMARY.
