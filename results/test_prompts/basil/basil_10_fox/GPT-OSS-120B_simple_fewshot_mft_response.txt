SUMMARY:
Newly sworn House members attempted a symbolic reading of the Constitution, but Republicans excised sections they deemed “obsolete,” such as the three‑fifths clause and the original prohibition amendment, arguing that later amendments supersede them. Democrats condemned this selective ritual as a subversive betrayal of authority, insisting that omitting the original text dishonors the nation’s struggle for fairness and erases the painful history of slavery and disenfranchisement. Lawmakers like Rep. Jesse Jackson Jr. and Rep. Elijah Cummings warned that skipping amended passages undermines the moral duty to acknowledge past injustices and to treat all citizens equitably, framing the omission as a cheat on the promise of a more perfect union. Critics such as Rep. Jerrold Nadler denounced the ceremony as sanctimonious propaganda, accusing Republicans of weaponizing reverence for the document to betray the public’s trust and to defend a limited‑government agenda. Supporters argued the reading affirms loyalty to the founding charter and its sacred role in restraining governmental overreach, while opponents saw it as a degrading performance that masks contemporary abuses of power. END OF SUMMARY.
