SUMMARY:  
On Thursday, newly sworn members of the U.S. House attempted a ceremonial reading of the Constitution, but Republicans omitted sections that have been superseded by amendments, such as the 18th Amendment (Prohibition) and the three‑fifths compromise. Democrats argued that skipping any original text undermines the exercise’s purpose and erases historical struggles. Representatives Jesse Jackson Jr., Jay Inslee, Elijah Cummings, and Jerrold Nadler criticized the selective reading, calling it “propaganda” and emphasizing the Constitution’s role as a living, imperfect document. Supporters, including Rep. Bob Goodlatte and Rep. Steve Israel, defended the event as a symbolic reminder that Congress is bound by law, not men, and cited the need to revisit the Founders’ intent to limit government. Historians and think‑tank scholars offered mixed views, noting the theatrical nature but also the relevance to debates over government size. Outside the Capitol, activists gathered to push the Equal Rights Amendment, highlighting ongoing calls to amend the Constitution for women’s equality. The reading marked the first full‑chamber recitation, sparking partisan debate over how the nation’s foundational text should be presented and interpreted.  
END OF SUMMARY.
