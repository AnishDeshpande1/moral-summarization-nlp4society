SUMMARY:

Newly sworn members of the House read the U.S. Constitution aloud on Thursday, but Republicans decided to skip obsolete parts that have been superseded by amendments. This decision was met with criticism from Democratic lawmakers who felt it undermined the exercise's purpose. They argued that reading only the amended sections would gloss over the document's imperfections and historical struggles.

Rep. Jesse Jackson Jr. (D-IL) expressed his concern, stating that exempting parts of the Constitution would "lose the ongoing struggle to improve" the country. Other lawmakers, like Rep. Jerrold Nadler (D-NY), criticized the exercise altogether, calling it a "ritualistic reading" and "propaganda." However, supporters saw it as an historic moment, noting that it was the first time the Constitution had been read on the House floor.

The debate highlights differing views on the Constitution's significance and the role of amendments in shaping its meaning. END OF SUMMARY.
