SUMMARY:
House Republicans staged a historic reading of the U.S. Constitution on the House floor, but chose to omit sections that have been superseded by amendments—such as the 18th Amendment, the three‑fifths clause, and other obsolete provisions. Democrats condemned the selective reading as a moral failure, arguing that skipping amended text erases the nation’s struggle for justice and the ongoing effort to perfect the “imperfect” document. Rep. Jesse Jackson Jr. warned that omitting these passages disrespects the sacrifices of African Americans, women, and other marginalized groups. Others, like Rep. Jerrold Nadler, dismissed the ceremony as “propaganda” and urged that the Constitution be treated as a living guide, not a sacred relic. Supporters, however, defended the exercise as a symbolic reminder that the United States is a government of laws, not of men, and a chance to reaffirm the Founders’ intent of limited government. Historians and policy experts debated whether the reading was theatrical or a necessary corrective to modern governmental overreach. The debate highlighted deep moral disagreements over how America should honor, interpret, and improve its founding charter.  
END OF SUMMARY.
