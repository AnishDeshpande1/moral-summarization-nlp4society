SUMMARY: In a rare showdown of running mates, Vice President Biden seized the vice‑presidential debate to make up for President Obama’s muted performance, adopting a combative, almost confrontational style. He relentlessly attacked Paul Ryan and the Romney ticket—especially Romney’s “47 percent” remark, which Biden framed as a betrayal of ordinary Americans, his own family and neighbors. While Ryan accused Obama of weak foreign policy and a hollow economic recovery, Biden countered with sarcasm and personal jabs, insisting he “always says what I mean” and dismissing Ryan’s critiques as “malarkey.” The debate spiraled into a heated exchange over Libya, Medicare, the debt crisis and social issues, each side accusing the other of endangering the middle class. Biden’s aggressive tone was meant to reset the campaign narrative, positioning himself as a “happy warrior” for ordinary people, while Ryan warned of a leadership vacuum in the Obama administration. The clash underscored the moral framing of Biden as the defender of the common citizen against a Republican ticket portrayed as out‑of‑touch and dangerous. END OF SUMMARY.
