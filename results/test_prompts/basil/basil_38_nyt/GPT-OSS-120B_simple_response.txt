SUMMARY:  
The Treasury Department and IRS announced that, beginning with the 2013 tax year, any couple legally married—whether same‑sex or opposite‑sex—must file federal returns as “married filing jointly” or “married filing separately,” regardless of the state’s stance on their union. This implements the Supreme Court’s June decision overturning DOMA, extending federal recognition and tax benefits to gay couples nationwide and affirming their right to move freely without losing filing status. Civil‑rights groups hailed the move as a correction of past discrimination, while tax experts note it may trigger a modest “marriage penalty” for some same‑sex couples and create paperwork challenges in states that do not recognize their marriage. States will need to reconcile federal joint filings with their own rules, and other agencies are issuing varied standards—Medicare uses a “place of celebration” approach, whereas Social Security currently applies a “place of residence” test, leading to uneven benefits. The Obama administration urges swift, consistent implementation, but the patchwork of regulations raises ongoing questions about fairness and equal treatment for LGBTQ + families.  

END OF SUMMARY.
