SUMMARY: President Trump announced he is “very close” to naming a new FBI director, with former Democratic‑turned‑independent Senator Joe Lieberman emerging as the frontrunner. Lieberman, 75, met Trump at the White House and, when pressed, the president confirmed Lieberman is the leading contender. Lieberman’s law‑enforcement credentials include two terms as Connecticut attorney general (1983‑89) before his Senate career. Though once a Democrat, he “changed” parties after losing a primary, later endorsing Republican John McCain and speaking at the GOP convention, yet he also backed Hillary Clinton in 2016. Republicans find the cross‑party pick “intriguing,” with Senate Majority Whip John Cornyn noting Lieberman could garner unanimous Senate support, while Democrats like Sen. Claire McCaskill call the move a “mistake.” Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer warned against a “career politician” from either side leading the FBI, without naming Lieberman. The shortlist also included acting director Andrew McCabe and former Gov. Frank Keating, but Cornyn withdrew his own name. END OF SUMMARY.
