SUMMARY: President Trump announced he is “very close” to naming a new FBI director, with former Democratic‑turned‑independent Senator Joe Lieberman emerging as the front‑runner. Lieberman, 75, met with Trump at the White House and has a background as Connecticut attorney general and a long, party‑crossing Senate career, including a vice‑presidential run with Al Gore and later support for Republican John McCain. Trump also conferred with acting FBI Director Andrew McCabe, ex‑Oklahoma Governor Frank Keating, and FBI official Richard McFeely. While some Republicans find the bipartisan pick “intriguing,” Democrats like Senator Claire McCaskill call it a mistake, and Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer warns against any “career politician” heading the FBI. Senate Majority Whip John Cornyn, who was himself on the shortlist, noted Lieberman might be the only candidate who could secure unanimous Senate approval. The nomination reflects Trump’s unconventional approach, stirring partisan debate over the suitability of a former Democrat‑turned‑independent leading the nation’s top law‑enforcement agency. END OF SUMMARY.
