SUMMARY: Senior White House officials are pressuring congressional Democrats to pass a bill extending the Bush tax cuts, warning that delay could raise the risk of a “double‑dip” recession. Outgoing economic adviser Larry Summers warned that failure to act within weeks would “significantly increase” recession risk, though his earlier comments had downplayed the cuts’ importance. Former Clinton commerce official Rob Shapiro cautioned that waiting would not cause a double dip; the real danger lies in a contractionary fiscal stance and the need to stabilize housing prices, which is not on the legislative agenda. The White House is aggressively marketing the deal— which includes extensions for wealthy taxpayers and a more generous estate tax— to skeptical Democrats, offering few concessions. Press Secretary Robert Gibbs said adjustments are acceptable if they don’t weaken support, while senior adviser David Axelrod stressed the real consequences of inaction, such as higher taxes and loss of unemployment benefits for millions. The administration argues that the current package, though imperfect, is necessary to avoid a difficult economic outlook. END OF SUMMARY.
