SUMMARY: Senior White House officials are using stark moral pressure on congressional Democrats to pass a Bush‑tax‑cut extension, warning that failure “significantly increases the risk” of a double‑dip recession. Outgoing economic adviser Larry Summers warned that a delay could stall the economy, while former Clinton official Rob Shapiro cautioned that the real danger lies in a contractionary fiscal stance and that stabilizing housing prices—not the tax cut—should be the priority. The administration’s pitch is clear: accept the current deal, which includes extensions for the wealthy and a softened estate tax, or be blamed for an economic downturn that would raise taxes and cut unemployment benefits for millions. White House press secretary Robert Gibbs and senior adviser David Axelrod framed the debate as a choice between compromise now or severe consequences later, emphasizing the ethical responsibility to avoid worsening conditions for ordinary Americans. No substantial concessions are offered, and the message is that the moral imperative is to act quickly to prevent foreseeable hardship. END OF SUMMARY.
