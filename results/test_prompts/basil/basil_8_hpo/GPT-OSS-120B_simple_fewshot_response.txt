SUMMARY:
Senior White House officials, led by outgoing economic adviser Larry Summers, warned that Democratic inaction on extending the Bush tax cuts could “significantly increase the risk” of a double‑dip recession. Summers warned that without the deal, the economy could stall, while former Clinton‑era official Rob Shapiro argued the delay itself would not cause a double dip but stressed the need for broader fiscal stimulus and, especially, a stable housing market—none of which are in the current proposal. The administration’s pitch to skeptical Democrats is stark: accept the package, which includes extensions of tax cuts for the wealthy, a revised estate‑tax break, and a 13‑month unemployment‑insurance extension, or be blamed for an economic downturn. White House Press Secretary Robert Gibbs and senior adviser David Axelrod said they would consider modest tweaks, but only if they do not weaken the core provisions. The moral framing presented is a choice between compromising on unpopular tax breaks to avert a possible recession, or risking severe economic consequences for the nation’s most vulnerable workers. END OF SUMMARY.
