SUMMARY:
President Donald Trump is “very close” to naming former Democratic‑turned‑independent Senator Joe Lieberman as an interim FBI director, a move that has sparked sharp criticism for subverting the agency’s traditional authority. Democrats argue that appointing a career politician—who lacks law‑enforcement experience and has publicly supported an independent Russia‑investigation commission—undermines the FBI’s institutional authority and threatens the fairness of its oversight. Senators Claire McCaskill, Brian Schatz, Dick Durbin and others denounced the choice as a “mistake” and “shocking,” warning that it would erode public confidence and betray the principle of merit‑based, non‑partisan leadership. By contrast, Republicans praised Lieberman, with Marco Rubio, John Cornyn and the late John McCain lauding his experience and predicting smooth confirmation, framing the nomination as a rightful assertion of executive authority. The controversy highlights a clash between preserving the FBI’s independent authority and a perceived partisan subversion that many view as unfair and potentially cheating the public of an unbiased law‑enforcement head amid the ongoing Russia investigation.

END OF SUMMARY.
