SUMMARY: President Donald Trump announced he is “very close” to naming an interim FBI director, with former Democratic Senator Joe Lieberman emerging as his leading candidate. Lieberman, a centrist who has never served in law‑enforcement and now heads a high‑profile law firm, would be an unconventional choice, prompting fierce moral criticism from Democrats who argue that appointing a politician threatens the FBI’s independence and erodes public trust. Senators Claire McCaskill, Brian Schatz, Dick Durbin and Sheldon Whitehouse condemned the idea as a “mistake” and “shocking,” warning that it would politicize a cornerstone of democracy and likely split the Senate. In contrast, Republicans such as Marco Rubio, John Cornyn and John McCain praised Lieberman, even suggesting he could secure unanimous confirmation. The controversy intensifies after Trump’s dismissal of James Comey amid the Russia investigation, raising alarms that the president may be seeking to place a political ally at the helm of the FBI to influence the probe. The moral framing centers on the danger of compromising the agency’s non‑partisan mission by installing a partisan figure. END OF SUMMARY.
