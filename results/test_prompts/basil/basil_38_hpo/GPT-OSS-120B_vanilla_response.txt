SUMMARY: The U.S. Treasury Department announced that, for federal tax purposes, same‑sex marriages will be recognized nationwide regardless of the couple’s state of residence. This policy follows the Supreme Court’s June decision overturning the Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA) and clarifies that the state where the marriage was performed determines federal filing status. Treasury Secretary Jacob Lew said the rule gives same‑sex couples certainty, access to tax benefits, and the ability to move freely without changing their federal status. All tax provisions that consider marital status—filing status, employee benefits, IRA contributions, earned‑income and child tax credits, as well as income, gift, and estate taxes—will now apply equally to legally married same‑sex couples. The change does not extend to domestic partnerships or civil unions and reintroduces the “marriage penalty” for some joint filers. The move aligns with other Obama‑era agency actions extending federal benefits to gay couples in non‑recognizing states. Edie Windsor, whose case led to DOMA’s repeal, praised the decision as the remedy she sought. END OF SUMMARY.
