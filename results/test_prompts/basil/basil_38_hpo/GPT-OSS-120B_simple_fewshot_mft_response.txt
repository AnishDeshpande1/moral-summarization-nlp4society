SUMMARY:
The Treasury Department, exercising its federal authority, announced a new rule that forces the Internal Revenue Service to recognize same‑sex marriages for tax purposes even when couples reside in states that refuse to acknowledge them. By overturning the discriminatory legacy of the Defense of Marriage Act, the administration is subverting outdated legal barriers and restoring fairness to LGBTQ Americans, granting them the same tax benefits, credits and responsibilities afforded to opposite‑sex couples. Treasury Secretary Jacob Lew stressed that the policy offers clear, uniform guidance and protects married couples from unequal treatment as they move across state lines. Advocates hailed the move as a correction of past cheating—denying equal tax treatment—and a concrete step toward equal protection under federal law. The ruling also means same‑sex couples will now face the same “marriage penalty” that heterosexual filers do, underscoring the commitment to treating all marriages equally under the law. Ed Windsor, whose lawsuit sparked the DOMA challenge, praised the decision as the remedy she sought.

END OF SUMMARY.
