SUMMARY:  
Nebraska and Oklahoma’s attorneys general have filed the first major interstate lawsuit challenging Colorado’s 2012 voter‑approved marijuana legalization. They argue that Colorado’s network of licensed recreational dispensaries creates a “dangerous gap” in federal drug control, allowing marijuana to flow into their states, draining tax revenues, and burdening criminal‑justice systems with extra arrests, impounded vehicles, and court costs. Local sheriffs on the border cite increased sightings of out‑of‑state marijuana and the strain on jail budgets as evidence of harm. Colorado’s Republican attorney general, John Suthers, calls the challenge “without merit” and vows a vigorous defense, noting that the suit targets only the commercial aspects of legalization, not personal possession. Critics warn that shutting down legal stores would revive the black market and empower violent criminal organizations. The plaintiffs accuse Colorado of ignoring federal drug‑control objectives, failing to track sales, and allowing anyone over 21 to purchase without background checks, thereby facilitating illicit diversion. The case bypasses lower courts and goes directly to the U.S. Supreme Court as a dispute among states.  

END OF SUMMARY.
