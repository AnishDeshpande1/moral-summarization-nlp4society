SUMMARY:
Nebraska and Oklahoma have brought the first major interstate lawsuit to the U.S. Supreme Court, demanding that Colorado’s state‑run recreational marijuana stores be shut down. The attorneys general argue that Colorado’s legal market creates a “dangerous gap” in federal drug policy, allowing marijuana to flow across state lines, draining neighboring states’ treasuries, and burdening their criminal‑justice systems. Sheriffs on the border cite increased arrests, impounded vehicles and costs, framing the issue as a public‑safety and fiscal threat. Colorado’s Republican attorney general, John Suthers, calls the suit “without merit” and pledges a vigorous defense, emphasizing the voters’ right to decide their own drug laws. The challenge targets the commercial aspects of legalization—licensed dispensaries and production facilities—while leaving the personal‑use provision of Amendment 64 untouched. Advocates warn that shutting down legal retailers could revive the black market and empower violent criminal groups. The case pits the moral claim of protecting neighboring communities from drug‑related harms against the principle of state autonomy and respect for voter‑approved reforms.

END OF SUMMARY.
