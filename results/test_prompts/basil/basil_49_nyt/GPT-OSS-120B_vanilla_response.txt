SUMMARY: Attorneys general from Nebraska and Oklahoma have filed a landmark lawsuit directly with the U.S. Supreme Court challenging key provisions of Colorado’s 2012 voter‑approved marijuana legalization (Amendment 64). The suit argues that Colorado’s regulated retail system creates a “dangerous gap” that allows marijuana to flow into neighboring states where it remains illegal, straining law‑enforcement resources, increasing arrests, and costing local treasuries. While the complaint targets the commercial aspects—licensed stores, dispensaries, and production facilities—it does not seek to overturn personal possession rights, so some elements of legalization could survive. Colorado’s attorney general, John Suthers, called the challenge meritless and pledged a vigorous defense. Marijuana industry officials warn that shutting down retailers could revive the black market and empower criminal groups. The lawsuit claims Colorado fails to track sales, enforce out‑of‑state transport bans, and prevent organized crime from acquiring product, though some border sheriffs report no noticeable increase in cross‑state traffic. The case raises broader questions about the clash between state‑level drug policies and federal drug‑control laws. END OF SUMMARY.
