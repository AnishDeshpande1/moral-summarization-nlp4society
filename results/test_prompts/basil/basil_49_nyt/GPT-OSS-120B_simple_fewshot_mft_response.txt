SUMMARY:
Nebraska and Oklahoma’s attorneys general have filed the first major interstate lawsuit challenging Colorado’s voter‑approved marijuana legalization, arguing that the state’s regulated dispensaries violate federal drug law and undermine neighboring states’ authority. The suit claims Colorado’s “gap” in the federal control system lets marijuana flow across borders, cheating Nebraska and Oklahoma out of tax revenue, overburdening their criminal‑justice systems and draining their treasuries. Sheriff Adam Hayward of Nebraska praised the challenge as a necessary defense of state authority and a fair response to the perceived injustice of out‑of‑state drug influxes. Colorado Attorney General John Suthers, a Republican who opposed Amendment 64 but now defends it, called the suit “without merit” and vowed to vigorously protect the state’s regulatory authority. The complaint targets the commercial framework of Colorado’s legalization—licensed stores, taxes and production—while leaving the personal‑use provision untouched, suggesting a nuanced approach to authority and fairness. Marijuana industry officials warn that overturning the retail system would hand power back to illegal cartels, framing the lawsuit as a subversive attempt that could create an unfair, crime‑driven market.  
END OF SUMMARY.
