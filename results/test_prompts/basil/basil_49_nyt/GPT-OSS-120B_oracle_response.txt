SUMMARY: Nebraska and Oklahoma have filed the first major court challenge to Colorado’s marijuana legalization, arguing that the state‑created system of retail shops “flows” marijuana into neighboring states, “draining” their treasuries and placing “stress” on their criminal‑justice systems. Sheriffs along the border say the “influx” of Colorado‑sourced cannabis is a “problem,” leading to more arrests, impounded vehicles and higher jail costs. The lawsuit asks the U.S. Supreme Court to strike down key parts of the 2012 voter‑approved amendment, claiming Colorado is “ignoring” federal drug‑control rules and has a “development” of “policies” and “schemes” that allow “acquiring” by criminal groups. Colorado’s attorney general, John Suthers, says the challenge is “without merit” and will “defend” the state’s law. If Nebraska and Oklahoma “succeed,” industry officials warn the black market could return, letting “violent criminal organizations” “put” the “charge” back on illegal trade. The case highlights a clash between state‑level legalization and neighboring states’ anti‑marijuana stance. END OF SUMMARY.
