SUMMARY: President Trump, just 24 hours from his self‑imposed deadline, told reporters he was “very close” to naming a new FBI director and highlighted former Democratic senator Joseph I. Lieberman as a finalist. While Trump cherishes the FBI’s reputation, his staff warned against a rapid embrace of the 75‑year‑old political operator, noting his lack of federal law‑enforcement experience and potential damage to morale after the controversial firing of James B. Comey. Administration officials, anxious about leaks, kept the search fluid, weighing internal candidates such as Acting Director Andrew G. McCabe and Richmond field‑office chief Adam S. Lee. Critics pointed to Lieberman’s past crime‑related roles, his ties to Trump lawyer Marc E. Kasowitz, and the specter of a short, symbolic tenure to lend bipartisan credibility. Democrats decried the move as a partisan ouster and invoked Watergate as a warning, while some Republicans, including the late John McCain, defended Lieberman’s bipartisan appeal. The White House remains divided, with other prospects including former Gov. Frank Keating, Rep. Mike Rogers, and judges Michael J. Garcia and Henry E. Hudson still under consideration. END OF SUMMARY.
