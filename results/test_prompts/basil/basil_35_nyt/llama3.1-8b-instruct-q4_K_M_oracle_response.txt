SUMMARY:

The White House released e-mails revealing internal jostling over the government's official talking points following the Benghazi attack in Libya, which killed four Americans, including Ambassador J. Christopher Stevens. E-mails showed a disagreement between David H. Petraeus and his deputy, Michael J. Morell, over how much to disclose about C.I.A. warnings of extremist threats in Libya. The final version of the talking points was stripped of material that would have raised questions about the State Department's security posture, leading critics to accuse the administration of airbrushing its account of the attack during an election campaign.

The e-mails revealed a sensitive process where White House officials mediated a bureaucratic tug of war between the State Department and the C.I.A. over how much to disclose, with some officials prioritizing protecting an ongoing investigation. The release of the e-mails confirmed that the administration had altered its talking points for political reasons, raising questions about the motivations behind these changes.

The attack on Benghazi was a threat that exposed weaknesses in the US security posture, and the e-mails suggest that officials were trying to break down the sensitive information into a more palatable narrative. END OF SUMMARY.
