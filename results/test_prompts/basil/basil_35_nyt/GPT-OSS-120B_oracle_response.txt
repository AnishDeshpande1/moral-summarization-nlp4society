SUMMARY:  
E‑mails released by the White House expose a fierce internal battle over the government’s talking points after the September 2012 Benghazi **attack** that **killed** four Americans, including Ambassador J. Christopher Stevens. The dispute pitted the State Department against the CIA, and even split CIA director David Petraeus and his deputy Michael Morell. Morell removed a reference to CIA **warnings** of extremist **threats** in Libya, a move the State Department called **sensitive** because it might reflect badly on them. Petraeus rejected the heavily **scrubbed** draft, noting it would not satisfy House Intelligence Chairman Rep. Ruppersberger. Republicans seized on the correspondence, accusing Obama’s aides of **airbrushing** the account for political gain, while the White House argued the edits were meant to **protect** an ongoing investigation and present the **facts** as known. The final version omitted language about Libya being awash with weapons and fighters—details that could have highlighted the State Department’s **threat**‑assessment failures and the risk of **attacks** such as the **break**‑in at the embassy. The release offers a rare glimpse of the frantic, high‑stakes effort to craft a public narrative under intense Capitol Hill pressure.  

END OF SUMMARY.
