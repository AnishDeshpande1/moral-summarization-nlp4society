SUMMARY:  
The released e‑mails expose a morally charged scramble inside the Obama administration to balance truth‑telling with institutional self‑protection after the September 2012 Benghazi attack. CIA Director David Petraeus and deputy Michael Morell clashed over how much of the agency’s own warnings to include in the talking points that UN Ambassador Susan Rice would deliver on television. Morell unilaterally removed references to CIA alerts about extremist threats, while State Department officials objected, fearing damage to their reputation. Petraeus rejected the heavily edited draft, noting it would not satisfy the House Intelligence Committee’s request for a full account. Throughout the night, senior officials—CIA counsel, NSC staff, the State Department’s Victoria Nuland, and deputy national‑security adviser Benjamin Rhodes—exchanged frantic messages, each trying to safeguard their agency’s interests, the ongoing FBI investigation, and the nation’s image as an election approached. The White House eventually released a sanitized final version, stripping out mentions of Libya’s weapons‑laden environment and CIA warnings. Republicans seized the episode as evidence of political spin, while the administration argued the process was driven by intelligence officials seeking factual accuracy and protection of the investigation. The episode underscores the ethical tension between governmental transparency and the instinct to shield agencies from blame.  
END OF SUMMARY.
