SUMMARY:

The Benghazi controversy highlights the State Department's failure to act on documented warnings and security incidents, despite a growing threat of Islamist attacks in Libya. Classified cables revealed that intelligence officials warned about the severe and increasing threat of terrorist activity, including Al Qaeda and Ansar al-Sharia, which ultimately carried out the assault on the US consulate. The attack was foreshadowed by an emergency meeting where the CIA briefed on the significant threat to the mission's security. State Department leadership was accused of stripping references to prior attacks from talking points, arguing that it would provide ammunition for Congressional criticism.

The incident exposed a lack of proactive leadership and management ability in responding to security concerns. The State Department acknowledged this failure but found no specific evidence of misconduct. Former diplomatic security agents have spoken out about the multiple warnings and requests for help being unanswered, leaving the consulate vulnerable to attack. The controversy underscores the need for effective leadership and management to protect US personnel and interests abroad.

END OF SUMMARY.
